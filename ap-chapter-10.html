Module 10.1 Overview of muscle tissue

Function of Muscle Tissue

What is the basic function of all muscle tissue and what are its other functions?

Generating force (muscle tension) is the basic function of all muscle tissue. Other functions include:

* Creating movement

* Maintaining posture

* Stabilizing joints

* Generating heat

* Regulating the flow of materials through hollow organs

Types of Muscle tissues

Skeletal Tissue - What is the structure, location, voluntary/involuntary/function

Long, cylindrical striated muscle fibers; cells are multinucleated.

Mostly attached to skeleton

Voluntary

Produces movement

Cardiac Tissue - What is the structure, location, voluntary/involuntary/function

Short, wide, branching striated cardiac muscle cells with intercalated discs; cells have a single nucleus or two nuclei

Heart

Involuntary

Produces beating of heart

Smooth Tissue - What is the structure, location, voluntary/involuntary/function

Thin, smooth muscle cells, generally joined by gap junctions, cells have a single nucleus

Walls of hollow organs, as well as in the skin, and the eyes

Involuntary

Changes diameter of hollow organs

Properties of Muscle Cells

What is contractility in muscle cells?

Contractility is the ability of muscle cells to contract; proteins in the cell draw closer together.

What is excitability in muscle cells?

Excitability is the ability of muscle cells to respond to a stimulus, such as chemical signals, mechanical stretch, or local electrical signals.

What is conductivity in muscle cells?

Conductivity is the ability of muscle cells to conduct electrical changes across the entire plasma membrane.

What is extensibility in muscle cells?

Extensibility is the ability of muscle cells to be stretched (up to 3 times resting length) without rupturing.

What is elasticity in muscle cells?

Elasticity is the ability of muscle cells to return to their original length after stretching.

Structure of Muscle Cells

What does the prefix “myo” mean?

“Myo” is a prefix meaning muscle.

What is a myocyte?

A myocyte is a muscle cell.

What is a muscle fiber?

A muscle fiber is a skeletal muscle cell.

What is sarcoplasm?

the cytoplasm of a muscle cell.

What is the sarcolemma and how is it different from a general cell membrane?

The sarcolemma is the plasma membrane of a muscle cell.

It is different from a general cell membrane because it forms inward extensions called transverse tubules (T-tubules) that surround myofibrils.

What is the sarcoplasmic reticulum and its functions?

Modified smooth endoplasmic reticulum

Stores and releases calcium ions.

It forms a web-like network surrounding myofibrils.

Myofibrils

What are myofibrils and where are they found?

Myofibrils are unique cylindrical organelles found in all muscle cells, making up 50–80% of the cell volume, with hundreds to thousands present in each myocyte.

What is the diameter of a myofibril?

The diameter of a myofibril is about one micrometer, roughly 1/100 the thickness of a human hair.

What are myofibrils made of and what is their function?

Myofibrils are made up of bundles of specialized proteins that allow for muscle contraction.

Where are other organelles located in relation to myofibrils?

Other organelles, such as mitochondria, are packed between myofibrils.

Module 10.2 Structure and Function of the Skeletal Muscle Fibers

Levels of Organization within a Skeletal Muscle

What surrounds a single muscle fiber?

A single muscle fiber is surrounded by the endomysium.

What do multiple muscle fibers form, and what surrounds them?

Multiple muscle fibers form a fascicle, which is surrounded by the perimysium.

What makes up a skeletal muscle, and what surrounds it?

Bundles of fascicles make up a skeletal muscle, which is surrounded by the epimysium.

How are tendons formed and what is their function?

The perimysium and epimysium come together at the end of a muscle to form a tendon, which binds the muscle to its attaching structure, usually a bone.

What is the fascia and what are its functions?

Fascia is a thick connective tissue layer that encloses skeletal muscles. It anchors muscles to surrounding tissues and holds groups of muscles together.

Structure of the Skeletal Muscle Fiber

What does skeletal muscle tissue consist of?

Skeletal muscle tissue consists of many muscle fibers (cells) surrounded by endomysium, which is made of connective tissue and extracellular matrix (ECM).

What is the shape and size of skeletal muscle fibers?

Skeletal muscle fibers are thin cylinders that can be up to 30 centimeters long and up to 100 micrometers thick.

Structure of the Skeletal Muscle Fiber

What are transverse tubules (T-tubules) and their functions?

Transverse tubules (T-tubules) are extensions of the sarcolemma that surround each myofibril and form a tunnel-like network within muscle fibers.

They are filled with extracellular fluid and carry electrical signals throughout the muscle cell.

What are terminal cisternae and their relationship to T-tubules?

Terminal cisternae are enlarged sections of the sarcoplasmic reticulum that flank each T-tubule.

Two terminal cisternae and one T-tubule together form a structure called a triad.

What is the sarcolemma?

The sarcolemma is the cell membrane of the muscle fiber.

Structure of the Myofibril

What are myofibrils made of?

hundreds to thousands of myofilaments.

What are myofilaments composed of?

consist of several types of proteins: contractile, regulatory, and structural proteins.

What is the function of contractile proteins in myofilaments?

generate tension during muscle contraction.

What is the function of regulatory proteins in myofilaments?

Regulatory proteins control when a muscle fiber may contract.

What is the function of structural proteins in myofilaments?

Structural proteins maintain proper myofilament alignment and fiber stability.

Three types of myofilaments

What are thick filaments made of?

bundles of the contractile protein myosin.

What are thin filaments made of?

proteins actin, tropomyosin, and troponin.

What are elastic filaments made of and what is their function?

a single massive, spring-like structural protein called titin; stabilizes myofibril structure and resists excessive stretching.

Structure of the Myofibril

What is the structure of thick filaments?

contractile protein myosin with globular heads at each end linked by intertwining tails.

What connects the myosin heads to the tails?

a hinge-like neck.

What is the function of the active site on each myosin head?

binds with actin.

What proteins make up thin filaments?

actin, tropomyosin, and troponin.

How are actin subunits arranged in thin filaments?

multiple actin subunits string together to form two intertwining strands in a functional thin filament.

What is found on each actin subunit?

an active site that binds with myosin heads.

What is tropomyosin and what does it do?

a long, rope-like regulatory protein that twists around actin and covers the active sites.

What is troponin and what does it do?

a small globular regulatory protein that holds tropomyosin in place and assists with turning contractions on and off.

Myofilament Arrangement and the Sarcomere

What are Sarcomeres?

The functional unit of contraction

What creates striations in muscle tissue?

alternating light and dark bands with specific regions.

What is the I band and what does it contain?

the light band that contains only thin filaments.

What is the Z disc and where is it located?

a structure in the middle of the I band composed of structural proteins.

What are the functions of the Z disc?

anchor thin filaments in place and to one another, serve as attachment points for elastic filaments, and attach myofibrils to one another across the entire diameter of the muscle fiber.

Myofilament Arrangement and the Sarcomere

What is the A band and what does it contain?

the dark band that contains the zone of overlap with both thick and thin filaments, generating tension during contraction.

What is the H zone and where is it located?

the middle of the A band where only thick filaments exist.

What is the M line and what is its function?

a dark line in the middle of the A band where structural proteins hold thick filaments in place and serve as an anchoring point for elastic filaments.

Sliding-Filament Mechanism of Contraction

What does the sliding-filament mechanism of contraction describe?

the process where thick and thin filaments slide past each other, shortening the sarcomere without changing the length of the filaments.

In the sliding-filament analogy, what do the hands and fingers represent?

the hands represent a single large sarcomere, and the fingers represent thick and thin filaments in the zone of overlap.

In the sliding-filament analogy, what do the inner edges of the palms represent?

the Z-discs.

What happens to the sarcomere during contraction?

it grows progressively shorter as the filaments slide, though the filaments themselves do not change in length.

Preview of Muscle Contraction

What is a sarcomere and what does it represent?

the functional unit of contraction, extending from one Z-disc to the next.

What changes occur in the I band, H zone, and A band during contraction?

the I band and H zone narrow, while the A band remains unchanged.

How do myosin heads contribute to sarcomere shortening?

they attach to actin and pull thin filaments toward the M line, bringing Z-discs closer together.

How do sarcomeres shorten a whole muscle fiber?

sarcomeres are arranged end to end within each myofibril; when they simultaneously contract, the whole muscle fiber shortens.

Module 10.4 The Process of Skeletal Muscle Contraction and Relaxation

The Neuromuscular Junction

What does it mean that all skeletal muscles are innervated

Connected to neuron

What is the neuromuscular junction (NMJ)?

a synapse where a single motor neuron communicates with many muscle fibers and transmits a nerve impulse from the neuron to the sarcolemma.

What is found in the axon terminal and its function?

synaptic vesicles filled with acetylcholine (ACh), a neurotransmitter that triggers changes in the target tissue and allows cell-to-cell communication.

What is the synaptic cleft and its role?

the space between the axon terminal and muscle fiber, filled with collagen fibers and gel that anchors the neuron in place.

What is the motor end plate and what special features does it have?

a specialized region of the muscle fiber plasma membrane with a folded surface containing many ligand-gated Na+ channels.

How does acetylcholine affect the motor end plate?

binds to ligand-gated Na+ channels, opening them and allowing Na+ to diffuse into the muscle cell.

Skeletal Muscle Contraction

What are the phases of skeletal muscle contraction?

excitation phase

excitation-contraction coupling

contraction phase

1. Excitation of Skeletal Muscle

What are the steps in order

1.An action potential arrives at the axon terminal and triggers Ca2+ channels in the axon terminal to open.

2.Ca2+ entry triggers exocytosis of synaptic vesicles.

3.Synaptic vesicles release ACh into the synaptic cleft.

4.ACh binds to ligand-gated ion channels in the motor end plate.

5.Ion channels open and Na+ enter the muscle fiber.

6.Entry of Na+ depolarizes the sarcolemma locally, producing an end-plate potential.

2. Excitation-Contraction Coupling

What happens in the second step of muscle contraction: excitation-contraction coupling?

Excitation of the sarcolemma and contraction of myofilaments

Muscle fiber will NOT contract without being excited

Cross Bridge Cycle: Key Players

Who are the 4 key players in the cross-bridge cycle?

contractile proteins, regulatory proteins, calcium, and ATP.

What is the role of contractile proteins in the cross-bridge cycle?

generate force during muscle contractions.

What is the role of regulatory proteins in the cross-bridge cycle?

help switch the contraction process on and off.

Contractile Proteins uses what molecules?

actin, myosin

Regulatory proteins uses what molecules?

troponin, and tropomyosin.

What roles do calcium and ATP play in the cross-bridge cycle?

calcium triggers contraction by interacting with regulatory proteins, and ATP provides the energy for muscle contraction and cross-bridge detachment.

3. Skeletal Muscle Contraction

What happens in the third step?

Calcium ions released from terminal cisternae bind to troponin

Skeletal Muscle Relaxation

What are the two components of skeletal muscle relaxation?

motor neuron action potentials stop signaling for release of acetylcholine (ACh) from axon terminals

calcium ions are actively pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum terminal cisternae using ATP.

Rigor Mortis

What is rigor mortis and when does it begin?

progressive stiffening of skeletal muscles that begins 3–4 hours after death.

Why does rigor mortis occur?

pumps that drive Ca²⁺ back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum no longer have ATP, so Ca²⁺ remains in the cytosol and binds to troponin, initiating contraction.

Why are muscles unable to relax during rigor mortis?

myosin heads cannot detach from actin without ATP.

How long do muscles remain contracted during rigor mortis?

until myofilament proteins begin to degenerate, about 48–72 hours after death.

Module 10.5 Energy Sources for Skeletal Muscle

Sources of Energy for Muscle Contraction

Why is ATP required in skeletal muscle?

to power Na⁺/K⁺ pumps that maintain ion gradients

release myosin heads from actin active sites and recock them

pump calcium back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum for relaxation.

How is ATP generated in muscle fibers?

through immediate cytosolic reactions

glycolytic catabolism in the cytosol (producing 2 ATP)

oxidative catabolism in mitochondria (producing many ATP).

Do the three ATP-generating processes occur separately or together during muscle contraction?

all three may occur simultaneously, used in different proportions depending on the resources and needs of the muscle cells.

Ion Channels and Gradients: Na+/K+ pump

How does the Na⁺/K⁺ pump maintain concentration gradients?

moves three Na⁺ ions out of the cell and two K⁺ ions into the cell.

Why is ATP hydrolysis required for the Na⁺/K⁺ pump?

because the pump moves ions against their concentration gradients.

Immediate Sources of Energy for Muscle Contraction

What is the main immediate source of energy for muscle contraction?

stored ATP in the muscle fiber, which is rapidly consumed during contraction.

How does creatine phosphate contribute to immediate energy?

its concentration is 5–6 times higher than ATP in the cytosol and can regenerate enough ATP for about 10 seconds of maximum muscle activity.

Energy Sources for skeletal muscle: Glycolysis

When does glycolysis provide energy for muscle contraction?

once immediate sources of energy are depleted.

What fuels glycolysis in skeletal muscle?

glucose from blood and glycogen stored in muscle or liver cells.

How long can glycolysis supply ATP for sustained contraction?

about 30–40 seconds.

Does glycolysis require oxygen?

no, it is anaerobic, but the fate of pyruvate depends on oxygen availability.

What happens to pyruvate when oxygen is abundant?

it enters mitochondria for oxidative catabolism.

What happens to pyruvate when oxygen is not abundant?

it is converted into two molecules of lactic acid

which can later be converted back to glucose by the liver or taken up in mitochondria for oxidative catabolism.

Energy Sources for skeletal muscle: Oxidative catabolism

What is oxidative catabolism and what does it require?

aerobic catabolism that requires oxygen directly.

Why is oxidative catabolism important for muscle contraction?

it allows longer-lasting contractions by producing many more ATP than glycolysis.

What fuels can muscle fibers use for oxidative catabolism?

preferably glucose, but if unavailable, fatty acids and amino acids.

When does oxidative catabolism become the predominant energy source?

after about 1 minute of contraction, providing nearly 100% of necessary ATP after several minutes.

How long can oxidative catabolism supply ATP?

for hours, as long as oxygen and fuels are available.

How is oxygen supplied to muscle fibers for oxidative catabolism?

oxygen diffuses from blood into the fiber and is bound to myoglobin within the fiber.

Module 10.6 Muscle Tension at the Fiber Level

Muscle Tension at the Fiber Level

What is a muscle twitch?

the smallest muscle contraction, consisting of a single muscle fiber contracting and relaxing.

What are the three phases of a muscle twitch in order?

latent period

contraction period

relaxation period.

What happens during the latent period?

time for the action potential to propagate across the sarcolemma.

What happens during the contraction period?

repeated cross-bridge cycles generate tension.

What happens during the relaxation period?

calcium ion levels are reduced in the cytosol by SR pumps, and tension diminishes.

Factors that determine the amount of tension produced by a twitch contraction

What is wave summation in muscle fiber twitches?

repeated stimulation of a muscle fiber by a motor neuron results in twitches with progressively greater tension.

What determines the number of cross-bridges that can form in a sarcomere?

the length of the sarcomere prior to contraction, known as the length-tension relationship.

How does muscle fiber type affect tension production?

different fiber types (Type I/slow and Type II/fast) produce different amounts of tension.

Tension Production and the Timing and Frequency of Stimulation

What is wave summation?

an increase in tension caused by repeated stimulation of a muscle fiber, where calcium ions accumulate in the cytosol and the muscle has little time to relax.

Why does wave summation occur?

because the SR does not have to pump all calcium ions back into the SR, allowing cytosolic calcium to increase with each stimulation.

What is fused (complete) tetanus?

a condition where the muscle fiber does not relax between stimuli and tension stays constant at nearly 100% of maximum.

How does increased calcium availability affect tension during fused tetanus?

it allows more cross-bridges to form, contributing to increased tension.

Length-Tension Relationship

What is the length-tension relationship?

the concept that a sarcomere at about 100–120% of its natural length can generate the greatest tension.

Why does optimal sarcomere length produce maximum tension?

because it allows enough overlap between thin and thick filaments for many cross-bridges to form while leaving space for thick filaments to pull thin filaments toward the M line without hitting Z-discs.

Two Classes of Skeletal Muscle Fibers

What are the two main classes of skeletal muscle fibers?

Type I/slow and Type II/fast.

How are Type I/slow and Type II/fast skeletal muscle fibers classified?

based on myosin ATPase activity (speed of power stroke) and predominant energy source (oxidative versus glycolytic catabolism).

Do muscles contain only one fiber type?

most muscles contain combinations of both fiber types.

What determines the proportion of fiber types in a muscle?

the function of the muscle itself.

Classes of Skeletal Muscle Fibers

What are Type I skeletal muscle fibers like?

slow-twitch fibers that contract slowly, produce less force, rely on oxidative catabolism, have many mitochondria and myoglobin, a well-developed blood supply, predominate in postural muscles, and can sustain contractions for long durations.

What are Type II skeletal muscle fibers like?

large-diameter, fast-twitch fibers that fatigue quickly, have high myosin ATPase activity, rely mainly on glycolytic catabolism, have fewer mitochondria, lower myoglobin, less extensive blood supply, and are classified as “white muscle.”

What are the subtypes of Type II fibers and their characteristics?

IIa (fast oxidative-glycolytic) – intermediate strength and fatigability; use both oxidative and glycolytic pathways.

IIx (fast glycolytic) – produce extremely fast, powerful twitches, fatigue quickly, and rely almost exclusively on glycolytic catabolism.

Classes of Skeletal Muscle Fibers

Do muscles contain only one fiber class?

most muscles contain all fiber classes, each stimulated under different conditions.

Which fibers are primarily used when a baseball player is sitting in the dugout?

Type I fibers in the back and abdomen to maintain posture.

Which fibers are primarily used when the player jogs to the plate?

Type IIa fibers in the legs.

Which fibers are primarily used when the player swings the bat?

Type IIx fibers in the arms.

Classes of Skeletal Muscle Fibers

Class I (Slow Oxidative)

Slow Oxidative fibers (Type I) primarily use oxidative catabolism.

They have an extensive blood supply, many mitochondria, and a large amount of myoglobin, giving them a red color. They contain little glycogen and have low myosin ATPase activity, making them highly fatigue-resistant (low fatigability). These are small to intermediate in diameter and are used for activities requiring sustained effort, such as standing and sitting.

Class IIa (Fast Oxidative Glycolytic)

Fast Oxidative Glycolytic fibers (Type IIa) utilize oxidative and glycolytic catabolism.

They have a less extensive blood supply than Type I, an intermediate number of mitochondria, and an intermediate amount of myoglobin, resulting in a light red color. They also have intermediate amounts of glycogen and exhibit high myosin ATPase activity. Their fatigability is intermediate. These are large in diameter and are used in activities like walking and writing.

Class IIx (Fast Glycolytic)

Fast Glycolytic fibers (Type IIx) rely almost entirely on glycolytic catabolism.

They have a limited blood supply, few mitochondria, and little myoglobin, giving them a color of light pink to white. These fibers store a large amount of glycogen and possess the highest myosin ATPase activity. They are easily fatigued (high fatigability) and are intermediate in diameter. They are recruited for powerful, short-duration activities such as heavy lifting and sprinting.

Botulism and Botox

What produces botulinum toxin and how potent is it?

Clostridium botulinum produces the toxin, which is one of the most lethal biological poisons; as little as one gram can kill about one million adults.

What happens when someone is exposed to botulinum toxin?

the toxin binds to motor neurons at the NMJ, blocks acetylcholine release from synaptic vesicles, and paralyzes affected muscles, potentially causing death from respiratory failure without treatment.

How is botulinum toxin used medically and cosmetically?

in minute quantities, it can treat painful muscle spasms, migraine headaches, and relax facial muscles for cosmetic purposes (Botox).

Module 10.7 Muscle Tension at the Organ Level

Twitch of a single muscle fiber vs. Twitch at multiple muscle fibers

Why don’t we usually observe a twitch in a single muscle fiber?

because muscle fibers are usually activated in groups called motor units, not individually.

What topics are important when studying twitches at multiple muscle fibers?

structure of the motor unit, how motor unit activation controls overall muscle tension, and the three types of skeletal muscle contractions with their tension levels.

Muscle Tension at the Organ Level

What is a motor unit?

a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates.

How do muscle fibers within a motor unit respond?

all fibers respond with the same amount of tension.

How does the number of fibers in a motor unit relate to movement?

smaller motor units allow fine movement, while larger motor units generate greater force.

What is recruitment in muscle contraction?

the process of stimulating more motor units as greater force is required.

What produces muscle tone and why is it important?

a small amount of tension produces muscle tone, which helps maintain posture, stabilize joints, generate heat, and keep muscles prepared for movement.

How does the nervous system manage motor units during sustained activity?

it alternates activation so some motor units can rest while others contract.

Types of Muscle Contractions

What factors influence the amount of tension a muscle generates?

the number of motor units activated and how the length of the muscle changes during contraction.

Is muscle contraction only about shortening the muscle?

no, contractions also occur during muscle fiber lengthening.

What are the three types of muscle contractions based on muscle length?

isotonic concentric, isotonic eccentric, and isometric.

Types of Muscle Contractions

What are isotonic contractions?

contractions where tension is constant but muscle length changes.

What happens during isotonic concentric contractions?

muscle shortens while generating constant tension; force generated is greater than the external force.

What happens during isotonic eccentric contractions?

muscle lengthens while generating constant tension; external force is greater than the force generated by the muscle, and elastic filaments allow stretching.

What are isometric contractions?

contractions where muscle length remains unchanged because the external force equals the force generated by the muscle.

Delayed-Onset Muscle Soreness (DOMS)

What is delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS)?

muscle soreness following exercise, traditionally thought to result from lactic acid accumulation during glycolysis.

What is the current understanding of the cause of DOMS?

minor structural damage to muscles, especially from isotonic eccentric contractions, and myofibril remodeling as an adaptive response to stress.

What is the most effective treatment for DOMS?

continued exercise to condition the muscle; stopping exercise can cause pain to return until muscles adapt.

How effective are other treatments like massage, topical therapies, acupuncture, or medications for DOMS?

they have shown little benefit.

Module 10.8 Skeletal Muscle Performance

Changes Caused By Physical Training

What is myoplasticity?

changes in muscle structure resulting from changes in function due to physical training.

Do mature skeletal muscle fiber nuclei undergo mitosis?

no, the majority are amitotic and do not undergo mitosis.

What role do satellite cells play in muscle adaptation?

they retain mitotic ability and help repair injured skeletal muscle.

Do changes from physical training involve changes in the number of muscle fibers?

no, changes occur within existing muscle fibers.

What determines the specific changes in muscle fibers from training?

the type of training, such as endurance versus resistance training.

Changes Caused By Physical Training

What are the effects of endurance training on muscles?

large increase in frequency of motor unit activation and moderate increase in force production, mostly causing biochemical changes.

What are the effects of resistance or strength training on muscles?

moderate increase in frequency of motor unit activation and large increase in force production, primarily causing anatomical changes such as hypertrophy.

What happens to muscles during disuse?

anatomical and biochemical changes occur, including a decrease in the number of myofibrils, reduction in fiber size, and decreased oxidative enzymes, leading to atrophy.

Muscular Fatigue

What is muscular fatigue?

inability to maintain a given level of intensity during activity.

What are the main causes of muscular fatigue?

depletion of key metabolites (creatine phosphate, glycogen, glucose)

decreased oxygen availability to muscle fibers

accumulation of chemicals in the fiber (Ca²⁺, ADP, phosphate)

environmental conditions such as extreme heat and electrolyte disturbances from sweating

Excess Postexercise Oxygen Consumption and Recovery Period

What is the recovery period after exercise?

the time it takes for the body to return to its pre-exercise state.

What is excess postexercise oxygen consumption (EPOC)?

an increased rate of breathing after exercise that supplies necessary oxygen.

What does EPOC allow the body to do?

recover from exercise-induced homeostatic imbalances, including heat dissipation, restoration of ion concentrations, and correction of blood pH imbalances.

Module 10.9 Smooth and Cardiac Muscle

Smooth muscle functions

What are the 3 main functions of smooth muscle?

propel materials through hollow organs (peristalsis)

form sphincters to control passage of materials

regulate flow rates through hollow organs by changing the diameter of the tubing.

Smooth Muscle Myofilaments

How are myofilaments arranged in smooth muscle cells compared to skeletal muscle?

they have no sarcomeres, no striations, no T-tubules, and no troponin.

How are actin filaments arranged in smooth muscle?

obliquely in the sarcoplasm and anchored to dense bodies.

What are dense bodies and what is their role?

proteins bound to scaffold-like intermediate filaments that connect dense bodies to each other.

How are thin and thick filaments organized around dense bodies?

several thin filaments radiate from each dense body to surround a single thick filament, resulting in a higher thin-to-thick filament ratio than in skeletal muscle.

What is unique about myosin filaments in smooth muscle?

both thick and thin filaments are longer, and myosin heads are along the entire length of the thick filament with opposite-facing heads.

Smooth Muscle Contraction and Relaxation

What are the steps in order:

1.Influx of extracellular calcium ions that bind to protein in sarcoplasm called calmodulin

2.This activates myosin light chain kinase (MLCK)

3.MLCK in turn activates myosin ATPase

4.Crossbridge cycle begins
