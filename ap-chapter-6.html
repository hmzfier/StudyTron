Bone Marrow Transplantation

Why do patients with blood diseases like leukemia, sickle-cell anemia, or aplastic anemia benefit from bone marrow transplantation?

Their hematopoietic cells are not functioning properly, so donor marrow provides healthy blood-forming cells.

How is bone marrow collected from a donor?

A needle is inserted into the donor’s pelvic bone, and red marrow is withdrawn, sometimes up to 2 quarts (about 2% of total).

Explain the bone marrow process (4)

The recipient’s existing marrow is destroyed to make room for the donor cells.

donor marrow given intravenously

donor cells travel to the recipient’s marrow cavities

begin producing new blood cells within 2–4 weeks if successful.

What are common complications after a bone marrow transplant?

Flu-like symptoms (first 2–4 weeks), infection, or transplant rejection.

Can recipients return to a normal life after a successful transplant?

Yes, many recipients can return to a healthy life if the transplant “takes.”

In this context, “takes” means that the donor bone marrow successfully starts producing new, healthy blood cells in the recipient.

If the transplant “takes,” the donor stem cells engraft in the recipient’s marrow, multiply, and restore proper blood cell production.

If it doesn’t take, the donor cells fail to grow or are rejected, and the transplant is unsuccessful.

So basically, “takes” = the transplant works as intended.

What makes the extracellular matrix of bone unique?

It contains both an inorganic mineral matrix and an organic matrix

What is the inorganic matrix of bone predominantly made of?

Calcium salts; bone stores about 85% of the body’s calcium ions and large amounts of phosphorus

What form do calcium and phosphorus salts take in bone?

Large molecules of hydroxyapatite crystals

What property does the crystalline structure of hydroxyapatite give bone?

It makes bone one of the hardest substances in the body, strong and resistant to compression

What functions does the inorganic matrix allow bone to perform?

Protective and supportive functions

What additional ions are found in the inorganic matrix besides calcium and phosphorus?

Bicarbonate, potassium, magnesium, and sodium

What is the organic matrix of bone also called?

Osteoid

What is the predominant protein fiber in the organic matrix?

Collagen

What is the primary function of collagen

forms cross-links to help resist torsion (twisting) and tensile (pulling/stretching) forces

How do collagen fibers enhance the hardness of bone?

They align with hydroxyapatite crystals

What role do glycosaminoglycans and proteoglycans play in the organic matrix?

They create an osmotic gradient that draws water into osteoid, helping tissue resist compression

What does it mean that bone is a dynamic tissue

Osteoblasts

Where are they metabolically active?

What is their function (2)

What is Bone deposition

The process where osteoblasts secrete organic matrix materials and assist in forming the inorganic matrix

Osteocytes

What happens to osteoblasts after they surround themselves with bone matrix in lacunae?

They become osteocytes that no longer actively synthesize bone matrix

What is Lacuna/Lacunae (plural)?

A small cavity or space within bone tissue that houses an osteocyte (a mature bone cell).

You can picture it like a little “apartment” inside the bone matrix where the osteocyte lives. The lacuna keeps the cell protected but also connects to other lacunae through tiny canals called canaliculi, which let nutrients and signals pass between cells.

What is the metabolic activity of osteocytes?

They are not metabolically active except for maintaining the bone extracellular matrix

What special ability do osteocytes appear to have?

They can recruit osteoblasts to build up or reinforce bone under tension

Osteclasts

What is the main function of osteoclasts?

Bone resorption; they secrete hydrogen ions and enzymes to break down bone matrix.

How do osteoclasts break down bone?

By secreting hydrogen ions and enzymes that dissolve the bone matrix.

Describe the structure of osteoclasts.

Large, multinucleated cells that resemble jellyfish.

Where do osteoclasts originate from?

They are derived from the fusion of cells from bone marrow.

Where are osteoclasts eventually located in bone?

In shallow depressions on the internal and external surfaces of bone.

Why are osteoclasts important in bone remodeling?

They remove old or damaged bone, allowing osteoblasts to build new bone, maintaining bone strength and health.

What do osteoclasts use hydrogen ions for?

Hydrogen ions dissolve components of the inorganic bone matrix.

What do osteoclasts use enzymes for?

Enzymes break down the organic matrix of bone.

What happens to substances liberated from bone breakdown?

They are absorbed into the osteoclast cytosol by various transport methods.

What can happen to these substances after absorption?

Structure of compact bone: Built to withstand lots of stress

What is Osteon (Haversian system)

The structural unit of compact bone

Osteon is the entire structural unit, which is made up of multiple lamellae surrounding a central canal.

What are lamellae and how many layers are typically in an osteon?

Rings of thin layers of bone

Typically 4 to 20 layers per osteon.

What is the function of lamellae in compact bone?

They greatly enhance the bone’s strength.

What is contained in the central canal of an osteon?

Blood vessels and nerves that supply the osteon.

What are lacunae and what do they contain?

Small spaces that contain osteocytes for bone maintenance.

What are canaliculi and what is their function?

Canals that connect lacunae, allowing osteocytes to share resources (oxygen, nutrients) and communicate.

How do osteocytes share resources (oxygen, nutrients) and communicate through the canaliculi?

Cytoplasmic extensions of osteocytes extend through the canaliculi networks.

Osteons are not permanent structures: regularly resorbed and rebuilt

Are osteons permanent structures?

No, they are regularly resorbed and rebuilt.

What are interstitial lamellae? (3)

Lamellae outside of the osteon

remnants of resorbed osteons

fill gaps between forming osteons.

What are circumferential lamellae and where are they located?

Lamellae that extend around the entire circumference of the diaphysis, just deep to the periosteum.

What is the function of circumferential lamellae?

They help resist twisting of the entire bone.

What are perforating (Volkmann’s) canals?

Canals that originate from blood vessels in the periosteum and travel perpendicular to the central canals of neighboring osteons.

What is the function of perforating canals?

They connect neighboring osteons to each other.

Is spongy bone usually weight-bearing like compact bone?

No, it is usually not weight-bearing.

How densely packed is spongy bone compared to compact bone?

It is much less densely packed.

What is the function of the network of struts in spongy bone? (2)

What additional role does spongy bone play?

It protects bone marrow tissue.

What is spongy bone composed of?

trabeculae

What are trabeculae?

Struts of bone in spongy bone, covered with endosteum.

Are trabeculae arranged into osteons?

No, they are usually not arranged into osteons.

What are trabeculae composed of?

Module 6.3 Bone Formation: Ossification

Ossification (or osteogenesis): Process of bone formation

When does bone formation begin and how long does it continue?

Bone formation begins in the embryonic period and continues through childhood, with most bones completing the process by age 7.

What is the first type of bone formed?

Immature primary (woven) bone.

What are the characteristics of immature primary (woven) bone?

Irregularly arranged collagen bundles, osteocytes, and sparse inorganic matrix.

What happens to immature primary bone over time?

It is eventually broken down by osteoclasts.

What is mature secondary bone?

Bone with more inorganic matrix and increased strength compared to primary bone.

Types of Ossification

Intramembranous ossification

Bone formation built on a model made of a membrane of embryonic connective tissue.

Which bones are formed by intramembranous ossification?

Many flat bones, including the skull and clavicles, during fetal development.

What type of bone does intramembranous ossification initially form?

Primary bone, which will later become secondary bone.

Endochondral ossification

Bone formation built on a model of hyaline cartilage.

Bones formed by endochondral ossification

Most bones of the body, except the skull and clavicles.

What is the structure of the hyaline cartilage model in endochondral ossification?

It contains chondrocytes, collagen, and extracellular matrix, all surrounded by a connective tissue membrane (perichondrium) and immature cartilage cells (chondroblasts).

Intremembranous Ossification Steps

What are the steps to Intremembranous Ossification

1.Osteoblasts develop in the primary ossification center from mesenchymal cells

2.Osteoblasts secrete organic matrix, which calcifies and trapped osteoblasts become osteocytes

3.Osteoblasts lay down trabechulae of early spongy bone, and some of the surrounding mesenchyme differentiates into periosteum

4.Osteblasts in the periosteum lay down early compact one

Endochondral Ossification Steps

Steps to Endochondral Ossification

1.The chondoblasts in the periochondrium differentiate into osteoblasts

2a. Osteoblasts build the bone collar on the bone’s external surface as the bone begins to ossify from the outside

2b. Simultaneously, the internal cartilage begins to calcify and the chondrocytes die

3.In the primary ossification center, osteoblasts replace the calcified cartilage with early spongy bone; the secondary ossification centers and the medullary cavity develop.

4.As the medullary cavity enlarges, the remaining cartilage is replaced by bone; the epiphyses finish ossifying.

Endochondral Ossification Continued

Where does cartilage remain after endochondral ossification?

In the epiphyseal plates and on articular surfaces where bones form joints (articular cartilage).

Does articular cartilage persist into adulthood?

Yes, articular cartilage persists into adulthood.

What happens to epiphyseal plates once bone growth in length ceases?

They are eventually replaced with bone.

Osteoporosis and Healthy Bones

What is the most common bone disease in the United States?

Osteoporosis.

What happens to bones in osteoporosis?

They become weak and brittle due to inadequate inorganic matrix.

How does osteoporosis affect fracture risk and healing?

It increases the risk of fractures and decreases the rate of healing.

What are the causes of osteoporosis? (7)

Dietary deficiencies (calcium and/or vitamin D)

female gender

advanced age

lack of exercise

hormonal factors (lack of estrogen in postmenopausal women)

genetic factors

other diseases

How can osteoporosis be prevented?

Balanced diet with supplementation as needed, weight-bearing exercise,

and estrogen replacement if appropriate.

What treatments are available for osteoporosis?

Drugs that inhibit osteoclasts or stimulate osteoblasts.

Module 6.4 Bone Growth in Length and Width

Long Bones length by longitudinal growth

Which cells are involved in bone growth at the epiphyseal plate?

Division of chondrocytes (not osteocytes or osteoblasts).

Where does bone growth take place in relation to the epiphysis?

At the epiphysis on the side closest to the diaphysis.

What is the epiphyseal plate composed of?

Hyaline cartilage that did not ossify.

How many zones of cells are in the epiphyseal plate?

Five different zones of cells.

Structure of the epiphyseal plate: 5 zones

Are these in a specific order

Yes, here’s the correct order from epiphysis (top) to diaphysis (bottom) of a growing long bone:

Zone of reserve cartilage → Zone of proliferation → Zone of hypertrophy and maturation → Zone of calcification → Zone of ossification

What is the zone of reserve cartilage?

Contains cells not directly involved in bone growth but can be recruited for cell division if needed.

What is the zone of proliferation?

Contains actively dividing chondrocytes in lacunae

What is the zone of hypertrophy and maturation?

They enlarge and mature

Contains: mature chondrocytes

What is the zone of calcification?

Chondrocytes die and their matrix calcifies

Contains: dead chondrocytes; some calcified

What is the zone of ossification in the epiphyseal plate?

Calcified cartilage is replaced with bone

Contains: calcified chondrocytes and osteoblasts

Appositional Growth-bone widening

What is appositional growth?

Bone widening through the addition of new bone on existing surfaces.

Which cells are responsible for appositional growth?

Osteoblasts between the periosteum and bone surface.

What is the first structure formed during appositional growth?

Circumferential lamellae.

What happens to deeper circumferential lamellae over time?

They are eventually removed or incorporated into osteons.

Role of Hormones in Bone Growth

What is the role of growth hormone in bone growth?

Secreted by the anterior pituitary gland

enhances protein synthesis and cell division in nearly all tissues, including bone.

How does testosterone affect bone growth?

Increases appositional growth (bones become thicker with more calcium deposition)

increases mitosis in the epiphyseal plate leading to growth spurts in teenage years

accelerates closure of the epiphyseal plate.

How does estrogen affect bone growth? (4)

Increases longitudinal bone growth

inhibits osteoclast activity

triggers growth spurts in teenage females

accelerates epiphyseal plate closure faster than testosterone.

How do testosterone and estrogen contribute to height differences between genders?

Estrogen accelerates epiphyseal plate closure faster than testosterone, resulting in shorter average height in females compared to males.

Module 6.5 Bone Remodeling and Repair

Bone Remodeling

What is bone remodeling?

A continuous process of bone formation and loss after growth in length is finished.

What are the purposes of bone remodeling? (5)

Maintenance of calcium ion homeostasis

Replacement of primary bone with secondary bone

Bone repair

Replacement of old brittle bone with newer bone

Adaptation to tension and stress

Bone Remodeling: Bone deposition by osteoblasts

Where are osteoblasts involved in bone deposition found?

In both the periosteum and endosteum.

What do osteoblasts produce during bone deposition?

They make organic matrix and facilitate formation of inorganic matrix.

What substances do osteoblasts secrete to bind calcium ions?

Proteoglycans and glycoproteins.

What is the role of vesicles secreted by osteoblasts?

They contain calcium ions, ATP, and enzymes that bind to collagen fibers

Calcium ions eventually crystallize, rupturing the vesicle and starting the calcification process.

Bone Remodeling: Resorption by Osteoclasts

How do osteoclasts resorb bone?

They secrete hydrogen ions onto the bone extracellular matrix (ECM), breaking down hydroxyapatite crystals in the inorganic matrix.

What happens to the calcium ions and other minerals released by osteoclasts?

They can be reused elsewhere in the body.

What enzymes do osteoclasts secrete and what is their function?

Enzymes degrade the organic matrix, including proteoglycans, glycosaminoglycans, and glycoproteins.

What happens to the components of the organic matrix after degradation by osteoclasts?

They can be reused by osteoclasts.

Calcium Ion Homeostasis - response to low blood calcium by a negative feedback loop (5)

Stimulus: Blood calcium decreases below normal range

Receptor: Parathyroid gland cells detect a low blood calcium level

Control Center: Parathyroid gland cells release PTH into the blood

Effectors: Osteoclasts resorb bone, kidneys retain calcium, intestines absorb calcium

Response: Blood calcium levels rise back to normal

Summary: Low calcium → PTH → raises calcium → stops PTH when normal

Calcium Ion Homeostasis - response to high blood calcium by a negative feedback loop (5)

Stimulus: Increased blood calcium levels

Receptor Thyroid gland detects the high calcium levels in blood

Control Center: Decrease in PTH secretion, Increase in Calcitonin by thyroid gland

Effectors: Decrease in Osteoclast activity = Increase in Osteoblast activity = more deposition

Response: Decrease in Blood calcium levels

Bone Repair: Fractures

Simple fracture

A fracture in which the skin and tissue around the fracture remain intact.

Compound fracture

A fracture in which the skin and tissues around the fracture are damaged.

Spiral Fracture

Fracture resulting from twisting forces applied to the bone

Comminuted Fracture

bone is shattered into multiple fragments

Greenstick

Bone breaks on one side but only bends on the other side

Common in children

Compression

Fracture in which the bone is crushed under the weight it is meant to support

Common in elderly and those with reduced bone mass

Avulsion

A tendon or ligament pulls off a fragment of bone

Often seen in ankle fractures

Epiphyseal Plate

Fracture that involves at least part of the epiphyseal plate

Occurs only in children and young adults, may interfere with growth

Process of Fracture Repair

What is the process of fracture repair (4)

1.A hematoma fills the gap between the bone fragments

2.Fibroblasts and chondroblasts infiltrate the hematoma, and a soft callus forms.

3. Osteoblasts build a bone callus

4. The bone callus is remodeled and primary bone is replaced with secondary bone

What do fibroblasts secrete during bone repair?

Collagen fibers that form dense irregular collagenous connective tissue.

What do osteogenic cells from the endosteum become during bone repair?

Chondroblasts that secrete hyaline cartilage.
