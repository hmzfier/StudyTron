Bone Markings

What are bone markings?

Surface features on bones.

What are depressions on bones and their function?

Indentation

Depressions are indentations that serve as pathways for blood vessels and nerves, or sites for articulations/joints between bones.

Simple explanation: These are dips or holes in a bone. They let other things fit in, like where bones meet (joints) or where blood vessels and nerves run.

What are openings on bones and their function?

Hole

Openings enclose delicate structures and allow them to pass through bones.

Simple explanation: These are holes or tunnels in a bone that let important things pass through, like nerves and blood vessels.

What are projections on bones and their function?

Bump - like a pimple

Projections are sites where bones articulate or where ligaments and tendons attach.

Simple explanations: These are bumps or sticks out from a bone. They are places where muscles, tendons, or other bones attach.

What are Sutures

Sutures are immovable joints found only between bones of the skull.

They are the lines you see in the skull

They are made of dense fibrous connective tissue that tightly binds the bones together and allow for slight movement in infants (to aid birth and brain growth), but they fuse and become immovable in adults.

The Fetal Skull

Are fetal skull bones fused at birth?

No, fetal skull bones are not fused to one another.

What are fontanels?

Membranous soft spots between fetal cranial bones.

When do a child’s cranial bones complete development?

At 18–24 months.

What are the functions of fontanels and unfused sutures?

They allow flexibility during birth and enable the skull to enlarge with normal brain growth.

Why is the hyoid bone grouped with skull bones?

No, it does not articulate with any skull bones or other bones.

Where is the hyoid bone located and what is its shape?

It is a C-shaped bone in the superior neck.

How is the hyoid bone suspended?

By ligaments that attach it to the styloid processes of the temporal bone and the larynx (voice box).

What is the function of the hyoid bone?

It provides attachment points for muscles involved in swallowing and speech.

Forensic Skull Anatomy

What is one obvious difference in the forehead between male and female skulls?

The male forehead is sloped, while the female forehead is straight.

How does the supraorbital ridge differ between male and female skulls?

The male’s supraorbital ridge is more prominent.

How does the mandibular angle differ between male and female skulls?

The male mandibular angle is closer to 90 degrees; the female’s is generally greater than 90 degrees.

What is notable about the mastoid process in male and female skulls?

The male mastoid process is larger and more prominent.

Overview of the vertebral column

How many bones are in the vertebral column on average?

Approximately 33 bones (vertebrae).

How are vertebrae classified?

By structure and location.

How is each vertebra labeled?

With a letter indicating the region and a number indicating its position.

What are the spaces between moveable vertebrae called?

Intervertebral foramina

They are visible from a lateral view.

What passes through the intervertebral foramina?

Spinal nerve roots.

Vertebral classification

How many cervical vertebrae are there and where are they located?

Seven cervical vertebrae (C1–C7) located in the neck.

How many thoracic vertebrae are there and what do they articulate with?

Twelve thoracic vertebrae (T1–T12) that articulate with the ribs.

How many lumbar vertebrae are there and where are they located?

Five lumbar vertebrae (L1–L5) located in the lower back.

How much vertebrae does the sacrum have and what does it articulate with?

Five fused sacral vertebrae

articulate with the pelvic bone

What shape is the vertebral column of a newborn?

C-shaped.

Into what shape does the vertebral column develop as the infant grows?

Which curvatures are primary and when are they present?

Thoracic and sacral curvatures

present during the fetal period

Which curvatures are secondary and when do they develop?

Cervical and lumbar curvatures

develop after the fetal period

Why is the development of secondary curvatures important?

They are critical for humans walking upright.

What is the function of the cervical curvature?

Allows us to hold our heads up.

What is the function of the lumbar curvature?

Shifts the weight of the body onto the sacrum, providing balance and support for walking on two legs.

What is scoliosis?

Abnormal lateral curvatures in the vertebral column.

What is lordosis (swayback)?

Exaggerated cervical and lumbar curvatures.

What is kyphosis?

Exaggeration of the thoracic curvature, resulting in a hunchback appearance.

What are intercostal spaces and their function?

Vertical Compression Fractures

What is a vertebral compression fracture?

A common fracture of the vertebral body that reduces its height by more than 20%, often caused by trauma or diseases that destroy bone such as osteoporosis or tumors.

How is treatment for vertebral compression fractures determined?

It depends on the type and severity of the fracture and the overall health of the patient.

What are conservative treatments for vertebral compression fractures?

Bed rest, bracing, and physical therapy.

When might surgery be required for vertebral compression fractures?

If conservative treatments are insufficient or the fracture is severe.

What is a wedge fracture?

A fracture involving only the anterior portion of the vertebra

Most common in elderly women with kyphosis or osteoporosis, often caused by minor trauma; symptoms usually include pain that may radiate.

What is a burst fracture?

A fracture involving the entire vertebral body

usually due to severe trauma; symptoms are more severe, and bone fragments can damage the spinal cord and nerves.

Herniated Disc

What is a herniated disc (slipped disc) and what causes it?

A tear in the anulus fibrosus that allows the nucleus pulposus to protrude, generally caused by trauma, improper lifting, or repeated motions like lifting and squatting.

Why does the anulus fibrosus usually tear on its posterolateral side?

Because of the arrangement of ligaments around the vertebral column.

What happens when the nucleus pulposus bulges?

It compresses nerve roots as they emerge from the spinal cord, causing pain, numbness, tingling, and muscle weakness, which may radiate to other parts of the body.

What treatments are used for a herniated disc?

Injection of anti-inflammatory steroid medications, physical therapy, and surgery to repair the tear and relieve nerve compression.

The Sternum and CPR

What is cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR)?

A lifesaving technique used when an individual’s heart and/or breathing has stopped, involving repeated compressions over the sternum to restore circulation.

Where should hands be placed during CPR?

In the center of the sternum, between the nipples, to deliver compressions over the sternal body.

Why should compressions not be applied directly to the xiphoid process?

Because it can break off and damage underlying organs, such as the liver.

Can CPR cause injuries even when performed correctly?

Yes, it can break the sternal body and ribs, especially in elderly individuals with low bone mass.

Which bone of the pectoral girdle directly articulates with the axial skeleton?

Where is the clavicle located?

The Pectoral Girdle: Scapula

Where is the scapula located and what is its general shape?

The scapula is a triangular bone on the posterosuperior rib cage, between the second and seventh ribs.

What are the three borders of the scapula?

Medial, lateral, and superior borders.

What is the coracoid process?

A hook-shaped projection on the anterior surface of the scapula.

A depression inferior to the coracoid process that provides an attachment site for the subscapularis muscle.

What is the glenoid cavity and its function?

A posterior ridge of bone that runs from the medial to lateral side along the superior scapula.

A depression on the scapula superior to the spine.

The Arm: The Humerus

What is the largest and strongest bone of the upper limb?

The humerus.

What are the main structural parts of the humerus?

It has two epiphyses (proximal and distal) and a long diaphysis.

What is the humeral head and its function?

A medially oriented, ball-shaped proximal epiphysis that articulates with the glenoid cavity at the shoulder joint.

What is the anatomical neck of the humerus?

A groove surrounding the humeral head.

What is the greater tubercle of the humerus?

A projection of bone lateral to the anatomical neck.

What is the deltoid tubercle and its function?

A feature on the diaphysis that serves as the attachment site for the deltoid muscle.

What are the medial and lateral epicondyles of the humerus?

Projections on the distal epiphysis that serve as attachment sites for muscles.

The Humerus

What structure on the humerus articulates with the radius at the elbow joint?

The capitulum, a spherical knob on the anterior and lateral aspect of the distal epiphysis.

What structure on the humerus articulates with the ulna at the elbow joint?

The trochlea, a spool-shaped knob on the anterior and medial aspect of the distal epiphysis.

What are the lateral radial fossa and medial coronoid fossa?

Small indentations just proximal to the capitulum and trochlea that accommodate parts of the radius and ulna during elbow flexion.

What is the olecranon fossa and its function?

A deep indentation on the posterior aspect of the distal epiphysis that is a continuation of the trochlea and accommodates the olecranon of the ulna when the elbow is extended.

Bones of the Forearm: Radius

Which bones make up the forearm and how are they held together?

The forearm consists of the lateral radius and medial ulna, held together by a fibrous interosseous membrane.

With which bones does the radius articulate?

Proximally with the humerus, distally with carpal bones, and with the ulna at both the proximal and distal radioulnar joints.

How does the radius change in size along its length?

It is narrow proximally and progressively enlarges distally.

What is the radial head?

The round and flattened structure of the proximal epiphysis of the radius.

What is the radial styloid process and its function?

A projection at the lateral tip of the radius that forms the lateral boundary of the wrist and provides joint stabilization.

How can you remember which side the radius is on?

The radius is on the same side as the thumb; making a “thumb’s up” gesture or taking the radial pulse can help identify it.

Bones of the Forearm: Ulna

How does the size of the ulna change along its length?

The ulna is widest at the proximal epiphysis and progressively narrows as it travels distally.

What is the olecranon of the ulna?

A palpable protrusion on the posterior aspect of the trochlear notch.

What is the coronoid process of the ulna?

The anterior lip of the trochlear notch that fits into the coronoid fossa of the humerus.

What is the ulnar head and its feature?

The distal epiphysis of the ulna; its medial side contains a small styloid process.

How can you remember the shape of the trochlear notch?

When viewed on its side, the trochlear notch resembles the letter “U” for ulna.

Bones of the Wrist: Carpals

How many bones are in the wrist (carpus) and how are they arranged?

The wrist has eight short bones (carpals) arranged in two rows of four bones each.

What are the four proximal carpal bones and their features?

Scaphoid: boat-shaped; articulates with radius

Lunate: slightly moon-shaped; articulates primarily with radius and to a lesser degree with ulna

Triquetrum: triangular; articulates with ulna

Pisiform: pea-shaped; articulates with the anterior surface of the triquetrum

What are the four distal carpal bones and their features?

Trapezium: articulates proximally with scaphoid

Trapezoid: articulates primarily with scaphoid

Capitate: rounded; articulates proximally with scaphoid and lunate

Hamate: hooklike projection; articulates proximally with triquetrum

Bones of the Hand and Fingers: Metacarpals and Phalanges

How many metacarpal bones are in the hand and how are they numbered?

The hand has five metacarpal bones, numbered I–V from lateral to medial, which articulate with the distal carpal bones and the bones of the fingers.

How many phalanges are in the fingers and how are they arranged?

There are 14 phalanges in the fingers. Each finger has three phalanges: proximal, middle, and distal.

How is the thumb (pollex) different from the other fingers in terms of phalanges?

The thumb has only two phalanges: proximal and distal.

Wrist Fractures

Why is the wrist a commonly injured region of the upper limb?

The wrist is frequently injured due to direct trauma or attempts to stop a fall with an outstretched arm.

Which bones are most commonly involved in wrist fractures?

Fractures commonly involve the distal radius and ulna, and may also involve carpal bones, particularly the scaphoid.

What is a Colles fracture?

A fracture of the distal radius, often resulting from falling on an outstretched hand.

Which age groups are most commonly affected by wrist fractures?

Children and adults aged 60–69, with incidence declining in older age.

What are the typical symptoms of a wrist fracture?

Pain and swelling over the affected area with slightly reduced mobility; inability to move the part only occurs if the bone is dislocated.

How are wrist fractures treated?

Treatment varies by severity and bone injured, ranging from simple casting to surgical intervention.

How can many wrist fractures be prevented?

By teaching individuals how to fall in a way that distributes force across the trunk instead of concentrating it on an outstretched arm.

What is the ischial body and its function?

The posterior part of the ischium that contributes to the acetabulum.

What is the ischial ramus and its function?

The anterior part of the ischium that forms a portion of the obturator foramen.

What is the ischial tuberosity and its significance?

A prominent feature on the posteroinferior aspect of the ischium that bears all of the upper body’s weight when seated.

Bones of the Pelvic Girdle and Lower Limb: Pubis

Which is the smallest bone of the coxal bone?

The pubis.

What is the general shape of the pubis?

It has three parts that approximate a C-shape.

What is the pubic symphysis?

The joint where the two pubic bodies meet, separated by a fibrocartilaginous pad.

Bones of the Pelvic Girdle and Lower Limb Males vs. Females

How does the female pelvis differ from the male pelvis?

The female pelvis is wider and shallower than the male pelvis.

What is the reason for the difference between male and female pelvises?

The female pelvis is adapted for childbirth.

The Femur and Patella

What is the femur and where is it located?

The femur is the largest and strongest bone in the body and the only bone in the thigh.

What feature is found on the proximal epiphysis of the femur?

A prominent spherical head that articulates with the acetabulum at the hip joint.

What is the neck of the femur?

The narrowed region just distal to the head.

What is the greater trochanter?

A large protuberance located lateral to the neck of the femur.

What is the lesser trochanter?

A smaller projection located medial and distal to the greater trochanter.

What is the linea aspera and its function?

A prominent line running down the posterior shaft of the femur that serves as a muscle attachment site and divides into two lines leading to the medial and lateral epicondyles.

What do the medial and lateral epicondyles lead to and articulate with?

They taper down to form the medial and lateral condyles, which articulate with the tibia at the knee joint.

Bones of the Leg: Tibia and Fibula

What are the two bones of the leg?

The tibia and fibula.

How are the tibia and fibula connected?

They are connected by an interosseous membrane.

Where do the tibia and fibula articulate with each other?

At the proximal and distal tibiofibular joints.

Bones of the Leg: Tibia

What is the tibia and what is its function?

The tibia is the larger, medial bone of the leg that bears the body’s weight.

What are the medial and lateral condyles of the tibia?

Two concave depressions at the proximal end that articulate with the femoral condyles in the knee joint.

What is the tibial tuberosity and its function?

A rough projection on the anterior surface, just distal to the condyles, where the patellar ligament attaches.

What is the anterior crest (shin) of the tibia?

A ridge along the tibial diaphysis that forms the shin.

What is the medial malleolus and where is it located?

A palpable lump of bone at the distal end of the tibia that forms part of the ankle.

Bones of the Leg: Fibula

What is the fibula and how much weight does it bear?

The fibula is the smaller, lateral bone of the leg and bears only about one-sixth of the body weight carried by the tibia.

What is the lateral malleolus and its function?

A palpable expansion at the distal end of the fibula that articulates with the lateral talus and helps stabilize the ankle joint.

How can you remember the difference between the tibia and fibula?

TIBia = Thick, Inner Bone; FibuLA = LAteral bone.

Bones of Ankle and Foot: Tarsals, Metatarsals, and Phalanges

How many tarsal bones are in the ankle and what is their function?

There are seven tarsal bones in the ankle, which connect the leg to the foot.

Which tarsal bones are proximal?

The talus, calcaneus, and navicular.

Which tarsal bones are distal and their order from medial to lateral?

Medial cuneiform, intermediate cuneiform, lateral cuneiform, and cuboid.

What is special about the talus?

It is dome-shaped and the only tarsal bone that articulates with the bones of the leg.

How does the talus articulate with the calcaneus?

The underside (plantar surface) of the talus articulates with the dorsal surface of the calcaneus.

What is the function of the posterior surface of the calcaneus?

It provides an attachment site for the calcaneal (Achilles) tendon.

How does the distal head of the talus articulate?

It articulates with the boat-shaped navicular bone.

Bones of Ankle and Foot: Tarsals, Metatarsals, and Phalanges

How many metatarsal bones are in the foot and how are they numbered?

There are five metatarsals, numbered I–V from medial to lateral, each with a proximal base, middle shaft, and distal head.

How many phalanges are in the toes and how are they arranged?

There are 14 phalanges; toes II–V have proximal, middle, and distal phalanges, while the great toe (hallux) has only proximal and distal phalanges.
