Chapter 8: Articulations

Module 8.1 Overview of Joints

Function of Joints

What are the three functions of joints?

Enable movement, provide stability, and allow long bones to lengthen.

How do joints enable movement?

By linking bones so muscles and tendons can exert force across the joint.

How do joints provide stability?

Some joints allow limited or no movement, making them very stable, which is critical for structures like the skull to protect underlying tissues.

How do joints allow long bones to lengthen?

The epiphyseal plate acts as a temporary joint where long bones grow in length during skeletal development.

Structural Classification

What are the three structural classifications of joints?

Fibrous joints, cartilaginous joints, and synovial joints.

How are fibrous joints structured?

Bones are fastened together by dense regular collagenous connective tissue and have no joint space between them.

How are cartilaginous joints structured?

Bones are fastened together with cartilage and have no joint space.

How are synovial joints structured?

Each bone has a layer of hyaline cartilage on its articulating surface, and a fluid-filled joint cavity separates the bones.

Module 8.2 Fibrous and Cartilaginous Joints

Fibrous Joints

Suture: What is it and where is it found?

An immoveable joint between edges of bones, making up the cranium; fully fused sutures are very stable and protect the brain.

Gomphosis: What is it and what connects the bones?

An immoveable joint (synarthrosis) between each tooth and its bony socket in the jaw, connected by the periodontal ligament, a strong fibrous membrane.

Syndesmosis: What is it and what is its function?

A joint between the tibia and fibula or ulna and radius, joined by an interosseous membrane or ligament of dense regular collagenous connective tissue

allowing a small amount of movement (amphiarthroses).

Cartilaginous Joints

What defines cartilaginous joints?

Articulating bones are held together by cartilage, lack a joint cavity, and allow little to no motion.

What is a synchondrosis and give examples?

Bones linked by hyaline cartilage; synarthroses.

Examples include epiphyseal plates in developing long bones, the first sternocostal joint, and costochondral joints.

What is the function of epiphyseal plates?

They connect the diaphysis and epiphysis of developing long bones and are replaced with bone during maturation.

What is a symphysis and give examples?

Bones united by a fibrocartilaginous pad; functionally amphiarthroses.

Examples include intervertebral joints and the pubic symphysis.

What is the primary function of a symphysis?

To resist compression and tension while allowing slight motion.

Module 8.3 Structure of Synovial Joints

Structural Elements of Synovial joints

What is a joint cavity (synovial cavity)?

The space between articulating bones in a synovial joint.

What is the articular capsule and its structure?

A double-layered structure with an outer fibrous layer that keeps bones together and an inner synovial membrane that secretes synovial fluid.

What are the three main functions of synovial fluid?

1. Lubrication – reduces friction between articulating surfaces.

2. Transportation – moves nutrients and waste in the absence of blood vessels.

3. Shock absorption – distributes stress and force on articular surfaces during movement.

Structural Elements of Synovial joints

What is articular cartilage and its function?

A thin layer of hyaline cartilage that provides a smooth surface for bones to interact, reduces wear and tear from friction, and is avascular.

How does articular cartilage receive oxygen and nutrients?

It relies on synovial fluid for oxygen, nutrients, and waste removal.

What other components are present in a synovial joint?

Adipose tissue, nerves, and blood vessels.

Stabilizing and Supporting Factors

What is the trade-off between mobility and stability in synovial joints?

Synovial joints allow more mobility but less stability.

What is a ligament and its function?

A strand of dense, regular collagenous connective tissue that links one bone to another and provides additional strength and reinforcement to the joint.

What is a tendon and its function?

A structural component of skeletal muscle made of dense, regular collagenous connective tissue that connects muscle to bone

it crosses joints and provides stabilization when muscles contract.

Stabilizing and Supporting Factors

What is muscle tone and its role in joint stability?

Continuous low-level muscle contraction that helps stabilize joints.

What are bursae and tendon sheaths and their functions?

Bursae are synovial fluid-filled fibrous structures that minimize friction between moving parts of joints.

Tendon sheaths are elongated bursae that surround tendons and protect them as they slide across joints.

Bursitis

Bursitis: What is it and what causes it?

Inflammation of a bursa, which can result from a single traumatic event, repetitive movements, or inflammatory diseases like rheumatoid arthritis.

What are the most common sites of bursitis?

Shoulder, elbow, hip, and knee.

What are the clinical features of bursitis?

Pain at rest and with motion, tenderness, swelling, and warmth of the affected joint.

How is bursitis treated?

Rest, ice, compression, anti-inflammatory steroid injections, or fluid removal from the bursa to relieve swelling.

What can happen if bursitis is left untreated?

It can become chronically painful and increasingly difficult to cure.

Arthritis

What is arthritis?

Inflammation of one or more joints, causing pain and limitations in joint movement.

What is osteoarthritis and its causes?

The most common form of arthritis, caused by wear and tear, injuries, and advanced age, resulting in pain, joint stiffness, and lost mobility.

What is rheumatoid arthritis?

Joint destruction mediated by the individual’s own immune system.

What is gouty arthritis?

Joint damage caused by an inflammatory reaction to uric acid crystal deposits.

Module 8.4 Function of Synovial Joints

Functional Classes of Synovial Joints

How are synovial joints classified based on movement axes?

They are grouped as nonaxial, uniaxial, biaxial, and multiaxial (triaxial) joints.

What is a nonaxial joint?

A joint that allows motion in one or more planes without rotating around an axis.

What is a uniaxial joint?

A joint that allows motion around only one axis.

What is a biaxial joint?

A joint that allows motion around two axes.

What is a multiaxial (triaxial) joint?

A joint that allows motion around three axes.

Uniaxial/ Biaxial

What type of joint is the elbow and how does it move?

Uniaxial hinge joint

allowing motion in one plane perpendicular to its axis.

What type of joint are the metacarpophalangeal joints and how do they move?

Biaxial joints between the proximal phalanges and metacarpals.

Axis 1: allows fingers to move toward and away from the palm.

Axis 2: allows fingers to be squeezed together or fanned out.

Multiaxial

What is an example of a multiaxial joint and its movements?

The shoulder joint.

Axis 1: humerus moves forward and backward (arm swing while walking).

Axis 2: humerus moves away from and toward the body (jumping jacks).

Axis 3: humerus rotates in a circular fashion (throwing a Frisbee).

Types of Movements at Synovial Joints

What are gliding movements at synovial joints?

Sliding motions between articulating surfaces; nonaxial. Bones slide past each other in a single plane, such as in the intercarpal joints of the hand.

Example: wiggling your wrist side to side

What are angular movements at synovial joints?

Movements that increase or decrease the angle between articulating bones.

What is flexion?

A movement that decreases the angle between bones by bringing them closer together.

Example: When doing a bicep curl, when the dumbell is that the top that is a flexion

What is extension?

A movement that increases the angle between bones, moving them away from each other; opposite of flexion.

Example: when doing a bicep curl, when the dumbell is at the bottom, that is an extension

What is hyperextension?

Extension beyond the anatomical position of a joint.

Example: moving your whole leg backward, beyond the normal rotation

Angular Movements at Synovial Joints

What is abduction?

Motion of a body part away from the midline of the body or another reference point.

Example: Doing a T-pose, your hand is no longer medial

Example: Spreading out your fingers or toes

What is adduction in angular movements?

Motion of a body part toward the midline of the body or another reference point.

Example: Putting fingers/toes back to its original position after spreading them out

Example: Returning your hand to the original anatomical position from the T-pose

What is circumduction in angular movements?

A unpaired angular movement where a freely movable distal bone moves in a cone-shaped motion on a fixed proximal bone; combines flexion-extension and abduction-adduction.

Example: making a circle with your whole arm

Types of Movements at Synovial Joints

What is rotation in joint movements?

A nonangular motion where one bone rotates around an imaginary line running down its longitudinal axis.

Example: Rotating your head to say no, rotating your foot in a circle

Types of Movements at Synovial Joints

What are opposition and reposition movements?

Opposition allows the thumb to move across the palmar surface of the hand at the first carpometacarpal joint.

Reposition is the opposite movement that returns the thumb to the anatomical position.

What are depression and elevation movements?

Depression moves a body part in an inferior direction.

Example: Opening your mouth, which lowers your mandible

Elevation moves a body part in a superior direction.

Example: closing your mouth, which raises your mandible

Types of Movements at Synovial Joints

What are protraction and retraction movements?

Protraction moves a body part in an anterior direction.

Example: Moving your lower Jaw Forward

Retraction moves a body part in a posterior direction.

Example: Moving your lower jaw backward

What are inversion and eversion movements?

Inversion rotates the plantar surface of the foot medially toward the midline of the body.

Example: On your right foot, the side that contains your thumb, index, and middle toe is raised up, while the ring/pinky stay on the ground. So basically half of your foot is on the ground, the other half is raised up.

Eversion rotates the foot laterally away from the midline.

Example: On your right foot, the side that contains your thumb, index, and middle toe is Stayed down, while the ring/pinky stay Rises up. So basically half of your foot is on the ground, the other half is raised up.

Special Movements at Synovial Joints

What are dorsiflexion and plantarflexion movements?

Dorsiflexion decreases the angle between the foot and the leg.

Example: raising your foot up, while the heel of the foot stays on the ground

Plantarflexion increases the angle between the foot and the leg.

Example: standing on your tippy toes

What are supination and pronation movements?

Supination: rotational movement where the palm faces anteriorly (thumb lateral, anatomical position).

Example: holding a bowl of SOUP

Note: Both start with S

Pronation: rotational movement where the palm faces posteriorly (thumb medial).

Example: POURING the bowl of soup

Note: both start with P

Range of Motion

What does the range of motion of a joint refer to?

The amount of movement a joint is capable of under normal circumstances.

What is the range of motion of the knee?

The movement of the knee from a relaxed state to full flexion and then back to full extension.

How does range of motion vary by joint type?

Uniaxial joints, like the knee, tend to have the smallest range of motion, while multiaxial joints, like the shoulder, tend to have the greatest range of motion.

Module 8.5 Types of Synovial Joints

Types of Synovial Joints

What is a plane (gliding) joint?

The simplest and least mobile synovial joint, where flat surfaces of two bones slide past each other.

Nonaxial

Example: intercarpal Joint

What is a hinge joint?

A synovial joint where the convex articular surface of one bone fits into the concave depression of another

allowing uniaxial movement.

Example: Elbow

What is a pivot joint?

A synovial joint where the rounded end of one bone fits into a groove on another bone, allowing uniaxial rotation around the other bone.

Example: Atlantoaxial joint (between the atlas and axis vertebrae in the neck) – allows you to rotate your head “no”.

Types of Synovial Joints

What is a condylar (ellipsoid) joint?

A biaxial joint where the oval, convex surface of one bone fits into a shallow, concave articular surface of another bone.

Examples: Radiocarpal joint (wrist joint), metacarpophalangeal joints of fingers (knuckles)

Allows flexion/extension and abduction/adduction.

What is a saddle joint?

A biaxial synovial joint where each bone’s articulating surface has both concave and convex regions, allowing a great deal of motion.

Example: Carpometacarpal joint of the thumb (between trapezium and first metacarpal)

Allows flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, and limited circumduction.

What is a ball-and-socket joint?

A multiaxial joint where a spherical surface of one bone fits into a cup-shaped depression of another, allowing a wide range of motion around all three axes.

Examples: Shoulder joint (glenohumeral), hip joint (acetabulofemoral)

Allows flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, rotation, and circumduction in multiple axes.

Specific Hinge Joints: Elbow

What are the key features of the elbow joint?

The elbow is a very stable hinge joint with two articulations and three strong ligaments supporting the articular capsule.

What is the humeroulnar joint?

The larger articulation of the elbow, between the trochlea of the humerus and the trochlear notch of the ulna.

What is the humeroradial joint?

The articulation between the capitulum of the humerus and the head of the radius.

What is the function of the radial collateral ligament?

Supports the lateral side of the elbow joint.

What is the function of the ulnar collateral ligament?

Supports the medial side of the elbow joint.

What is the function of the anular ligament?

Binds the head of the radius to the neck of the ulna and stabilizes the radial head.

Specific Hinge Joints: Knee

What is the function of the Tibiofemoral joint?

Articulation between femoral and tibial condyles

What is the function of the patellar ligament?

Distal continuation of the quadriceps tendon; connects the distal patella to the anterior tibia.

What are the medial and lateral menisci?

C-shaped fibrocartilaginous pads between the femoral and tibial condyles that provide shock absorption and stability.

What is the function of the tibial (medial) collateral ligament?

Connects the femur, medial meniscus, and tibia; provides medial joint stabilization and prevents the tibia from shifting too far laterally on the femur.

What is the function of the fibular (lateral) collateral ligament?

Connects the femur to the fibula; prevents the tibia from moving too far medially on the femur.

Specific Hinge Joints: Knee

What are cruciate ligaments in the knee?

Stabilizing ligaments located within the joint cavity that control anterior-posterior movement of the tibia.

What is the function of the anterior cruciate ligament (ACL)?

Extends from the anterior tibia to the posterior femur; prevents hyperextension of the knee and stops the tibia from moving too far anteriorly on the femur.

What is the function of the posterior cruciate ligament (PCL)?

Extends from the posterior tibia to the anterior femur; prevents the tibia from displacing posteriorly.

Knee Injuries and the Unhappy Triad

Why is the knee joint susceptible to injury?

Because of quick changes in direction or lateral or posterior blows, which can stress its ligaments and menisci.

What occurs in a lateral blow to the knee?

The tibial collateral ligament may rupture, transmitting force to the menisci and often resulting in tearing of the lateral meniscus, sometimes the medial meniscus, and occasionally the anterior cruciate ligament, forming the "unhappy triad."

Which other ligaments can be damaged in knee injuries?

The fibular (lateral) collateral and posterior collateral ligaments, though damage to these is less common.

Specific Ball and Socket Joints: Shoulder

What bones form the Nshoulder (glenohumeral) joint?

The ball-shaped head of the humerus articulates with the glenoid cavity of the lateral scapula.

What is the function of the glenoid labrum?

A fibrocartilaginous ring that increases the depth of the glenoid cavity, providing more stability to the multiaxial joint.

How does the biceps brachii tendon contribute to shoulder stability?

It passes over the joint, providing a stabilizing force that helps keep the head of the humerus within the glenoid cavity.

Which ligaments support the articular capsule of the shoulder?

The coracohumeral ligament and the three glenohumeral ligaments.

Which muscles form the rotator cuff and what is their function?

The supraspinatus, infraspinatus, subscapularis, and teres minor; they provide most of the shoulder joint’s structural stabilization and strength.

Specific Ball and Socket Joints: Hip

What bones form the hip (coxal) joint?

The ball-shaped head of the femur articulates with the acetabulum of the pelvic bone.

What is the function of the acetabular labrum?

A fibrocartilaginous ring that helps stabilize the head of the femur within the acetabulum.

What are retinacular fibers and their role in the hip joint?

Intracapsular ligaments that surround the neck of the femur and reinforce the joint capsule.

Which ligaments provide stability to the hip joint and where are they located?

Iliofemoral ligament: Y-shaped, reinforces anterior capsule

Ischiofemoral ligament: spiral-shaped, supports posterior capsule

Pubofemoral ligament: triangular-shaped, supports inferior capsule

What is the ligament of the head of the femur and its function?

A small ligament connecting the femoral head with the acetabulum, providing a pathway for small blood vessels to the femoral head.
