the 5 classes of bone (by shape)

Long bones

longer than they are wide; include most bones in arms and legs

Ex: femur, humerus

Function: support + movement

Short bones

roughly cube-shaped or about as long as they are wide; include bones of wrist or carpals and ankle or tarsals

Ex: carpals, tarsals

Function: stability + some movement

Flat bones

thin and broad bones; include ribs, pelvis, sternum (breastbone), and most skull bones

Ex: skull, ribs, sternum

Function: protection + muscle attachment

Irregular bones

include vertebrae and certain skull bones; do not fit into other classes because of irregular shapes

Ex: vertebrae, hip bones

Function: varied (protection, support)

Sesamoid bones

specialized bones located within tendons; usually small, flat, and oval-shaped; give tendons mechanical advantage; give muscles better leverage; patella (kneecap) is example

Ex: patella

Function: reduce friction + protect tendons

External Anatomy of Long Bones

Diaphysis

Long shaft

covered with thin layer of hyaline cartilage (articular cartilage) found within joints (articulations) between bones

Epiphyses

Ends of the bone

Epiphyseal lines

separate both proximal and distal epiphyses from diaphysis;

remnant of epiphyseal plates (growth plates); line of hyaline cartilage found in developing bones of children

Articular cartilage

Hyaline cartilage on epiphyses

Periosteum

Outer covering; dense connective tissue

membrane composed of dense irregular collagenous connective tissue; rich with blood vessels and nerves; surrounds outer surface of long bones

Perforating fibers (Sharpey’s fibers)

made of collagen;

anchors periosteum firmly to underlying bone surface by penetrating deep into bone matrix

Nutrient foramen

Hole for blood vessels

Internal Anatomy of Long Bones

Compact bone

Dense outer layer

allows bone to resist linear compression and twisting forces among other stresses

Spongy bone (trabecular)

In epiphyses; contains red marrow

second bone texture; inside cortical bone;

honeycomb-like framework of bony struts;

allows long bones to resist forces from many directions; provides cavity for bone marrow

Medullary cavity

Hollow shaft space

within diaphysis contains either red or yellow bone marrow, depending on bone and age of individual

Contains yellow marrow (fat)

Children = red bone marrow

Adults = yellow bone marrow (fat storage)

Endosteum

Inner lining of medullary cavity

Bony struts of spongy bone and all inner surfaces of bone are covered by thin membrane;

contain different populations of bone cells involved in maintenance of bone homeostasis

Red bone marrow

Hematopoiesis (blood cell production)

Osteons

Structural units of compact bone

Structure of short, flat, irregular, and sesamoid bones

Do not have diaphyses, epiphyses, medullary cavities, epiphyseal lines, or epiphyseal plates

Covered by periosteum, with associated perforating fibers, blood vessels, and nerves

Two outer layers of thin compact bone with middle layer of spongy bone (diploë) and associated bone marrow

Sinuses: some flat and irregular bones of skull contain hollow, air-filled spaces, which reduce bone weight

Blood supply to bones

Blood supply to short, flat, irregular, and sesamoid bones is provided mostly by vessels in periosteum that penetrate bone

Long bones get third of their blood supply from periosteum; mostly supplies compact bone

Remaining two-thirds supplied by one or two nutrient arteries; enter bone through small hole in diaphysis (nutrient foramen)

Bone Marrow

Red bone marrow

loose connective tissue; supports islands of blood-forming hematopoietic cells

Amount of red marrow decreases with age

Red marrow in adult is only in pelvis, proximal femur and humerus, vertebrae, ribs, sternum, clavicles, scapulae, and some bones of skull

Children need more red marrow to assist in growth and development

Yellow bone marrow

triglycerides, blood vessels, and adipocytes

composition of the extracellular (organic and inorganic) matrix of bone

Organic Portion

about 1/3 of matrix

known as osteoid

Collagen – predominant protein fiber; forms cross-links with one another; helps bone resist torsion (twisting) and tensile (pulling or stretching) forces

Collagen fibers align themselves with hydroxyapatite crystals; enhances hardness of

bone

Glycosaminoglycans and proteoglycans - create an osmotic gradient; draw water into osteoid; help tissue resist compression

Cells contribute: osteoblasts make osteoid

Function:

gives flexibility

resists stretching/twisting

Inorganic Portion

about 2/3 of matrix

Inorganic matrix – predominantly calcium salts; bone stores around 85% of total calcium ions as well as large amount of phosphorus:

Hydroxyapatite crystals - Calcium and phosphorus salts exist as large molecules of hydroxyapatite crystal

Crystalline structure makes bone one of hardest substances in body; strong

and resistant to compression

Function:

gives hardness

resists compression

the 3 types of bone cells and their functions

1. Osteoblasts

Build bone

Produce osteoid (organic matrix)

Initiate calcification

2. Osteocytes

Mature bone cells

Maintain bone matrix

Monitor stress on bone

3. Osteoclasts

Break down bone (resorption)

Breaks down bone by secreting hydrogen ions (H+) and enzymes. IMPORTANT!

the structure of compact bone

Osteons (Haversian systems)

Cylindrical units

Central canal

Contains blood vessels + nerves to supply osteon

Lamellae

Rings of calcified matrix

Lacunae

Small spaces housing osteocytes

For bone maintenance

Canaliculi

Tiny channels connecting lacunae

to help with sharing resources (oxygen, nutrients) & communicate

Interstitial lamellae

Lamellae outside of the osteon

Remnants of resorbed osteons

Fill gaps between forming osteons

Circumferential lamellae

Extend around the entire circumference of the diaphysis

Just deep to the periosteum

Resist twisting of the entire bone

Perforating (Volkmann’s) canals

Connect central canals horizontally

Structure function: strong, resists compression, forms outer shell of all bones

Structure of Spongy bone

Usually not weight-bearing like compact bone

Much less densely packed

Network of struts reinforces compact bone - resists forces from variety of directions

Protects bone marrow tissue

Composed of Trabeculae – struts of bone; covered with endosteum

Trabeculae

Lattice-like network of bone plates

No osteons

Structure is irregular

Spaces between trabeculae

Filled with red bone marrow

Lacunae with osteocytes

Canaliculi

Connect cells within trabeculae

For communication

Structure function: lightweight, absorbs stress from many directions, supports marrow
