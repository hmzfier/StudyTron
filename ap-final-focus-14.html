the 4 general types of movements at synovial joint

1. Gliding

sliding motion between articulating surfaces; nonaxial

2. Angular

Flexion = decrease angle

Extension = Increase angle

Hyperextension = extension beyond normal

Abduction = away from midline

Adduction = toward the midline

Circumduction = circular motion

3. Rotation

nonangular motion; one bone rotates on an imaginary line running down its middle

longitudinal axis

4. Special movements

Dorsiflexion - angle between foot and leg decreases

Plantarflexion - angle between foot and leg increases

Supination - when your palm faces anteriorly

Pronation - when your palm faces posteriorly

opposition - allows thumb to move across palmar surface of hand

Reposition - opposite movement returns thumb to anatomical position

Protraction = move body part anterior

Retraction = move body part Posterior

Depression – moves body part in inferior direction

Elevation – moves body part in superior direction

Inversion = plantar surface of foot rotates medially toward midline of body

Eversion – rotates foot laterally away from midline

6 types of synovial joints

Plane = gliding joint

most simple and least mobile articulation between flat surfaces of two bones

Hinge = uniaxial movement

Convex articular surface of one bone interacts with concave depression of second bone

Pivot = uniaxial movement

rounded end surface of one bone fits into groove on surface of second bone

Condylar = biaxial joint

oval, convex surface of one bone fits into shallow, concave articular surface of second bone

Saddle = biaxial joint

Each bone’s articulating surface has both concave and convex region

Ball and socket = multiaxial articulation

articulating surface of one bone is spherical

fits into cup-shaped depression in second bone

wide range of motion around all three available axes

the gross anatomy of the skeletal muscle

Summary:

Muscle cell (endomysium) > Fascicle (perimysium) > The entire muscle (epimysium) > Fascia (epimysium)

Muscle Cell (Myocyte):

Surrounded by endomysium (thin layer of connective tissue)

Fascicle:

Bundle of 10–100 muscle cells

Surrounded by perimysium (connective tissue)

The entire Muscle:

Not an official term

All fascicles enclosed by epimysium (outer fibrous connective tissue)

Fascia:

A bunch of entire muscles

Most superficial connective tissue layer

Continuous with epimysium (located under fascia)

Separates individual muscles from one another

the fascicles and their shapes and how that affects their function

1. Parallel Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles run parallel to the long axis of the muscle

Function: Produce great range of motion but less force

Example: Sartorius, biceps brachii

2. Fusiform Fascicles:

Description: Spindle-shaped, wider in the middle and taper at ends

Function: Moderate force and range of motion

Example: Biceps brachii

3. Circular Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles arranged in concentric rings around openings

Function: Act as sphincters, controlling opening and closing

Example: Orbicularis oris, orbicularis oculi

4. Convergent Fascicles:

Description: Broad origin and converge to a single tendon

Function: Versatile; can generate strong force

Example: Pectoralis major

5. Pennate Fascicles:

Description: Short fascicles attach obliquely to a central tendon

Function: Produce more force but less range of motion

Unipennate: Fibers on one side of tendon – extensor digitorum

Bipennate: Fibers on both sides of tendon – rectus femoris

Multipennate: Tendon branches within muscle – deltoid

6.Spiral Fascicles

Description: Fascicles wrap around a bone or another structure in a spiral pattern.

Function: Can twist during contraction, allowing complex movements and transfer of force along a curved path.

Example: Supinator (forearm), latissimus dorsi (partially spiral orientation)

the functions of skeletal muscle

Movement: Contractions move bones at joints.

Heat Production: Contractions generate heat, helping maintain body temperature.

Respiration: Diaphragm contraction enables breathing.

Facial Expression: Muscles attached to skin produce expressions.

Swallowing: Throat muscles assist in moving food.

Control of Openings: Skeletal muscle sphincters allow voluntary control of body openings.

the functional groups of muscles working together

Agonists (prime movers) – provide most force for muscle Action

Antagonists – opposes action of agonist

allows for modulation and control of agonist movement

Synergists – aid agonists

supplies supplemental force

minimizes unwanted movement

help stabilize joints

provide for more efficient movement

Fixators – provide stabilizing force that anchors bone

provides movement efficiency

Protects muscle from injury

the muscles that: Move our eyes

Epicranial aponeurosis - aise eyebrows and skin of forehead

Orbicularis oculi - Closes the eyelids (blinking, squinting).

Levator palpebrae superioris - Opens upper eyelid.

Corrugator supercilii - Draws eyebrows together, producing a frown and wrinkles above the nose.

Frontalis muscle - Elevates eyebrows. Wrinkles skin of forehead horizontally

Superior rectus - Moves eye up and slightly medially

Inferior rectus - Moves eye down and slightly medially

Medial rectus - Moves eye medially (toward nose)

Lateral rectus -Moves eye laterally (away from nose)

Superior oblique - Moves eye down and laterally

Inferior oblique - Moves eye up and laterally

the muscles that: Help with ventilation

Diaphragm - Main muscle of inspiration; flattens to increase thoracic volume.

External intercostals - Elevate ribs during inspiration.

Internal intercostals - Depress ribs during forced expiration.

the muscles that: Help move our limbs

Deltoid - Abducts, flexes, and extends the shoulder.

Biceps brachii - Flexes elbow, supinates forearm.

Triceps brachii - Extends elbow.

Brachialis - Flexes elbow.

Pectoralis major - Flexes, adducts, and medially rotates arm.

Latissimus dorsi - Extends, adducts, and medially rotates arm.

Gluteus maximus - Extends and laterally rotates hip.

Gluteus medius/minimus - Abduct and medially rotate hip.

Quadriceps femoris - Extends knee; rectus femoris also flexes hip.

Hamstrings - Flex knee, extend hip.

Gastrocnemius & Soleus - Plantarflex ankle.

Tibialis anterior - Dorsiflexes ankle.

Rotator Cuff Muscles

Supraspinatus: Abducts the humerus

Infraspinatus: Laterally rotates the humerus

Teres minor: Laterally rotates the humerus

Subscapularis: Adducts and Medially rotates the humerus
