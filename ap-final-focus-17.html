the classification of neurons by structural features

Multipolar Neuron

Motor (efferent) neurons, interneurons

99% of all neurons

One axon with 2 or more dendrites

Most neurons in the CNS, motor neurons in the PNS

Bipolar Neuron

Sensory (afferent) neurons

One axon and one dendrite

Special sense organs in the PNS, such as the retina and olfactory epithelium

Pseudounipolar Neuron

Sensory (afferent) neurons

Two axons with no dendrites

Sensory neurons in the PNS associated with touch, pain, and vibration

the different ion channels of the neuron plasma membrane

Leak channels: always open; allow continuous ion flow to maintain resting membrane potential.

Ligand-gated channels: open when a chemical (neurotransmitter) binds; create local potentials.

Voltage-gated channels: open/close in response to membrane voltage changes; generate action potentials.

Mechanically-gated channels: open when the membrane is physically deformed (pressure, stretch).

what local potentials are and how they are different from action potentials

Local potentials

Same size - action potentials always depolarize to about +30 mV and their size doesn’t change with stimulus strength, frequency, or duration.

Depolarization: voltage-gated sodium channels open, potassium channels closed.

Compare and contrast the 2 refractory phases

Absolute refractory period: no new action potential can occur at all because sodium channels are open or inactivated.

Relative refractory period :after absolute refractory period; only strong stimulus can produce action potential

▪ takes much larger stimulus to trigger action potential

how action potential propagate down the axon and what affects the speed of the action potential

Steps

1. The axolemma depolarizes to threshold due to local potentials

2. As sodium channels activate, an action potential is triggered and spreads down the axon

3. the next section of the axolemma depolarizes to threshold and fires an action potential as the previous section of the axolemma repolarizes

4. the current continues to move down the axon, and the process repeats
