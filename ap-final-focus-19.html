the basal nuclei location and function. Also how it prevents spontaneous, inappropriate muscle contractions

Location:

Deep inside the cerebrum, buried within the cerebral hemispheres.

They surround and flank the thalamus and include structures such as the caudate nucleus, putamen, and globus pallidus.

Function:

• Regulate movement

• Start and stop voluntary movements smoothly

• Help with motor planning and coordination

• Suppress unwanted or inappropriate movements

• Influence posture and muscle tone

• Contribute to habit formation and certain cognitive functions

How they prevent spontaneous, inappropriate muscle contractions:

The basal nuclei inhibit motor neurons at rest.

They do this by sending constant inhibitory signals (mainly GABA) to motor pathways.

This steady inhibition stops random or unintended muscle activity.

the location and function of the limbic system

Location: A group of 4 structures (fornix, limbic lobe (region of medial cerebrum), hippocampus, amygdala, and pathways) deep in the cerebrum around the diencephalon.

Function: Involved in memory, learning, emotion, and behavior

the diencephalon, know its location, the function of the thalamus and hypothalamus and pineal gland

And subthalamus and the location and function of the cerebellum

Location of diencephalon: Deep in the brain, between the cerebrum and brainstem.

Thalamus: Main relay station for sensory information going to the cortex.

Hypothalamus: perform several vital functions critical to survival (regulation of autonomic

nervous system, sleep/wake cycle, thirst and hunger, and body temperature)

Pineal gland: Produces melatonin and regulates sleep–wake cycles.

Subthalamus - functionally connected with basal nuclei; together control movement

Cerebellum

Location - Posterior part of the brain, below the occipital lobes, behind the brainstem.

Function - Coordinates voluntary movements, maintains balance and posture, and fine-tunes motor activities.

the 3 divisions of the brainstem and their location and functions

Midbrain – top of the brainstem; Functions in movement, sensation, and reflexes

Pons – middle section; regulation of movement, breathing, reflexes, and complex functions associated with sleep and arousal

Medulla oblongata – lowest section; controls vital functions like heart rate, breathing, and blood pressure.

the location and function of the reticular formation

Location: A network of over 100 neurons running through the brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla).

Function:

▪Function in sleep, pain transmission, and mood

▪Serve motor functions for both skeletal muscles and autonomic nervous system

▪Involved in homeostasis of breathing and blood pressure

▪Play role in sensation and alertness and activity levels of cerebral cortex

the area of brains that associated with language and what would happen if these areas stopped functioning

Broca’s area – produces speech.

If damaged: you know what you want to say but can’t form words (non-fluent speech).

Wernicke’s area – understands language.

If damaged: you can speak fluently but the words don’t make sense and you can’t understand language.

the 3 meningeal layers and 3 spaces that help protect the brain

Meningeal layers: dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater.

dura mater- thickest and most durable meningeal layer. double-layered membrane (periosteal and meningeal dura)

arachnoid mater- thin weblike membrane

pia mater-– deepest meningeal layer. Has physical contact with brain tissue. Permeable to substances in brain extracellular fluid and CSF. Allows for substances to move between fluid compartments

Spaces: epidural, subdural, subarachnoid.

epidural space-”Potentially” between skull and dura mater. Only allows passage of blood vessels

subdural space- serous fluid-filled space. houses veins that drain blood from brain

subarachnoid space –contains major blood vessels of brain. filled with CSF

the location of the 4 ventricles

• Lateral ventricles – one in each cerebral hemisphere.

• Third ventricle – in the diencephalon.

• Fourth ventricle – between pons/medulla and cerebellum.

Connections:

Lateral ventricles → interventricular foramen → third ventricle → cerebral aqueduct → fourth ventricle → subarachnoid space.

how Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is produced

Produced mostly in choroid plexuses (and in ependymal cells), where blood plasma is filtered and modified into CSF.

the blood brain barrier: how it’s formed, what it allows to pass through and what it prevents from penetrating the brain

Composed of simple squamous epithelial cells (endothelial cells) of blood capillaries, their basal laminae, and astrocytes

Endothelial cells form tight junctions to prevent fluids and molecules from passing between them

Substances that can pass through blood-brain barrier are: water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and nonpolar, lipid-based molecules

Protein channels or carriers allow for passage of other essential molecules across blood-brain barrier are: glucose, amino acids, and ions

Most large, polar molecules CAN’T cross blood-brain barrier

Barrier is protective, but can hinder access of medications into brain
