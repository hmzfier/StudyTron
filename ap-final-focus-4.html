Active Transport via Membrane Proteins - uniport, Symport, Antiport

Three types of pumps in plasma membrane:

• Uniport – transport single substance through membrane in one direction (into or out of cell)

• Symport – transport two or more substances through membrane in same direction (into or out of cell)

• Antiport – transport two or more substances in opposite directions through membrane

Active Transport via Vesicles

Active transport using carrier proteins and channels is effective but has limitations; large polar macromolecules are too big to fit:

• Vesicles – small sacs filled with large molecules too big to transport by other means

• Enclosed in phospholipid bilayer; allows fusion with or formation from plasma membrane or other membrane-bound organelles

• Active transport process; requires energy from ATP

Endocytosis and the 3 types

A type of active transport

• Endocytosis – fluid, molecules taken into cell

3 types of Endocytosis

1. Phagocytosis (“cell eating”) –cells ingest large particles like bacteria or dead or damaged cells or parts of cell

2. Pinocytosis (fluid-phase endocytosis or “cell drinking”) – cells engulf fluid droplets from E C F

3. Receptor-mediated endocytosis – special form of pinocytosis; receptors fill vesicles with specific molecule (cholesterol, hormones, iron)

Exocytosis

A type of active transport

large molecules exit cell (secretion); vesicles fuse with plasma membrane, opening into ECF

Primary and secondary transport

These are both active transport

Primary Active Transport

Directly uses ATP

Pumps molecules against their gradient

Example: Sodium–potassium pump

Memory tip:

“Primary = ATP pump.”

Secondary Active Transport

Does NOT use ATP directly

Uses the energy from one molecule moving down its gradient to push another against its gradient

Depends on gradients created by primary pumps

Two types:

Symport: both molecules move same direction

Antiport: molecules move opposite directions
