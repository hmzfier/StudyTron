the cytoskeleton’s 3 types of filaments

1. Microfilaments (Actin Filaments)

Smallest filaments

Supports the plasma membranes

Form the core of microvilli

Involved in cell motion (phagocyte crawling) and cell division

Memory: “Actin = action + shape.”

2. Intermediate Filaments

Form the framework of the cell

Support the shape and size of the nucleus

Provide cell strength

Help the cell and tissue to withstand mechanical stress

Memory: “Intermediate = internal support.”

3. Microtubules

Support the cell

Maintain the position of organelles

Associate with motor proteins that move vesicles and organelles throughout the cell

Form the core of cilia and flagella

Memory: “Microtubules = transport + division.”

Cellular Extensions - microvilli - cillia - flagella

Microvilli

Finger-like extensions of plasma membrane with actin filament core; help maintain shape;

Increase surface area of cells in organs specialized for absorption

Cilia

Hair-like projections composed of microtubules and motor proteins

Move in unison to propel substances past cells

Found in great numbers on each cell

Flagella

Solitary; longer than cilia

Found only on sperm cells

Beat in whiplike fashion; propel entire cell

Flagella and cilia are structurally similar to centrioles except they contain two central microtubules not found in centrioles

the structure and function of the nucleus

Structure

Nuclear envelope – membrane that surrounds nucleoplasm (cytosol-like gel containing many components – water, free nucleotides, enzymes, other proteins, DNA, and RNA)

• DNA and associated proteins in nucleoplasm are loose structural arrangement (chromatin)

• One or more nucleoli are suspended in nucleoplasm

Function

Stores DNA (cell’s genetic instructions)

Controls cell activities (growth, repair, protein production)

Makes ribosomes in the nucleolus

Regulates what enters/leaves through nuclear pores

How many chromosomes do humans have

Human cells contain two sets of 23 chromosomes; one maternal and one paternal set (46 total)

the protein synthesis: from transcription through translation

1. Transcription

in nucleus

DNA → mRNA

RNA polymerase copies a gene into mRNA

mRNA leaves nucleus through a pore

Memory: “Transcription = write mRNA.”

2. Translation

in cytoplasm at ribosomes

mRNA → protein

Ribosome reads mRNA codons

tRNA brings amino acids

Amino acids link to form a protein

Memory: “Translation = build protein.”

Complete order

DNA > Transcription > mRNA > Translation > Protein

the define of histology and tissue

Histology

The study of tissues.

Tissue

A group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function.

the 2 basic components of all tissues

1. Cells – the living part

2. Extracellular matrix (ECM) – the nonliving material around the cells

the components and functions of extracellular matrix

Components - 2

1. Ground Substance - Gel-like fluid. Made of water, ions, sugars, proteins and 3 families of macromolecules:

Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) polysaccharide chains– chondroitin sulfate (small) and hyaluronic acid (enormous)

Proteoglycans – GAGs bound to protein core (resembles bottle brush)

Cell-adhesion molecules (CAMs) – different types of glycoproteins

2. Protein Fibers:

• Collagen: strength

• Elastic fibers: stretch

• Reticular fibers: support/networks

Functions

Support and structure

Bind tissues together

Regulate water, nutrients, and ions

Strength + flexibility

Helps cells communicate and survive

Epithelial Tissue Functions - 5

Functions

• Immune defenses – form physical barriers; prevent invasion by microorganisms; specialized cells of immune system are scattered throughout epithelial tissues to protect underlying tissues

• Secretion – form glands that produce substances like hormones and oils; secreted into blood or through ducts respectively

• Transport into other tissues – selectively permeable membranes; substances are able to cross these barriers by passive or active transport and enter other tissues on every internal and external body surface; barrier between body and external environment; line organs and fluid-filled cavities

• Protection – shield underlying tissues from mechanical and thermal injury

• Sensation – most associated with rich nerve supply; detect changes in internal and external environments; taste buds are examples of specialized sensory epithelial cells
