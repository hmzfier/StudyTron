what a membrane is

A membrane is a thin layer of tissue that covers, lines, or separates parts of the body.

Made of:

Epithelial tissue + connective tissue

Functions:

anchor organs in place, serve as barriers, function in immunity, and secrete various substances

all 4 types of membranes: structure, function and location

1. Mucous Membrane (Mucosa)

Line all passages opening to outside of body; respiratory passages, mouth, nasal cavity, digestive tract, and male and female reproductive tracts

Structure: Epithelium + underlying loose connective tissue (lamina propria)

Function: Secretes mucus, protects, traps particles

Location: Respiratory, digestive, urinary, reproductive tracts

2. Serous Membrane (Serosa)

Line pericardial, peritoneal, and pleural body cavities

Consist of mesothelium (thin layer of simple squamous epithelium), associated basement membrane, and layer of connective tissue

Structure: Simple squamous epithelium (mesothelium) + thin connective tissue

Function: Secretes serous fluid to reduce friction

Location: Heart (pericardium), lungs (pleura), abdomen (peritoneum)

3. Cutaneous Membrane

Refers to skin; largest organ of body; consists of

Epidermis

Outer layer of keratinized stratified squamous epithelium; tough, continuous protective surface; protects structures deep to it.

Dermis

▪ Layer of loose connective tissue beneath epidermis plus deeper layer of dense irregular connective tissue

▪ Home to many blood vessels; provides means for oxygen and nutrients to diffuse into avascular epidermis

Structure: Keratinized stratified squamous epithelium (epidermis) + dense connective tissue (dermis)

Function: Protection, barrier against pathogens, prevents water loss

Location: Skin (covers the body)

4. Synovial Membrane

line cavities surrounding freely moveable joints (knee or shoulder);

Structure: Connective tissue only (no epithelium)

Function: Secretes synovial fluid to lubricate joints

Location: Joint cavities (knees, shoulders, elbows)

the location, structure and function of the hypodermis

Not part of skin; anchors skin to deeper structures like muscle and bone

Location

Deepest layer under the dermis

Between skin and underlying muscles/bones

Structure

Made of loose connective and adipose tissues; has abundant blood supply

Functions

Insulation (keeps body heat)

Energy storage (fat reserve)

Cushioning/shock absorption

Anchors skin to underlying structures

the functions of the integumentary system

Protection - physical barrier against injury, pathogens, UV

Sensation - touch, pressure, pain, temperature receptors

Temperature regulation - sweat, blood vessel dilation/constriction

Excretion - sweat removes salts, urea, wastes

Vitamin D Synthesis - skin makes vitamin D with UV exposure

Water balance - prevents water loss, controls absorption

Immune defense - Langerhans cells in the skin fight microbes

the role of keratinocytes in the epidermis

Manufacture keratin – tough fibrous protein;

the structure and function of the layers of the epidermis

1. Stratum Basale (bottom layer)

Structure: single row of stem cells + melanocytes

Function:

Closest cells to dermal blood supply; most metabolically and mitotically active strata

Responsible for Vitamin D synthesis and replacement of dead keratinocytes (lost from superficial layers

2. Stratum Spinosum

thickest layer; metabolically and mitotically active

Structure: several layers of keratinocytes with “spiny” connections; Langerhans cells

Function: strength + flexibility; immune defense

3. Stratum Granulosum

Structure: 3–5 layers; keratinocytes filled with keratin + waterproofing lipids

Function: waterproof barrier; cells start dying

4. Stratum Lucidum

only in thick skin: palms, soles

Structure: thin, clear layer of dead keratinocytes

Function: extra protection + reduces friction

5. Stratum Corneum

top layer

Structure: outermost layer; several layers of dead, flattened keratinocytes with thickened plasma membranes, filled with keratin bundles (little else)

Function: main protective barrier; prevents water loss; resists abrasion

the other cells of the epidermis and their function

1. Melanocytes

Function: make melanin → protects against UV + gives skin color

in stratum basale;

2. Langerhans cells (dendritic cells)

Function: immune defense → detect and fight pathogens

3. Merkel cells

Function: touch receptors → detect light touch/pressure

Large numbers in regions specialized for touch: fingertips, lips, and at base of hairs

the structure and function of the 2 layers of the dermis

1. Papillary Layer

superficial

Structure: thin; loose areolar connective tissue; dermal papillae; capillaries; sensory receptors

Also contains tactile (meissner) corpuscles)

Function: nourishes epidermis; supports/anchors it; provides touch sensation; increases surface area for nutrient exchange

2. Reticular Layer

deep thicker layer; separates dermis from hypodermis; mostly dense irregular connective tissue;

Structure: thick; dense irregular connective tissue; collagen + elastic fibers; glands; hair follicles; blood vessels

Function: strength + elasticity; houses accessory structures (glands, follicles); provides structural support and flexibility

Proteoglycans - Rich in proteoglycans that draw water into ground substance; keeps skin firm and hydrated

Lamellated (Pacinian) corpuscles

sensory receptors ; respond mainly to changes in pressure and vibration

Blood vessels, sweat glands, hairs, sebaceous glands, and adipose tissue are also present

Dermal papillae

tiny projections at surface of papillary layer where it contacts epidermis

Contain tiny blood vessels (capillaries) arranged in loops;

Tactile (Meissner) corpuscles

also in dermal papillae

sensory receptors; respond to light touch stimuli

more numerous where sensation is primary function (skin of fingertips, lips, face, and external genitalia)
