The skeletal system helps maintain acid-base homeostasis by releasing phosphate and carbonate ions from bones into the blood when it becomes too acidic, buffering excess hydrogen ions and keeping blood pH stable.

Module 6.2: Microscopic Structure of Bone Tissue

What makes bone’s extracellular matrix (ECM) unique?

It contains both inorganic and organic components that provide strength and flexibility.

Note: the bone ECM is inside the bone, and surrounds bone cells.

What is the inorganic matrix of bone made of?

It is primarily composed of calcium and phosphorus salts that form hydroxyapatite crystals.

Why is the inorganic matrix important?

Provides hardness and compressive strength, allowing bones to support weight and resist compression.

What is the organic matrix of bone called and what does it contain?

It is called osteoid and consists of collagen fibers and other ECM components.

Why is the organic matrix important?

Provides flexibility and tensile strength, preventing bones from being brittle and breaking easily.

What are the three main types of bone cells?

Osteoblasts, osteocytes, and osteoclasts.

What is the function of osteoblasts?

They are responsible for bone deposition, forming new bone tissue.

What are osteocytes and how do they form?

They are mature osteoblasts that become trapped in the ECM they secreted, maintaining bone tissue.

What is the function of osteoclasts?

They are responsible for bone resorption, breaking down bone tissue.

What is compact bone primarily composed of?

Subunits called osteons, which are made up of concentric rings of bone ECM called lamellae.

What is the structure of spongy bone?

It has branching trabeculae that form a framework for bone marrow and is not composed of osteons.

Module 6.3: Bone Formation: Ossification

What is the process by which bones form called?

Ossification, or osteogenesis.

What is the first type of bone formed during ossification?

Primary bone, which is later replaced by stronger secondary bone.

What is intramembranous ossification?

A process of bone formation that occurs within a membrane composed of embryonic mesenchyme.

Intramembranous ossification is another way bones form, but it’s different from endochondral ossification. Here’s a simple explanation:

Instead of starting as cartilage, some bones start as a soft layer of tissue called mesenchyme (this is like embryonic “connective tissue”).

This soft tissue turns directly into bone without first becoming cartilage.

This is how flat bones like your skull and clavicle (collarbone) form.

So in one sentence: Intramembranous ossification is when bone forms directly from a soft tissue membrane instead of from cartilage.

What type of bone forms first in intramembranous ossification?

Spongy bone forms first at the primary ossification center.

Which bones are formed by intramembranous ossification?

Many flat bones, including the skull and clavicles, during fetal development.

What happens after spongy bone forms in intramembranous ossification?

The periosteum differentiates, and then compact bone forms.

What is endochondral ossification?

A process of bone formation that occurs within a hyaline cartilage model.

Endochondral ossification is basically how most of your bones grow from cartilage. Here’s a simple way to think about it:

Imagine your bones start out as soft cartilage shapes (like squishy molds).

Over time, this cartilage gets replaced by hard bone.

This is how long bones like your femur (thigh bone) or humerus (arm bone) grow.

So, in one sentence: Endochondral ossification is the process where cartilage is slowly turned into bone as you grow.

Where does ossification begin in endochondral ossification?

It begins with early compact bone forming in the bone collar.

What forms after the bone collar in endochondral ossification?

The inner early spongy bone forms.

What bones formed by endochondral ossification

Most bones of the body, except the skull and clavicles.

Module 6.4: Bone Growth in Length and Width

Where does longitudinal bone growth occur?

At the epiphyseal plate in long bones, which has five zones of cells.

What are the 5 zones of endochondral ossification

The five zones of endochondral ossification (in a growing long bone’s epiphyseal plate) in order from epiphysis to diaphysis are:

1.Zone of Resting (Reserve) Cartilage – small, inactive chondrocytes that anchor the epiphyseal plate to the epiphysis.

2.Zone of Proliferation – chondrocytes divide rapidly, forming columns of stacked cells.

3.Zone of Hypertrophy – chondrocytes enlarge and mature.

4.Zone of Calcification – the cartilage matrix calcifies, chondrocytes begin to die.

5.Zone of Ossification – osteoblasts invade, lay down bone matrix, forming new bone.

This sequence is essential for longitudinal bone growth.

What happens when the epiphyseal plate fully ossifies?

Longitudinal bone growth stops and no further lengthening is possible.

How do short, flat, irregular, and sesamoid bones grow?

They grow from their ossification centers through either endochondral or intramembranous ossification.

How do bones grow in width?

Through appositional growth, where osteoblasts in the periosteum secrete new circumferential lamellae.

Which three hormones significantly affect bone growth?

Growth hormone, testosterone, and estrogen.

Module 6.5: Bone Remodeling and Repair

What two processes make up bone remodeling?

Bone deposition by osteoblasts and bone resorption by osteoclasts.

How does mechanical tension affect bone remodeling?

Tension on a bone stimulates osteoblasts and bone deposition, while pressure stimulates osteoclasts and bone resorption.

Which hormones generally promote net bone deposition?

Vitamin D, testosterone, and estrogen.

Which dietary factors influence bone remodeling?

Adequate intake of calcium ions, vitamin D, vitamin C, vitamin K, and protein.

What does parathyroid hormone do in bone remodeling?

It stimulates osteoclasts, calcium absorption in the intestines, and calcium retention by the kidneys, raising blood calcium levels.

Chatgpt Explanation:

Parathyroid hormone (PTH) is like a bone manager for your body. Here’s a simple explanation:

PTH helps control calcium levels in your blood.

When blood calcium is low, PTH tells bone cells (osteoclasts) to break down some bone and release calcium into the blood.

It also helps your kidneys and intestines keep more calcium in the blood.

So basically: PTH makes sure your blood always has enough calcium by pulling it from bone when needed.

What are the two main classifications of bone fractures?

Simple (closed) -bone breaks, skin and tissue around fracture remain intact

compound (open) - bone breaks, skin and tissues around fracture are damaged

Compound = higher risk of infection because the bone is exposed to the environment.

How does bone fracture healing begin?

Chondroblasts secrete cartilage to form a soft callus.

What happens after the soft callus forms during bone healing?

Osteoblasts replace the soft callus with a bone callus made of primary bone.

Module 8.4: Function of Synovial Joints

How are synovial joints classified based on movement?

Nonaxial, uniaxial, biaxial, or multiaxial, depending on the number of axes of motion.

What is gliding motion in a synovial joint?

Simple back-and-forth or up-and-down motion between articulating surfaces.

Examples: Carpals in the wrist Tarsals in the ankle

What are angular movements in synovial joints?

Movements in which the angle between articulating bones changes, including flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, and circumduction.

Flexion

decreases the angle between bones (e.g., bending the elbow)

Extension

increases the angle between bones (e.g., straightening the elbow)

Hyperextension

extending beyond the normal straight position

Abduction

moving a limb away from the midline of the body (e.g., lifting arm sideways)

Adduction

moving a limb toward the midline of the body

Circumduction

moving a limb in a circular motion, like drawing a circle with your hand

What is rotation in a synovial joint?

A nonangular motion in which a bone pivots or twists along its long axis.

Examples: Turning your head side to side (neck)

What are special movements in synovial joints?

Movements including opposition and reposition, depression and elevation, protraction and retraction, inversion and eversion, dorsiflexion and plantarflexion, and supination and pronation.

Opposition

touching your thumb to your fingers on the same hand.

Reposition

moving your thumb back to its normal position.

Depression

lowering a body part

Example: dropping your shoulders down.

Elevation

raising a body part

Example: shrugging your shoulders.

Protraction

moving a body part forward. Example: jutting your jaw out.

Retraction

moving it back.

Example: pulling your jaw back in.

Inversion

a rotational motion of the foot in which the plantar surface (the sole of the foot) rotates medially toward the midline of the body

Ex. Half the foot goes up. Like the thumb side goes up, and the pinky stays down

Eversion

The plantar surface of the foot rotates laterally away from the midline of the body

Ex. Half the foot goes up. Like the pinky side goes up, and the thumb side stays down

Dorsiflexion

lifting the top of the foot upward toward your shin.

Plantarflexion

pointing the foot downward like standing on your tippy toes.

Supination

turning the palm upward or facing forward (anatomical position).

Pronation

turning the palm downward or facing back.

Module 8.5: Types of Synovial Joints

What are the types of synovial joints?

Plane, hinge, pivot, condylar, saddle, and ball-and-socket joints.

Plane joint

bones slide past each other, mostly flat surfaces.

Example: carpals (wrist bones), tarsals (ankle bones)

Hinge joint

allows bending and straightening like a door hinge.

Example: elbow, knee, fingers

Pivot joint

allows rotation around a single axis.

Example: neck (turning head side to side), forearm (turning palm up or down)

Condylar (ellipsoid) joint

allows movement in two planes (up/down and side/side) but no rotation.

Example: wrist (radius meets carpals), knuckles

Saddle joint

allows movement in two planes with more freedom than condylar.

Example: thumb base (carpometacarpal joint)

Ball-and-socket joints

allows movement in all directions including rotation.

Example: shoulder, hip

Which synovial joint is the least mobile?

Plane joints, which are nonaxial.

Which synovial joint is the most mobile?

Ball-and-socket joints, which are multiaxial.

Chapter 10: Muscle Tissue and Physiology

Module 10.1: Overview of Muscle Tissue

What are the three types of muscle tissue?

Skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle tissue.

What do all muscle cells convert chemical energy into, and how?

They convert chemical energy into mechanical energy by contracting to generate muscle tension.

What does all muscle tissue consist of?

Muscle cells and the surrounding extracellular matrix called the endomysium.

What common properties are shared by all types of muscle tissue?

Contractility: The ability of muscle cells to shorten and generate force.

Excitability: The ability to respond to stimulation, usually from nerves.

Conductivity: The ability to transmit electrical signals along the muscle cell.

Distensibility: The ability to stretch without being damaged.

Elasticity: The ability to return to original shape after being stretched or contracted.

What is the plasma membrane and cytoplasm of a muscle cell called?

The plasma membrane is the sarcolemma

the cytoplasm is the sarcoplasm.

What structures are found within the sarcoplasm?

Myofibrils and the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR).

What are Myofibrils

Long, threadlike structures inside the muscle fiber made of actin and myosin

They are responsible for muscle contraction.

Module 10.2: Structure and Function of Skeletal Muscle Fibers

What are skeletal muscle fibers composed of?

Very long, striated, multinucleated voluntary muscle cells.

What is Actin and Myosin?

Actin = a thin filament that forms the track along which myosin moves.

Myosin = a thick filament with heads that attach to actin and pull it inward using energy from ATP.

Together, they slide past each other (the sliding filament mechanism), causing the muscle to shorten and contract.

What are T-tubules and terminal cisternae?

T-tubules are inward extensions of the sarcolemma that surround myofibrils, and the calcium-storing sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) swells at these junctions to form terminal cisternae.

What are myofibrils made of?

Myofibrils are composed of myofilaments made up of contractile, regulatory, and structural proteins.

What are the three types of myofilaments and their main proteins?

Thick filaments – myosin (contractile protein)

Thin filaments – actin (contractile) with troponin and tropomyosin (regulatory)

Elastic filaments – titin (structural protein)

What causes the striations in skeletal muscle tissue?

The arrangement of myofilaments into repeating light and dark regions.

What is the importance of Z-Lines?

It marks the end of one sarcomere and the start of the next.

It anchors the actin (thin) filaments, keeping the structure organized during muscle contraction.

What are I bands and what do they contain?

Light regions of striations containing only thin filaments; each I band has a Z-disc in its middle.

shortens during contraction.

What are A bands and what do they contain?

Dark regions of striations where thick and thin filaments overlap; the central area is the H zone, bisected by the M line.

This region stays the same length during contraction, helping maintain the overall structure while filaments slide past each other.

What is the importance of H-zones?

It’s the area with only myosin (no overlap with actin).

The H-zone disappears during contraction as actin slides over myosin — a clear sign that the muscle is shortening.

What is the importance of M-line?

It’s the center of the sarcomere, holding the myosin filaments in place.

It helps stabilize the thick filaments during contraction so the sliding motion stays coordinated.

What is the sarcomere?

The functional unit of contraction, defined as the area between two Z-discs.

What is the sliding-filament mechanism?

The process in which thick and thin filaments slide past one another during muscle contraction.

Longer answer:

During muscle contraction, the thin filaments (actin) slide past the thick filaments (myosin) inside each sarcomere.

This causes the sarcomere to shorten, and when many sarcomeres shorten together, the whole muscle contracts.

Module 10.3: Skeletal Muscle Fibers as Electrically Excitable Cells

What is the electrical gradient or potential across the sarcolemma called?

The membrane potential—an electrical difference across the muscle cell’s plasma membrane.

What is the resting membrane potential of an unstimulated muscle fiber?

About -90 mV.

What are the two main types of ion channels in the sarcolemma?

Leak channels and gated channels.

Where are sodium and potassium ions most concentrated?

Sodium (Na⁺) concentration is higher in the extracellular fluid, while potassium (K⁺) concentration is higher in the cytosol.

What maintains the sodium and potassium ion gradient?

The sodium-potassium (Na⁺/K⁺) pump.

What causes the negative resting membrane potential?

The continuous loss of K⁺ through leak channels and the activity of Na⁺/K⁺ pumps.

What forces drive ion movement across the sarcolemma?

The concentration gradient and electrical gradient, together forming the electrochemical gradient.

What is an action potential?

A temporary, rapid reversal of the membrane potential consisting of:

Depolarization - Na⁺ enters, making the membrane positive

and

Repolarization - K⁺ exits the fiber, returning it to resting state

Module 10.4: The Process of Skeletal Muscle Contraction and Relaxation

Where does a motor neuron meet a skeletal muscle fiber?

At the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), which includes the axon terminal, synaptic cleft, and motor end plate.

What are the three main phases of skeletal muscle contraction?

Excitation, excitation-contraction coupling, and contraction.

What happens during the excitation phase?

The axon terminal releases acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft, which binds to receptors on the motor end plate and generates an end-plate potential.

What occurs during excitation-contraction coupling?

The end-plate potential triggers an action potential in the sarcolemma that travels down the T-tubules, opening calcium (Ca²⁺) channels in the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) and flooding the cytosol with Ca²⁺.

What happens when calcium ions enter the cytosol?

Ca²⁺ binds to troponin, allowing tropomyosin to move away from actin’s active sites so contraction can occur.

What occurs during the contraction phase?

ATP hydrolysis “cocks” the myosin head, enabling it to bind actin. The power stroke then pulls actin toward the sarcomere’s center.

What are the two main components of muscle relaxation?

1. ACh is broken down in the synaptic cleft.

2. Ca²⁺ levels in the cytosol return to resting levels.

Module 10.5: Energy Sources for Skeletal Muscle

What are the immediate energy sources for muscle fibers?

Stored ATP within the cytosol and creatine phosphate.

What is glycolytic catabolism?

An anaerobic process in which glucose in the cytosol is split to produce ATP.

What is oxidative catabolism?

An aerobic process in the mitochondria that oxidizes products of glycolysis, fatty acids, and amino acids to generate ATP.

Module 10.6: Muscle Tension at the Fiber Level

What is a single contraction-relaxation cycle of a muscle fiber called?

A twitch, which includes the latent period, contraction period, and relaxation period.

What determines the amount of tension generated by a muscle fiber?

The frequency of stimulation by the motor neuron and the resulting concentration of calcium ions (Ca²⁺).

What happens when a muscle fiber is stimulated before the relaxation period ends?

Unfused tetanus occurs.

What happens when a muscle fiber is stimulated 80–100 times per second before the relaxation period begins?

Fused tetanus occurs.

It is a good thing, like for weight lifting.

Fused tetanus is when a muscle fiber is stimulated so rapidly (about 80–100 times per second) that it doesn’t have time to relax between contractions.

As a result, the individual twitches blend into one smooth, sustained contraction with maximum tension.

It’s the strongest, steady state of contraction a muscle can produce.

What does the length-tension relationship describe?

Maximal tension is produced when the sarcomere is at its optimal length.

What are Type I and Type II muscle fibers?

Type I fibers are slow-twitch fibers that use primarily oxidative catabolism

Type II fibers are fast-twitch fibers that use primarily glycolytic catabolism.

Module 10.7: Muscle Tension at the Organ Level

What is a motor unit?

A single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates.

What is recruitment in muscle contraction?

The activation of additional motor units to produce more forceful contractions.

What is muscle tone?

Small, involuntary contractions of alternating motor units that maintain posture and readiness.

What are the three types of muscle contractions?

Isotonic concentric (shortening)

isotonic eccentric (lengthening)

isometric (no change in length).

Module 10.8: Skeletal Muscle Performance

What effects do endurance and resistance training have on skeletal muscle fibers?

They cause structural and biochemical changes that improve strength, size, and endurance.

What happens to muscle fibers during disuse?

They undergo atrophy, leading to a decrease in muscle size and strength.

What causes muscular fatigue?

Depletion of key metabolites, inadequate oxygen delivery, accumulation of waste metabolites, and environmental factors.

What is excess postexercise oxygen consumption (EPOC)?

The elevated oxygen intake after exercise that helps restore the body’s homeostasis.

Module 10.9: Smooth and Cardiac Muscle

What are the main functions of smooth muscle tissue?

Peristalsis, forming sphincters, and regulating the flow of material through hollow organs.

Peristalsis is the rhythmic, wave-like contraction of smooth muscle that moves substances—such as food, urine, or bile—through hollow organs like the intestines, ureters, and bile ducts.

What are the structural characteristics of smooth muscle cells?

Uninucleate, lack T-tubules, striations, and sarcomeres, and have a less extensive sarcoplasmic reticulum than skeletal muscle fibers.

What stimulates smooth muscle contraction?

The autonomic nervous system, stretch, hormones, and pacemaker cell activity.

How is smooth muscle contraction activated?

Calcium (Ca²⁺) binds to calmodulin, which activates myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK) to initiate crossbridge cycling.

How does smooth muscle relax?

By removing calcium (Ca²⁺) from the cytosol.

What are the 2 types of smooth muscle tissue?

Single-unit smooth muscle, which contracts as a single unit

multi-unit smooth muscle, which contracts independently.

How are cardiac muscle cells connected and coordinated?

They are joined by intercalated discs and coordinated electrically by pacemaker cells.
