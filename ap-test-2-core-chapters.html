Chapter 6

Module 6.1: Introduction to Bones as Organs

What are the main organs of the skeletal system?

Bones, joints, and other supporting structures.

What are the functions of the skeletal system?

Protection, mineral storage, maintenance of acid-base homeostasis, blood cell formation, fat storage, movement, and support.

What are the five different shapes of bones?

Long bones, short bones, flat bones, irregular bones, and sesamoid bones.

What are long bones and an example of one?

Long bones are longer than they are wide and function as levers for movement

an example is the femur/humerus

What are short bones and an example of one?

Short bones are roughly cube-shaped and provide stability with limited motion;

an example is the tarsal/carpal bones in the wrist.

What are flat bones and an example of one?

Flat bones are thin and broad, providing protection and large surfaces for muscle attachment

an example is the skull and sternum.

What are irregular bones and an example of one?

Irregular bones have complex shapes that do not fit other categories

an example is the vertebrae.

What are sesamoid bones and an example of one?

small, round bones embedded in tendons that reduce friction and modify pressure

an example is the patella.

What covers bones externally?

The periosteum, a membrane that sits on top of hard, outer compact bone.

What are the two types of bone tissue found within bones?

Compact bone (hard outer layer) and spongy bone (inner honeycomb-like layer).

What are the 2 main parts of a long bone?

A diaphysis (shaft) and two epiphyses (ends of the bone).

What is the structure of short, flat, irregular, and sesamoid bones?

Two thin layers of compact bone with spongy bone in the middle.

What is found in red bone marrow?

Hematopoietic tissue responsible for blood cell formation.

What is found in yellow bone marrow?

Blood vessel, triglycerides and adipocytes for fat storage.

Module 6.2: Microscopic Structure of Bone Tissue

What makes bone’s extracellular matrix (ECM) unique?

It contains both inorganic and organic components that provide strength and flexibility.

What is the inorganic matrix of bone made of?

It is primarily composed of calcium and phosphorus salts that form hydroxyapatite crystals.

What is the organic matrix of bone called and what does it contain?

It is called osteoid and consists of collagen fibers and other ECM components.

What are the three main types of bone cells?

Osteoblasts, osteocytes, and osteoclasts.

What is the function of osteoblasts?

They are responsible for bone deposition, forming new bone tissue.

What are osteocytes and how do they form?

They are mature osteoblasts that become trapped in the ECM they secreted, maintaining bone tissue.

What is the function of osteoclasts?

They are responsible for bone resorption, breaking down bone tissue.

What is compact bone primarily composed of?

Subunits called osteons, which are made up of concentric rings of bone ECM called lamellae.

What is the structure of spongy bone?

It has branching trabeculae that form a framework for bone marrow and is not composed of osteons.

Module 6.3: Bone Formation: Ossification

What is the process by which bones form called?

Ossification, or osteogenesis.

What is the first type of bone formed during ossification?

Primary bone, which is later replaced by stronger secondary bone.

What is intramembranous ossification?

A process of bone formation that occurs within a membrane composed of embryonic mesenchyme.

What type of bone forms first in intramembranous ossification?

Spongy bone forms first at the primary ossification center.

Which bones are formed by intramembranous ossification?

Many flat bones, including the skull and clavicles, during fetal development.

What happens after spongy bone forms in intramembranous ossification?

The periosteum differentiates, and then compact bone forms.

What is endochondral ossification?

A process of bone formation that occurs within a hyaline cartilage model.

Where does ossification begin in endochondral ossification?

It begins with early compact bone forming in the bone collar.

What forms after the bone collar in endochondral ossification?

The inner early spongy bone forms.

What bones formed by endochondral ossification

Most bones of the body, except the skull and clavicles.

Module 6.4: Bone Growth in Length and Width

Where does longitudinal bone growth occur?

At the epiphyseal plate in long bones, which has five zones of cells.

What happens when the epiphyseal plate fully ossifies?

Longitudinal bone growth stops and no further lengthening is possible.

How do short, flat, irregular, and sesamoid bones grow?

They grow from their ossification centers through either endochondral or intramembranous ossification.

How do bones grow in width?

Through appositional growth, where osteoblasts in the periosteum secrete new circumferential lamellae.

Which three hormones significantly affect bone growth?

Growth hormone, testosterone, and estrogen.

Module 6.5: Bone Remodeling and Repair

What two processes make up bone remodeling?

Bone deposition by osteoblasts and bone resorption by osteoclasts.

How does mechanical tension affect bone remodeling?

Tension on a bone stimulates osteoblasts and bone deposition, while pressure stimulates osteoclasts and bone resorption.

Which hormones generally promote net bone deposition?

Vitamin D, testosterone, and estrogen.

Which dietary factors influence bone remodeling?

Adequate intake of calcium ions, vitamin D, vitamin C, vitamin K, and protein.

What does parathyroid hormone do in bone remodeling?

It stimulates osteoclasts, calcium absorption in the intestines, and calcium retention by the kidneys, raising blood calcium levels.

What are the two main classifications of bone fractures?

Simple (closed) and compound (open).

How does bone fracture healing begin?

Chondroblasts secrete cartilage to form a soft callus.

What happens after the soft callus forms during bone healing?

Osteoblasts replace the soft callus with a bone callus made of primary bone.

Chapter 8: Articulations

Module 8.1: Overview of Joints

What are the functions of joints?

Enabling movement, providing stability, and allowing for the growth of long bones.

What are the functional classes of joints?

Immoveable (synarthrosis), partially moveable (amphiarthrosis), and freely moveable (diarthrosis).

What are the structural classes of joints?

Fibrous joints (dense regular collagenous connective tissue), cartilaginous joints (cartilage between bones), and synovial joints (fluid-filled joint cavity).

Module 8.2: Fibrous and Cartilaginous Joints

What are the three types of fibrous joints?

Sutures, gomphoses, and syndesmoses.

What are sutures and where are they found?

Synarthroses between the bones of the skull.

What are gomphoses and where are they found?

Synarthroses between each tooth and the alveolus in the mandible or maxilla.

What are syndesmoses and their functional classification?

Amphiarthroses in which the bones are joined by an interosseous membrane.

What are the two types of cartilaginous joints?

Synchondroses and symphyses.

What are synchondroses and their functional classification?

Synarthroses in which the bones are held together by hyaline cartilage.

What are symphyses and their functional classification?

Amphiarthroses in which the bones are connected by a fibrocartilaginous pad.

Module 8.3: Structure of Synovial Joints

What separates the bones in a synovial joint?

A joint cavity, also called a synovial cavity.

What holds the bones of a synovial joint together?

An articular capsule lined by a synovial membrane.

What covers the ends of bones in a synovial joint?

A layer of articular cartilage.

What is synovial fluid and its functions?

A fluid secreted by the synovial membrane that lubricates the joint, absorbs shock, and performs metabolic functions.

What structures provide additional support to synovial joints?

Ligaments, tendons, bursae, and tendon sheaths.

Module 8.4: Function of Synovial Joints

How are synovial joints classified based on movement?

Nonaxial, uniaxial, biaxial, or multiaxial, depending on the number of axes of motion.

What is gliding motion in a synovial joint?

Simple back-and-forth or up-and-down motion between articulating surfaces.

What are angular movements in synovial joints?

Movements in which the angle between articulating bones changes, including flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, and circumduction.

What is rotation in a synovial joint?

A nonangular motion in which a bone pivots or twists along its long axis.

What are special movements in synovial joints?

Movements including opposition and reposition, depression and elevation, protraction and retraction, inversion and eversion, dorsiflexion and plantarflexion, and supination and pronation.

Module 8.5: Types of Synovial Joints

What are the types of synovial joints?

Plane, hinge, pivot, condylar, saddle, and ball-and-socket joints.

Which synovial joint is the least mobile?

Plane joints, which are nonaxial.

Which synovial joint is the most mobile?

Ball-and-socket joints, which are multiaxial.

Module 8.6: Specific Synovial Joints

What type of joint is the elbow and how is it structured?

A hinge joint made up of two separate joints enclosed in a single articular capsule.

What type of joint is the knee and what is notable about it?

A hinge joint made up of two articulations; it is the largest diarthrosis in the body.

What is notable about the shoulder joint (glenohumeral joint)?

It is the most mobile and least stable joint in the body.

What type of joint is the hip and what bones form it?

A ball-and-socket joint formed by the head of the femur and the acetabulum.

Chapter 9: The muscular System

Module 9.1: Overview of Skeletal Muscles

What surrounds a muscle fiber and what surrounds a fascicle?

A muscle fiber is surrounded by endomysium, and a fascicle is a bundle of muscle fibers enclosed by perimysium.

What surrounds the whole muscle?

The epimysium wraps around many fascicles to form the whole muscle.

How does the pattern of fascicle arrangement affect a muscle?

It contributes to the muscle’s appearance and function, with orientations including parallel, convergent, pennate, circular, or spiral.

How are some muscles named?

By their shape, appearance, size, position, or structural features, such as the number of heads (e.g., triceps).

What is the action of a skeletal muscle?

The movement of bones at joints to which the muscle is attached.

What are other functions of skeletal muscle besides movement?

Facial expression, breathing, and generating heat to regulate body temperature.

How do muscles often work together?

In coordinated groups, with each muscle having a specific role.

What are the points of attachment of a muscle?

The origin (more stationary) and the insertion (more mobile).

How do bones and muscles function as lever systems?

They work together, with muscles applying force to move bones as levers.

What is a first-class lever?

A lever where the fulcrum is positioned between the point of force and the load to be moved.

What is a second-class lever?

A lever where the load is positioned between the force and the fulcrum.

What is a third-class lever?

A lever where the force is applied between the load to be moved and the fulcrum.
