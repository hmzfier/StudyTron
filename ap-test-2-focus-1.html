What is the process by which bones form called?

What is intramembranous ossification?

A process of bone formation that occurs within a membrane composed of embryonic mesenchyme.

Intramembranous ossification is another way bones form, but it’s different from endochondral ossification. Here’s a simple explanation:

Instead of starting as cartilage, some bones start as a soft layer of tissue called mesenchyme (this is like embryonic “connective tissue”).

This soft tissue turns directly into bone without first becoming cartilage.

This is how flat bones like your skull and clavicle (collarbone) form.

So in one sentence: Intramembranous ossification is when bone forms directly from a soft tissue membrane instead of from cartilage.

What type of bone forms first in intramembranous ossification?

Spongy bone forms first at the primary ossification center.

Which bones are formed by intramembranous ossification?

Many flat bones, including the skull and clavicles, during fetal development.

What happens after spongy bone forms in intramembranous ossification?

The periosteum differentiates, and then compact bone forms.

What is endochondral ossification?

A process of bone formation that occurs within a hyaline cartilage model.

Endochondral ossification is basically how most of your bones grow from cartilage. Here’s a simple way to think about it:

Imagine your bones start out as soft cartilage shapes (like squishy molds).

Over time, this cartilage gets replaced by hard bone.

This is how long bones like your femur (thigh bone) or humerus (arm bone) grow.

So, in one sentence: Endochondral ossification is the process where cartilage is slowly turned into bone as you grow.

Where does ossification begin in endochondral ossification?

It begins with early compact bone forming in the bone collar.

What bones formed by endochondral ossification

Most bones of the body, except the skull and clavicles.

What are the 5 zones of endochondral ossification

The five zones of endochondral ossification (in a growing long bone’s epiphyseal plate) in order from epiphysis to diaphysis are:

1. Zone of Resting (Reserve) Cartilage – small, inactive chondrocytes that anchor the epiphyseal plate to the epiphysis.

2. Zone of Proliferation – chondrocytes divide rapidly, forming columns of stacked cells.

3. Zone of Hypertrophy – chondrocytes enlarge and mature.

4. Zone of Calcification – the cartilage matrix calcifies, chondrocytes begin to die.

5. Zone of Ossification – osteoblasts invade, lay down bone matrix, forming new bone.

This sequence is essential for longitudinal bone growth.

What does parathyroid hormone do in bone remodeling?

It stimulates osteoclasts, calcium absorption in the intestines, and calcium retention by the kidneys, raising blood calcium levels.

Chatgpt Explanation:

Parathyroid hormone (PTH) is like a bone manager for your body. Here’s a simple explanation:

PTH helps control calcium levels in your blood.

When blood calcium is low, PTH tells bone cells (osteoclasts) to break down some bone and release calcium into the blood.

It also helps your kidneys and intestines keep more calcium in the blood.

So basically: PTH makes sure your blood always has enough calcium by pulling it from bone when needed.

Intramembranous Ossification Steps (6)

Bone develops directly from mesenchymal (embryonic connective tissue) cells — no cartilage model first.

1. Mesenchymal cells cluster → form an ossification center.

2. Cells become osteoblasts → start secreting bone matrix (osteoid).

3.Osteoid calcifies → calcium is deposited, and the tissue hardens.

4. Osteoblasts become osteocytes → trapped in small spaces called lacunae.

5. Bone grows outward → forming trabeculae (spongy bone).

6. Periosteum forms on the outside → and compact bone develops around the spongy bone.

Endochondral Ossification Steps (8)

Bone forms by replacing a cartilage model (specifically hyaline cartilage).

1. Cartilage model forms from mesenchyme.

2. Cartilage grows in size as the model for the future bone.

3. Cartilage cells (chondrocytes) die in the center → space forms for bone to grow.

4. Blood vessels enter → bringing osteoblasts that start forming bone (primary ossification center in the diaphysis).

5. Bone tissue replaces cartilage in the shaft.

6. Secondary ossification centers form in the epiphyses (bone ends).

7. Epiphyseal plate (growth plate) remains between shaft and ends → allows the bone to grow in length until adulthood.

8. Epiphyseal plate ossifies → growth stops.

Module 10.2: Structure and Function of Skeletal Muscle Fibers

What is Actin and Myosin?

Actin = a thin filament that forms the track along which myosin moves.

Myosin = a thick filament with heads that attach to actin and pull it inward using energy from ATP.

Together, they slide past each other (the sliding filament mechanism), causing the muscle to shorten and contract.

What are T-tubules and terminal cisternae?

T-tubules are inward extensions of the sarcolemma that surround myofibrils, and the calcium-storing sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) swells at these junctions to form terminal cisternae.

What are the three types of myofilaments and their main proteins?

Thick filaments – myosin (contractile protein)

Thin filaments – actin (contractile) with troponin and tropomyosin (regulatory)

Elastic filaments – titin (structural protein)

What is the importance of Z-disc?

Line bisecting the I band.

Both the thin and elastic filaments anchor to the z-discs, which also attach myofibrils to one another

It anchors the actin (thin) filaments, keeping the structure organized during muscle contraction.

What are I bands and what do they contain?

Light regions of striations containing only thin filaments; each I band has a Z-disc in its middle.

shortens during contraction.

What are A bands and what do they contain?

The dark band that contains both thick and thin filaments

This region stays the same length during contraction, helping maintain the overall structure while filaments slide past each other.

What is the importance of H-zones?

The middle portion of the A band in which only thick filaments are found

KNOW THAT IT IS IN THE A BAND. HA! acronym

The H-zone disappears during contraction as actin slides over myosin — a clear sign that the muscle is shortening.

What is the importance of M-line?

Middle line of the A band. Contains structural proteins that hold thick filament in place

It helps stabilize the thick filaments during contraction so the sliding motion stays coordinated.

What is the sarcomere?

The functional unit of contraction, defined as the area between two Z-discs.

What is the sliding-filament mechanism?

The process in which thick and thin filaments slide past one another during muscle contraction.

Longer answer:

During muscle contraction, the thin filaments (actin) slide past the thick filaments (myosin) inside each sarcomere.

This causes the sarcomere to shorten, and when many sarcomeres shorten together, the whole muscle contracts.

Module 10.3: Skeletal Muscle Fibers as Electrically Excitable Cells

What is the electrical gradient across the sarcolemma called?

The membrane potential

Explanation:

The inside of the cell is slightly negative compared to the outside, which is positive.

This difference in charge is created by ions (like sodium, potassium, and chloride) moving in and out through channels in the membrane (the sarcolemma in muscle cells).

Simply way to remember it:

Membrane potential = tiny battery in the cell membrane.

It stores electrical energy that’s used to start muscle contraction

In short:

The membrane potential is the electrical difference across the sarcolemma that allows a muscle cell to become excited and contract.

What is the resting membrane potential of an unstimulated muscle fiber?

About -90 mV.

What are the two main types of ion channels in the sarcolemma?

1. Leak channels = always open

Function = maintain resting membrane potential

2. Gated channels = opens in response to stimulus

Enable action potentials and muscle contraction

Where are sodium and potassium ions most concentrated?

Sodium (Na⁺) concentration is higher in the extracellular fluid, while potassium (K⁺) concentration is higher in the cytosol.

What maintains the sodium and potassium ion gradient?

The sodium-potassium pump.

What causes the negative resting membrane potential?

The continuous loss of potassium through leak channels and the activity of sodium potassium pumps.

What forces drive ion movement across the sarcolemma?

1. Concentration Gradient

Ions naturally move from areas of high concentration → low concentration (like diffusion).

Example:

There’s more sodium outside the cell than inside → sodium tends to move into the cell.

There’s more potassium inside the cell → potassium tends to move out of the cell.

2. Electrical Gradient

Because ions are charged, they’re also influenced by electric charge differences across the membrane.

The inside of a resting muscle cell is negatively charged compared to the outside.

So:

Positive ions (sodium, calcium) are pulled into the negatively charged inside.

Negative ions (like Cl⁻) are pushed out.

What is an action potential?

An action potential is a brief electrical signal that travels along the membrane of a nerve or muscle cell — it’s how cells communicate and trigger responses, like muscle contraction.

A temporary, rapid reversal of the membrane potential consisting of 3 things:

1. Depolarization - Sodium enters, making the membrane positive

2. Repolarization - Potassium exits the fiber, returning it to resting state

3. Return to rest - The sodium–potassium pump restores the original ion balance

Module 10.4: The Process of Skeletal Muscle Contraction and Relaxation

Where does a motor neuron meet a skeletal muscle fiber?

At the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), which includes the axon terminal, synaptic cleft, and motor end plate.

Short summary of muscle contraction

1. Excitation - Nerve signal releases ACh, starting an action potential in the muscle.

2. Excitation-contraction coupling - Action potential travels through T-tubules, causing the sarcoplasmic reticulumto release calcium.

3. Preparation for contraction - Calcium binds to troponin, moving tropomyosin to expose actin sites.

4. Contraction - Myosin heads binds to actin and pulls to the center of the Sarcomere. Cycle repeats leading to muscle contraction.

5. Relaxation - Calcium returns to the sarcoplasmic reticulum, ACh breaks down, and the muscle relaxes.

Long answer of what happens when you decide to move. Example: dumbbel curl

Here’s the full process of what happens when you do a dumbbell curl — from brain signal to muscle movement:

1. Brain sends the signal

Your brain’s motor cortex decides to lift the dumbbell and sends an electrical signal down your spinal cord and through motor neurons to your biceps brachii.

2. EXCITATION - Nerve reaches the muscle (neuromuscular junction)

At the neuromuscular junction, the nerve ending releases a neurotransmitter called acetylcholine (ACh) into the synaptic cleft.

3. EXCITATION - Action potential in the muscle fiber

ACh binds to receptors on the sarcolemma (muscle cell membrane), causing sodium ions (Na⁺) to rush in.

This changes the electrical charge, generating an action potential that travels along the sarcolemma and down the T-tubules.

4. EXCITATION-CONTRACTION-COUPLING - Calcium release

The action potential triggers the sarcoplasmic reticulum (a calcium-storing structure) to release calcium ions (Ca²⁺) into the sarcoplasm (muscle cytoplasm).

5. Preparation for contraction -Binding of calcium to troponin

Calcium binds to troponin, which is attached to tropomyosin on the actin filaments.

This moves tropomyosin out of the way, exposing the myosin-binding sites on actin.

6. Contraction -Cross-bridge formation

Myosin heads (on the thick filaments) attach to the exposed sites on actin (thin filaments), forming cross-bridges.

7. Contraction -Power stroke

Each myosin head pivots using energy from ATP, pulling the actin filament toward the center of the sarcomere — this shortens the muscle fiber.

→ This is what causes your biceps to contract and lift the dumbbell upward.

8. Detachment and resetting

A new ATP molecule binds to the myosin head, causing it to detach from actin.

The ATP is then broken down into ADP + Pi, re-cocking the myosin head for another power stroke if calcium is still present.

9. Relaxation

When you lower the dumbbell:

The brain stops sending the signal.

ACh is broken down by acetylcholinesterase.

Calcium is pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum.

Tropomyosin covers the binding sites again.

The muscle fiber relaxes and returns to its resting length.

In summary:

Brain → Nerve → ACh release → Action potential → Calcium release → Actin–myosin interaction → Contraction → Relaxation.

What is a cross bridge and what is the cross-bridge cycle

A cross bridge is the connection (link) that forms between a myosin head and an actin filament during muscle contraction.

It’s called a bridge because it literally connects the thick and thin filaments inside the muscle cell.

Cross-bridge cycle

1. Myosin head binds to actin → cross bridge forms.

2. Myosin pulls on actin (the power stroke) → actin slides toward the center of the sarcomere → muscle shortens.

3. ATP binds to myosin → the cross bridge breaks (myosin detaches from actin).

4. Myosin resets (ATP is used to “cock” the head back) → ready to form another bridge.

This repeats many times — like tiny rowing motions — to produce contraction.

Easy way to remember:

Cross bridge = Myosin grabs actin and pulls.

What are the two main components of muscle relaxation?

1. ACh is broken down in the synaptic cleft.

2. Calcium levels in the cytosol return to resting levels.

Module 10.5: Energy Sources for Skeletal Muscle

What are the immediate energy sources for muscle fibers?

Stored ATP within the cytosol and creatine phosphate.

What is glycolytic catabolism?

An anaerobic process in which glucose in the cytosol is split to produce ATP.

What is oxidative catabolism?

An aerobic process in the mitochondria that oxidizes products of glycolysis, fatty acids, and amino acids to generate ATP.

Module 10.6: Muscle Tension at the Fiber Level

What is a single contraction-relaxation cycle of a muscle fiber called?

A twitch, which includes the latent period, contraction period, and relaxation period.

What determines the amount of tension generated by a muscle fiber?

The frequency of stimulation by the motor neuron and the resulting concentration of calcium ions.

What happens when a muscle fiber is stimulated before the relaxation period ends?

Unfused tetanus occurs.

Unfused tetanus is normal and healthy — it’s how your body produces smooth, controlled muscle contractions during regular activity.

If you get no relaxation, this can cause muscle fatigue or cramping.

Explanation:

What happens when a muscle fiber is stimulated before it fully relaxes?

If a second stimulus (nerve impulse) arrives before the muscle fiber has completely relaxed from the previous contraction, the new contraction starts on top of the previous one.

This causes the tension (force) in the muscle to increase — because calcium hasn’t been fully taken back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum yet, and the muscle is still partially contracted.

Unfused Tetanus Definition:

Unfused tetanus is when a muscle is stimulated repeatedly at a rate that is fast enough to produce partial summation of contractions, but slow enough that the muscle still relaxes slightly between stimuli.

What happens when a muscle fiber is stimulated 80–100 times per second before the relaxation period begins?

Fused tetanus occurs.

It is a good thing, like for weight lifting.

Fused tetanus is when a muscle fiber is stimulated so rapidly (about 80–100 times per second) that it doesn’t have time to relax between contractions.

As a result, the individual twitches blend into one smooth, sustained contraction with maximum tension.

It’s the strongest, steady state of contraction a muscle can produce.

During weight lifting:

When you lift a heavy weight, your brain sends rapid, continuous nerve impulses to your muscle fibers.

These signals come so fast that the fibers don’t get a chance to relax between them.

That produces a fused (complete) tetanus — a smooth, sustained, maximal contraction.

This allows you to generate maximum force to lift or hold the weight.

So yes — fused tetanus is what lets you push, pull, or lift with full strength.

What does the length-tension relationship describe?

Maximal tension is produced when the sarcomere is at its optimal length.

What are Type I and Type II muscle fibers?

Type I fibers are slow-twitch fibers that use primarily oxidative catabolism

Type II fibers are fast-twitch fibers that use primarily glycolytic catabolism.

Module 10.7: Muscle Tension at the Organ Level

What is a motor unit?

A single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it controls.

What is recruitment in muscle contraction?

The activation of additional motor units to produce more forceful contractions.

What is muscle tone?

Small, involuntary contractions of alternating motor units that maintain posture and readiness.

What are the three types of muscle contractions?

Isotonic concentric (shortening)

isotonic eccentric (lengthening)

isometric (no change in length).

Module 10.8: Skeletal Muscle Performance

What effects do endurance and resistance training have on skeletal muscle fibers?

They cause structural and biochemical changes that improve strength, size, and endurance.

What happens to muscle fibers during disuse?

They undergo atrophy, leading to a decrease in muscle size and strength.

What causes muscular fatigue?

Depletion of key metabolites, inadequate oxygen delivery, accumulation of waste metabolites, and environmental factors.

What is excess postexercise oxygen consumption (EPOC)?

The elevated oxygen intake after exercise that helps restore the body’s homeostasis.

Module 10.9: Smooth and Cardiac Muscle

What are the main functions of smooth muscle tissue?

Peristalsis, forming sphincters, and regulating the flow of material through hollow organs.

Peristalsis is the rhythmic, wave-like contraction of smooth muscle that moves substances—such as food, urine, or bile—through hollow organs like the intestines, ureters, and bile ducts.

What are the structural characteristics of smooth muscle cells?

Uninucleate, lack T-tubules, striations, and sarcomeres, and have a less extensive sarcoplasmic reticulum than skeletal muscle fibers.

What stimulates smooth muscle contraction?

The autonomic nervous system, stretch, hormones, and pacemaker cell activity.

How is smooth muscle contraction activated?

Calcium binds to calmodulin, which activates myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK) to initiate crossbridge cycling.

How does smooth muscle relax?

By removing calcium from the cytosol

What are the 2 types of smooth muscle tissue?

Single-unit smooth muscle, which contracts as a single unit

multi-unit smooth muscle, which contracts independently.

How are cardiac muscle cells joined and coordinated?

They are joined by intercalated discs and coordinated electrically by pacemaker cells.
