6. the motor areas of the cerebral cortex

1.Primary Motor Cortex (precentral gyrus)

involved in conscious planning (voluntary) of movements.

2.Premotor Cortex

involved in planning, guidance, coordination and execution of movement

3. Frontal eye fields

involved in back and forth eye movements (as in reading)

7. the Cerebral cortex’s 2 main somatosensory areas

1.Primary Somatosensory Cortex (postcentral gyrus)

interprets incoming somatic sensory stimuli: touch, pressure, pain, temperature, proprioception.

2.Somatosensory Association Area

Integrates incoming somatic sensory stimuli (recognizing objects by touch, understanding shape/texture).

8. where the special senses are located in the cerebral cortex

• Vision – primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe (calcarine sulcus).

• Hearing – primary auditory cortex in the temporal lobe (superior temporal gyrus).

• Taste – primary gustatory cortex in the insula and frontal operculum.

• Smell (olfaction) – primary olfactory cortex in the medial temporal lobe (piriform cortex).

• Balance (vestibular sense) – vestibular cortex in the insula and parietal lobe.

10. the basal nuclei location and function. Also how it prevents spontaneous, inappropriate muscle contractions (discussed later in the chap in 12.8)

Location:

Deep inside the cerebrum, buried within the cerebral hemispheres.

They surround and flank the thalamus and include structures such as the caudate nucleus, putamen, and globus pallidus.

Function:

• Regulate movement

• Start and stop voluntary movements smoothly

• Help with motor planning and coordination

• Suppress unwanted or inappropriate movements

• Influence posture and muscle tone

• Contribute to habit formation and certain cognitive functions

How they prevent spontaneous, inappropriate muscle contractions:

The basal nuclei inhibit motor neurons at rest.

They do this by sending constant inhibitory signals (mainly GABA) to motor pathways.

This steady inhibition stops random or unintended muscle activity.

11. the 3 types of white matter fibers and their functions

Association fibers – restricted to single hemisphere; connect gray matter of cortical gyri with one another

Commissural fibers – connect the two hemispheres (e.g., corpus callosum).

Projection fibers – connect cerebral cortex of one hemisphere with other areas of same hemisphere, other parts of brain, and spinal cord

13. For the diencephalon, know its location, the function of the thalamus and hypothalamus and pineal gland and subthalamus

Location of diencephalon: Deep in the brain, between the cerebrum and brainstem.

Thalamus: Main relay station for sensory information going to the cortex.

Hypothalamus: perform several vital functions critical to survival (regulation of autonomic

nervous system, sleep/wake cycle, thirst and hunger, and body temperature)

Pineal gland: Produces melatonin and regulates sleep–wake cycles.

Subthalamus - functionally connected with basal nuclei; together control movement

14. the location and function of the cerebellum

Location: At the back of the brain, inferior to the cerebrum and posterior to the brainstem.

Function: Coordinates balance, posture, and smooth, precise voluntary movements.

16. the location and function of the reticular formation

Location: A network of over 100 neurons running through the brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla).

Function:

▪Function in sleep, pain transmission, and mood

▪Serve motor functions for both skeletal muscles and autonomic nervous system

▪Involved in homeostasis of breathing and blood pressure

▪Play role in sensation and alertness and activity levels of cerebral cortex

17. how the endocrine and nervous system work together to help the body maintain homeostasis

The nervous system sends fast, short-term signals (nerve impulses), and the endocrine system sends slow, long-lasting signals (hormones).

They work together by detecting changes (nervous system) and correcting them through hormone release (endocrine system), keeping the body in homeostasis.

20. the 2 main types of memory + classification based on duration of storage

Types of memory:

• Declarative (facts/events)

• Non-declarative (skills/habits)

By duration:

Immediate memory –critical for carrying out normal conversation, reading, and daily tasks

Short-term (working) memory – allows you toremember and manipulate info with general behavioral goal in mind

Long-term memory – more permanent form of storage for days, weeks, or even lifetime
