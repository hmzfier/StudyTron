9. how we classify sensory receptors: forms, location, and stimulus that triggers depolarization.

Encapsulated nerve endings – surrounded by specialized supportive cells

▪ Like mechanoreceptors

Free nerve endings – lack supportive cells

▪ Like hair follicle on thin skin; temperature

Sensory receptors can be sorted by location of stimuli they detect:

1. Exteroceptors –detect stimuli originating from outside body

2. Interoceptors –detect stimuli originating from within body itself

Mechanoreceptors – encapsulated interoceptors or exteroceptors

▪ Found in musculoskeletal system, skin, and other organs

▪ Mechanically gated ion channels:depolarize when mechanically deformed

▪ Allow for sensory transduction from vibration, light touch, stretch, and pressure

Thermoreceptors – exteroceptors

▪ Depolarize in response to temperature changes (separate receptors detect hot and cold)

▪▪ Slowly adapting receptors

Chemoreceptors – can be either interoceptors or exteroceptors

▪ Depolarize in response to binding to specific chemicals (in body fluids or in air)

Photoreceptors – special sensory exteroceptors found only in eye

▪ Depolarize in response to light

Nociceptors – usually slowly adapting exteroceptors

▪ Depolarize in response to noxious stimuli

10. classes of mechanoreceptors, thermoreceptors, nociceptors

MECHANORECEPTORS

Lamellated corpuscles (Pacinian corpuscles) – layered onion-shaped appearance; rapidly adapting receptors deep within dermis; detect high-frequency vibratory and deep pressure stimuli

Hair follicle receptors – free nerve endings at base of hair follicles in thin skin

• Respond to stimuli that cause hair to bend

Proprioceptors – in musculoskeletal system

• Detect movement and position of joint or body part

THERMORECEPTORS

Usually small knobs at end of free nerve endings in skin

“Cold” receptors – respond to temperatures between 50–104°F; in superficial

Dermis

“Hot” receptors – respond to temperatures between 90–118°F; deep in dermis

• Temperatures outside these ranges are detected by nociceptors

• • Reason extremes of temperature are interpreted as pain

11. what sensory nerve receptive fields and 2-points discriminations are

Receptive field: the area of skin that one sensory neuron monitors.

Two-point discrimination: how close two touches can be and still be felt as two separate points; smaller fields = better discrimination.

12. Describe dermatomes and referred pain

Dermatomes: skin regions supplied by a single spinal nerve.

Referred pain: pain felt in a different area than the actual source because visceral and somatic nerves share spinal pathways.

Powerpoint info on referred pain:

Referred pain – pain that originates in an organ is perceived as cutaneous pain

• Visceral sensations travel along same pathways as do somatic sensations

• Referred pain is generally located along dermatome for particular nerve

14. the 2 mechanoreceptors within muscles and tendons and how they function

Muscle spindles: detect muscle stretch; cause reflex contraction to prevent overstretching.

Golgi tendon organs: detect tendon tension; cause reflex relaxation to prevent excessive force.

2. Be able to compare the Somatic and Autonomic Nervous Systems

Somatic motor division

• Directly innervates their target

• Neurons innervate skeletal muscle

• Leads to voluntary muscle contractions

Autonomic motor division

• Does not directly innervate their target and require two-neuron circuit

• Neurons innervate smooth muscle cells, cardiac muscle cells, and glands

• Produce involuntary actions

4. the Sympathetic Neurotransmitters and Receptors (you don’t need to know the receptors at the level of alpha1 and Beta1...just cholinergic and adrenergic)

Acetylcholine (ACh) –used in synapses between sympathetic preganglionic and postganglionic neurons

• postganglionic axons then transmit action potentials to target cell

• Neurotransmitters bind to specific receptors of target cells

1. Adrenergic receptors (α or β) bind to epinephrine & norepinephrine

2. Cholinergic receptors (Nicotinic & Muscarinic) bind to Acetylcholine

• Muscarinic receptors – on sweat glands in skin

• Nicotinic receptors – in membranes of all postganglionic neurons within sympathetic ganglia and adrenal medullae

5. the effects of the Sympathetic Nervous System on the target cells

Smooth muscle cells around the pupil: Dilation of the pupil.

Cells of salivary glands: Increase in secretion in certain cells.

Cardiac muscle cells: Increase in heart rate and force of contraction.

Smooth muscle cells of bronchi: Dilation of bronchioles (bronchodilation).

Cells of sweat glands: Increase in secretion.

Smooth muscle cells of blood vessels to skin: Constriction of blood vessels (vasoconstriction).

Smooth muscle cells of blood vessels to skeletal muscles: Dilation of blood vessels (vasodilation).

Smooth muscle cells of blood vessels to digestive and urinary organs: Vasoconstriction.

Smooth muscle cells of digestive and urinary tracts and sphincters: Relaxation of digestive and urinary tracts, contraction of sphincters.

Cells of digestive glands: Decrease in secretion.

Cells of pancreas and liver: Increase in release of glucose.

Cells of adrenal medulla: Release of epinephrine and norepinephrine.

6. the relationship between the Adrenal Medulla and the Sympathetic Nervous System.

1. it prolongs the duration of the sympathetic nervous system’s effects.

2. The epinephrine and norepinephrine in the blood can reach cells that are not innervated by sympathetic neurons

3.The adrenal medulla acts as a “backup” for the sympathetic nervous system—even if the pathways connecting this system with many of its target organs are disrupted, it can still affect these organs indirectly via the adrenal medulla

7. the anatomy of the Parasympathetic Nervous System and how it’s organized.

craniosacral division - cranial and sacral

Parasympathetic cranial nerves

associated with oculomotor (CN III), facial (CN VII), glossopharyngeal (CN IX), and vagus (CN X) nerves

Vagus nerves – main parasympathetic nerves that innervate most thoracic and abdominal viscera; 90% of parasympathetic output

Contribute to cardiac, pulmonary, and esophageal plexuses

Parasympathetic sacral nerves

innervate last segment of large intestine, urinary bladder, and reproductive organs.

Sacral nerve branches form pelvic splanchnic nerves; then form plexuses in pelvic floor

9. the effects of the Parasympathetic Nervous System on the target cells

Smooth muscle cells around pupil and lens: constriction of pupil, adjustment of lens for near vision.

Cells of lacrimal and salivary glands: increase in secretions.

Cardiac muscle cells: decrease in heart rate.

Smooth muscle cells of bronchioles: bronchoconstriction.

Smooth muscle cells of digestive tract and sphincters: contraction of digestive tract smooth muscle, relaxation of sphincters.

Cells of digestive glands: increase in secretions.

Smooth muscle cells of urinary sphincters: relaxation of sphincters.

11. what autonomic tone is.

Autonomic tone refers to fact that neither division is ever completely shut down

Sympathetic tone dominates in blood vessels; keeps them partially constricted

Parasympathetic tone dominates in heart; keeps heart rate at ~72 beats per minute
