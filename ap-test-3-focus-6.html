13. Function and location of the Reticular Formation

Location

• Runs through the brainstem

• Spans the midbrain, pons, and medulla

• Forms a network of neurons scattered throughout the brainstem’s central core

Function

• Maintains arousal + consciousness

• Regulates sleep–wake cycle

• Filters sensory input (decides what reaches your awareness)

• Helps with muscle tone + posture

• Controls basic autonomic functions (heart rate, breathing, blood pressure)

2. olfaction anatomy

Basic Gist: Nerve > Bulb > Tract

Olfactory nerve (CN I) – combined axons of olfactory neurons

Olfactory bulb – sits above cribriform plate of ethmoid bone; below frontal lobe of brain

Mitral cells (neurons in olfactory bulb) axons form bulk of olfactory tract

Olfactory tract – axons that leave olfactory bulb and go to CNS for interpretation

3. the path from the olfactory nerve to olfactory bulb to olfactory tract to primary olfactory cortex

Short summary

Neuron > Bulb > Mitral cells > Tract > Primary olfactory cortex

Long Explanation

1. Olfactory neurons send signals to the olfactory bulb

2. Where they synapse with mitral cells. Mitral cell axons form the olfactory tract

3. which carries the signal to the primary olfactory cortex for odor awareness. From there, the limbic system (amygdala, hippocampus, hypothalamus) adds emotion and memory to the smell.

6. the anatomy of the taste bud

Taste buds – usually located on lateral surfaces of papillae

Contain three cell types:

1. Gustatory (taste) cells

▪ specialized epithelial cells with microvilli

▪ project into opening on papilla surface (taste pore)

▪ Have receptors that detect different tastes

▪ Carry taste stimuli to CNS via CN VII, CN IX, CN X

2. Basal cells

stem cells differentiate into new gustatory cells live 10–14 days

3. Supporting cells

▪ physically support gustatory cells

▪ no role in taste sensation

Classified by shape:

1. Vallate (circumvallate) papillae – largest, dome-shaped

▪ Contain 100s of taste buds

2. Fungiform papillae – mushroom-shaped

▪ Contain few taste buds

3. Foliate papillae – ridges on sides of tongue

▪ Only contain taste buds in childhood

4. Filiform papillae – long, thin cylinders scattered across tongue

▪ Devoid of taste buds

▪ Contain sensory nerve endings to detect food texture and temperature

12. the 3 tissue layers of the eyeball and their basic functions

Fibrous layer – outer layer; protection and shapes the eye. Includes cornea and sclera

Vascular layer – middle layer; supplies blood and controls light entry. Includes choroid, ciliary body, iris, pupil, suspension ligaments.

Neural layer – inner layer; contains the retina that detects light and sends signals to the brain.

13. how the pupil dilates and constricts

Pupillary sphincter muscle – in iris

Contracts during parasympathetic stimulation

Reduces size of pupil

Restricts amount of light entering eyeball

Pupillary dilator muscle –in iris

Contracts during sympathetic activation

Allows pupil to increase in size

More light enters eyeball

16. how light is focused on the retina

Clear vision requires that light rays are focused on retina (not in front or behind it)

• This is a function of lens and cornea

• Most of eye’s refractive power occurs as light passes through cornea: refractive index similar to water

• Lens provides for fine tuning and refractive adjustment

Distant objects: Emmetropic state – eye is relaxed and focusing on distant objects

• Lens is in normal flattened shape

• Parallel light rays are minimally refracted by cornea and focused on retina

Close objects: Light rays from objects closer to eye are more scattered

• need more refraction (greater than cornea can provide)

• lens becomes more thickened (accommodation)

• refracts light more than flattened lens so more light rays are focused on retina

17. how the lens changes shape

Ciliary body surrounding lens relaxes when viewing distant objects

• Flattens lens, reducing its refractive capability

Ciliary body contracts when viewing nearby object; allows for accommodation

• Thickens lens, increasing refractive power

Myopia (nearsightedness) – distance between cornea and lens is too great or cornea is too curved

Results in blurred vision at all distances
