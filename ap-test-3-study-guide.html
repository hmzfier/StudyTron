11. different ion channels of the neuron plasma membrane

Leak channels: always open; allow continuous ion flow to maintain resting membrane potential.

Ligand-gated channels: open when a chemical (neurotransmitter) binds; create local potentials.

Voltage-gated channels: open/close in response to membrane voltage changes; generate action potentials.

Mechanically-gated channels: open when the membrane is physically deformed (pressure, stretch).

15.5 Steps of an action potential

1. A local potential depolarizes the axolemma of the trigger zone to threshold

2. Voltage gated sodium channels activate, sodium enter, and the axon section depolarizes

3. Sodium channels inactivate and voltage-gated potassium channels activate, and repolarization begins.

4. Sodium channels return to the resting state and repolarization continues

5. the axolemma may hyperpolarize before potassium channels return to the resting state; after this, the axolemma returns to the resting membrane potential

15.6 Why does an action potential only move forward?

An action potential only moves forward because the previous section of the axon is in the refractory period:

Absolute refractory period: Na⁺ channels are inactivated, so no new AP can fire there.

This prevents the AP from traveling backward and ensures unidirectional propagation.

16. Compare and contrast saltatory and continuous conduction

Continuous conduction: AP moves along every part of an unmyelinated axon; slower.

• in unmyelinated axons

• Every section of axolemma from trigger zone to axon terminal must propagate action potential

• Slows conduction speed as each successive section of axon must depolarize

Saltatory conduction: AP jumps from node to node on a myelinated axon; much faster and more energy-efficient.

• In myelinated axons where insulating properties of myelin sheath increase efficiency and

speed of signal conduction

• Action potentials only depolarize nodes of Ranvier; “jumps” from node to node

18. Compare and contrast electrical vs. chemical synapses

Electrical synapses: direct ion flow through gap junctions; very fast; bidirectional; rare in humans.

•Axolemmas of each cell in synapse are nearly touching

• Gap junctions align channels forming pores that ions or other small substances can flow through

▪ In areas of brain responsible for programmed, automatic behaviors (breathing)

▪ In cardiac and visceral smooth muscle to allow for coordinated muscle activity

• Transmission is bidirectional and nearly instantaneous

Chemical synapses: use neurotransmitters to signal across a synaptic cleft; slower; unidirectional; most common in humans.

• Make up majority of synapses in nervous system

• More efficient than electrical synapses

• No signal strength is lost (as at electrical synapses)

• Electrical signal becomes weaker as it dissipates into ECF

• Convert electrical signals into chemical signals

20. the postsynaptic potentials (EPSP vs. IPSP)

EPSP (excitatory postsynaptic potential): depolarizes the postsynaptic membrane, bringing it closer to threshold.

Ion channels open and positive charges enter the postsynaptic neuron, causing an EPSP

IPSP (inhibitory postsynaptic potential): hyperpolarizes the postsynaptic membrane, moving it further from threshold.

Ion channels open and the postsynaptic neuron gains negative charges or loses positive charges, causing an IPSP

21. neural integration

the process of combining all excitatory and inhibitory inputs a neuron receives to determine whether it will fire an action potential.

22. summation

the addition of multiple postsynaptic potentials (EPSPs and IPSPs) to influence the likelihood of an action potential.

EPSPs > IPSPs = depolarization

IPSPs > EPSPs = hyperpolarization

23. Compare and contrast temporal vs. spatial summation

Temporal summation: multiple signals from the same neuron arrive in rapid succession; effects add up over time.

Spatial summation: signals from multiple neurons arrive at the same time; effects add up across space.

24. the 3 ways synaptic transmission is terminated

Reuptake – neurotransmitters are taken back into the presynaptic neuron.

Enzymatic degradation – enzymes break down neurotransmitters in the synaptic cleft.

Diffusion – neurotransmitters drift away from the synaptic cleft and are returned to the presynaptic neuron

27. what neuronal circuits are

Short Answer: Neuronal circuits are networks of interconnected neurons that transmit and process signals to produce responses. They can be simple, like reflexes, or complex, like those for learning and memory, and involve sensory neurons, interneurons, and motor neurons.

Long Answer:

Neuronal circuits are networks of interconnected neurons that work together to process specific types of information and generate responses. Essentially, they are the pathways through which signals travel in the nervous system to perform functions like sensing, thinking, moving, or regulating bodily processes.

Key points:

Components: Neurons (sensory, motor, interneurons), synapses, and sometimes supporting glial cells.

Function: Transmit and integrate electrical and chemical signals.

Types:

Simple circuits: Reflex arcs (e.g., knee-jerk reflex).

Complex circuits: Those in the brain for learning, memory, and higher-order processing.

Direction of flow: Signals usually move from sensory neurons → interneurons → motor neurons, but some circuits can be recurrent or feedback loops.

In short, neuronal circuits are the “wiring” of the nervous system that determines how information is processed and how the body responds.

29. Difference between somatic and visceral

Somatic

•Relates to voluntary control and skeletal muscles.

•Also includes sensory input from skin, muscles, and joints (like touch, pain, temperature, and proprioception).

•Example: Moving your arm or sensing pressure on your skin.

Visceral

•Relates to involuntary control and internal organs (smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands).

•Also includes sensory input from internal organs (like stretch, chemical changes, or pain).

•Example: Heart rate regulation, digestion, or sensing stomach fullness.

In short: somatic = body wall & voluntary, visceral = internal organs & involuntary.

10. the basal nuclei location and function. Also how it prevents spontaneous, inappropriate muscle contractions (discussed later in the chap in 12.8)

Location:

Deep inside the cerebrum, buried within the cerebral hemispheres.

They surround and flank the thalamus and include structures such as the caudate nucleus, putamen, and globus pallidus.

Function:

• Regulate movement

• Start and stop voluntary movements smoothly

• Help with motor planning and coordination

• Suppress unwanted or inappropriate movements

• Influence posture and muscle tone

• Contribute to habit formation and certain cognitive functions

How they prevent spontaneous, inappropriate muscle contractions:

The basal nuclei inhibit motor neurons at rest.

They do this by sending constant inhibitory signals (mainly GABA) to motor pathways.

This steady inhibition stops random or unintended muscle activity.

Substances that can pass through blood-brain barrier are: water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and nonpolar, lipid-based molecules

Protein channels or carriers allow for passage of other essential molecules across blood-brain barrier are: glucose, amino acids, and ions

Most large, polar molecules CAN’T cross blood-brain barrier

Barrier is protective, but can hinder access of medications into brain

25. the 2 primary roles of the spinal cord

Relay station

The spinal cord acts as a relay station and an intermediate point between the body and the brain; in fact, it’s the only means by which the brain can communicate with most of the body below the head and neck. It receives outgoing stimuli from the brain and sends them to the rest of the body; it also receives incoming stimuli from the body and sends them to the brain.

Processing station

Although the spinal cord cannot perform the higher functions characteristic of the brain, it does do some integration and processing. Indeed, certain activities called spinal reflexes can be carried out by the spinal cord alone, without influence from the brain

8. sensory transduction

Mechanical stimulus is converted into electrical signal

Easy to remember steps:

1. Resting: Ion channels closed (no stimulus yet).

2. Stimulus hits: Pressure opens mechanically-gated SODIUM channels → SODIUM enters → generates receptor potential

3. Threshold reached: Enough SODIUM enters → voltage-gated SODIUM channels open → action potential travels to spinal cord.

Steps

1. Before any stimulus arrives, the ion channels in the axolemma in the somatic sensory neuron is closed.

2. When a stimulus such as pressure is applied, mechanically gated sodium channels open and sodium enter the axoplasm, generating a receptor potential.

3. If enough sodium enter the membrane potential reaches threshold, voltage-gated sodium channels open. This triggers an action potential, which will be propagated along the axon to the spinal cord.

31. The anatomy of the maculae and how they detect head position and movement and linear acceleration

maculae = found in utricle and saccule

Maculae Anatomy easy version:

• Located in: Utricle + Saccule (vestibule of inner ear)

• Hair cells: Have stereocilia + one kinocilium

• Gel layer: Hair cells are embedded in a gelatinous membrane

• Otoliths: Tiny calcium carbonate crystals sitting on top of the gel (add weight)

Maculae Anatomy:

Found in utricle and saccule

Macula – contains receptor cells for head position and movement

Each hair cell displays stereocilia and one true cilium (kinocilium)

Kinocilium and stereocilia are embedded in gelatinous mass (otolithic membrane); suspended in endolymph

Otoliths – calcium carbonate crystals in otolithic membrane that increase the membrane density

Gel layer topped with otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals)

HOW THEY DETECT HEAD POSITION & LINEAR ACCELERATION - Easy Version

1. Head tilts / body accelerates

2. Otoliths shift due to gravity or straight-line movement

3. Otolith movement pulls the gel layer

4. Gel bends the stereocilia on hair cells

5. Hair cell bending changes action potentials sent through the vestibular branch of CN VIII

6. Brain interprets this as head position and linear acceleration (not rotation).

How They Detect Head Position & Linear Acceleration:

When the head tilts or moves in a straight line, otoliths shift

This bends the stereocilia of hair cells

Bending changes how much the hair cells fire

Brain interprets this as head position + linear acceleration

Hair cells release neurotransmitter glutamate onto dendrites of bipolar neurons that form vestibular division of CN VIII

Stereocilia in utricle are vertically oriented when head is upright and stationary

▪bend in response to tilting head side to side or front to back

Stereocilia in saccule are horizontally oriented when head is upright and stationary

▪bend in response to up and down movements of head

32. the anatomy of the semicircular ducts and how they function to detect rotation of the head

Semicircular Duct Anatomy:

3 ducts: anterior, posterior, lateral

Each duct is connected to utricle at the ampulla

Inside each ampulla is the crista ampullaris (hair cells)

Hair cells’ stereocilia are embedded in a gel structure called the cupula

How They Detect Head Rotation:

When the head rotates, endolymph lags behind and pushes the cupula

Cupula bends the hair cell stereocilia

Bending changes hair cell firing → brain detects rotational movement
