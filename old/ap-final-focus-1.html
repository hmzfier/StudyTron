the 11 organ systems and their general functions

Integumentary System = Protects body from external Environment = Hair, Skin, Nails

Skeletal System = Supports the body, protects the organs = Bones, Joins

Muscular = Movement, posture, generates heat = Skeletal Muscles

Nervous = Regulates body functions, voluntary and involuntary movement = Brain, Spinal cord

Endocrine = Secretes hormones to regulate many body processes = Glands (lots of glands)

Cardiovascular system = Delivers oxygen, transports cells and nutrients = Heart, Blood Vessels, Blood

Lymphatic (immune) System = Returns excess fluid into the blood stream, provides immunity = Lymph nodes, Lymphatic vessels

Respiratory = REGULATES pH. Delivers oxygen to blood, removes CO2 = Lungs, trachea, nasal cavity

Digestive = Digests food, removes waste = Intestines, liver, pancreas

Urinary System = REGULATES pH. Removes metabolic waste, maintains fluid balance = Kidney, bladder, uretha

Reproductive = Produces eggs, sex hormones = Penis, Vagina

the body cavities (anterior, posterior)

Posterior (Dorsal) Cavities:

Cranial cavity – brain

Vertebral (spinal) cavity – spinal cord

Subdivisions are continuous; filled with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF); bathes and protects brain and spinal cord

Anterior (Ventral) Cavities:

1. Thoracic cavity – lungs + heart

• Pleural cavities – each surround either left or right lung

• Mediastinum – between pleural cavities; houses heart, great vessels, trachea (windpipe), and esophagus; not within serous membrane

• Pericardial cavity – within mediastinum; within serous membrane that surrounds heart

2. Abdominopelvic cavity

• Abdominal cavity – diaphragm to bony pelvis - digestive organs

• Pelvic cavity – within bony pelvis - bladder, reproductive organs

the planes of section

1. Sagittal plane - divides into right and left sections

Midsagittal plane - divides into equal left and right sections

Parasagittal plane - divides into unequal right and left sections

2. Frontal plane/ Coronal plane - divides into anterior and posterior sections

3. Transverse plane/ Horizontal/ Cross section –divides into superior and inferior sections

Body Cavities Region

From right to left

Top: Right hypochondriac region, epigastric region, left hypochondriac region

Middle: Right lumbar region, umbilical region, left lumbar region

Bottom: Right iliac region, hypogastric region, left iliac region

Serous membranes and their order

Thin sheets of tissue; form cavities in ventral cavity; surround heart, lungs, and many abdominal organs

Single, continuous layer of tissue; folds over itself to create a double-layered structure:

Visceral layer – in contact with underlying organ

Serous fluid - within cavity between two membrane layers; extremely thin layer of fluid

Parietal layer – outermost layer attached to surrounding structures

Layer order from outer to inner: Parietal > Serious Fluid > Visceral layer

the 4 core principles

Structure → Function

The shape of a structure determines what it can do.

Homeostasis

The body maintains stable internal conditions.

Gradients

Substances move from high → low (pressure, concentration, temperature).

Cell–Cell Communication

Cells use chemical or electrical signals to coordinate body functions.

atoms and atomic structures including subatomic particles and electron shells

Atom – smallest unit of matter that retains original properties

Subatomic particles:

Protons = Positive

Electron = Negative

Neutrons = No charge

Electron shells – regions surrounding atomic nucleus where

electrons exist; each holds certain number of electrons:

1st shell (closest to nucleus) – 2 electrons

2nd shell – 8 electrons

3rd shell – 18 electrons; “satisfied” with 8

how to read the periodic table

Atomic number – number of protons in atomic nucleus

Note Atomic number = same number of electrons in the atom.

Mass number – equal to sum of all protons and neutrons in atomic nucleus

Isotope – atom with same atomic number (number of protons) but different mass number (number of neutrons)

Radioisotopes – unstable isotopes; high energy or radiation released by radioactive decay; allows isotope to assume more stable form

chemical bonds (ionic and covalent)

Molecule – formed by chemical bonding between two or more atoms of same element

Compound – formed when two or more atoms from different elements combine by chemical bonding

Ionic Bond

Compound contains both metal and nonmetal

electrons are transferred from metal atom to nonmetal atom; results in formation of ions: cations and anions

Cation – positively charged ion; metal loses one or more electrons

Anion – negatively charged ion; nonmetal gains one or more electrons

Attraction between opposite charges bonds ions to one another forming salt

Covalent Bond

strongest bond

two or more nonmetals share electrons

Can be nonpolar (equal sharing) or polar (unequal sharing)

Memory trick:

Ionic = I give / I take

Covalent = Co-own electrons

valence shells, octet rule and duet rule

Valence Shell:

Outer electron shell of an atom

Valence electrons = electrons in that outer shell

These determine reactivity and bonding

Octet Rule:

Atoms are most stable with 8 electrons in the valence shell

Drives atoms to gain, lose, or share electrons to reach 8

Duet Rule:

atoms with 5 or fewer electrons

They are stable with 2 electrons in their valence shell (first shell only holds 2)
