where and how melanin is produced

Produced by melanocytes in stratum basale

Composed of two molecules of amino acid tyrosine; chemically bonded by series of reactions catalyzed by enzyme tyrosinase; occurs in stepwise fashion within special vesicle (melanosome)

Melanin synthesis increases with exposure to natural or artificial UV radiation; leads to

tanning (darkening of skin pigmentation); has both immediate and delayed effects on

skin pigmentation:

• Immediate response – oxidation of melanin already present in keratinocytes; causes melanin to quickly darken

• Delayed or secondary effects – UV light causes DNA damage in melanocytes; stimulates melanin production; appears within 72 hours; lasts longer than melanin oxidation

the functions of melanin

Primary function – protecting keratinocyte DNA from mutations induced by UV radiation

Secondary function of melanin: reduces synthesis of vitamin D in response to UV radiation; leads to less calcium ion absorption and maintenance of calcium ion homeostasis within narrow range:

• Individuals living in regions exposed to high amounts of UV radiation (Africa) developed darker skin to prevent excess vitamin D production

• People in areas with less UV radiation (northern Europe) developed lighter skin so they could synthesize enough vitamin D

how human skin tone can range in a variety of tones

Amount of melanin produced → more melanin = darker skin

Spectrum of human skin tones is due to differences in amount of tyrosinase activity and type (color) of melanin produced

Type of melanin

Eumelanin = brown/black

Pheomelanin = red/yellow

Activity of melanocytes → some people’s melanocytes are more active

UV exposure → increases melanin production (tanning)

Genetics → inherited genes control melanin levels and distribution

Brown/black or yellow/red types

the basic structure of hair and its functions

Functions

Provides protection by preventing substances and organisms from entering eyes and nose

Protects underlying skin of scalp from UV radiation and mechanical trauma

Associated with small sensory neuron; plays role in detecting changes in environment

Structure

Composed of two main parts, both made of stratified squamous keratinized epithelial cells in various stages of development

Shaft

• Portion of hair projecting from skin’s surface

• Made of columns of dead keratinized epithelial cells that have completed keratinization process

Root

• Segment of hair embedded in dermis; surrounded by small sensory neuron

• Indented at base by projection of blood vessels from dermis (hair papilla)

• Root and hair papilla collectively known as hair bulb

• Many epithelial cells in root are still alive; have not completed keratinization process

• Root is embedded in hair follicle; infolding of epidermis (epithelial root sheath) extends deep into dermis or even hypodermis

Strand of hair has three visible regions in transverse section:

Inner medulla – soft core only found in thick hair (head); composed of soft keratin

Middle cortex – highly structured and organized with several layers of keratinocytes containing hard keratin; provides strength

Outermost cuticle – single layer of overlapping keratinocytes containing hard keratin; provides mechanical strength

Dermal root sheath – surrounds epithelial root; consists of connective tissue; supports follicle and keeps it

separate from dermis

Arrector pili muscles – small bands of smooth muscle; attach to dermal root sheath on one end and dermal papillary layer on other

Contraction of arrector pili causes hair to stand up (piloerection); gives skin dimpled appearance (“goosebumps”)

the basic structure of nails and its functions

Nails – hard accessory structures at ends of digits; composed of stratified squamous epithelium filled with hard keratin

Structure

Nail plate - visible hard keratin divided into: Nail Body and Nail Root

Nail body – visible portion of nail plate

Folded regions of skin surround and reinforce nail plate:

Proximal nail fold – on proximal edge covering nail root; distal edge of fold is eponychium (cuticle); consists of only stratum corneum

Medial and lateral nail folds – on medial and lateral edges of nail plate respectively

Distal or free edge of nail plate – attached to underlying nail bed by accumulation of stratum corneum called hyponychium

Nail growth occurs at nail matrix

Lunula - Nails do not contain melanocytes; mostly translucent except at lunula; half-moon shaped region of proximal nail plate; represents accumulation of keratin

Functions

Protection of fingertips and toes

Support for grasping small objects

Enhanced touch by providing counter-pressure

the different sweat and sebaceous glands: and a. how they secrete substances and b. where they are located and c. their basic functions

Sweat (sudoriferous) glands – produce sweat

Sebaceous glands – produce oily sebum

Different types: Eccrine, Apocrine, and Sebaceous

1. Eccrine (Merocrine) Sweat Glands - location, functions, how they are secreted

a. Secretion method

Leaves duct via sweat pore

Use merocrine secretion (release sweat by exocytosis)

b. Location

In the dermis

Most on palms, soles, forehead

c. Basic functions

Cool the body (thermoregulation)

Apocrine Sweat Glands - location, functions, how they are secreted

a. Secretion method

Release sweat into hair follicle

Thicker secretion enters hair follicles

b. Location

Armpits, groin, around nipples

in the axillae, anal region, and areolae

c. Basic functions

Produce thicker sweat that bacteria break down (body odor)

Activate at puberty

Secretion influenced by sex hormones

Involved in emotional sweating

Sebaceous Glands - location, functions, how they are secreted

a. Secretion method

Acini cells secrete hair oil (“sebum”)

Use holocrine secretion (whole cells burst to release oil)

Secrete onto hair follicles

b. Location

Found on face and scalp

c. Basic functions

Provide hydrophobic barrier (keep moist and deters bacteria)

Produce sebum (oil)

Lubricate skin and hair
