the processes of the 2 types of ossification

Intramembranous Ossification and Endochondral Ossification

Intramembranous Ossification

FOR FLAT BONES ONLY - SUCH AS THE SKULL, CLAVICLE

Definition: bone forms directly from mesenchyme (connective tissue)

Simple definition: Intramembranous ossification is the process your body uses to make certain bones directly from a sheet of connective tissue

Forms many flat bones skull and clavicles during fetal development

Forms primary bone (which will become secondary bone)

Steps:

1. Mesenchymal cells in primary ossification center → become osteoblasts

2. Osteoblasts secrete organic matrix → calcifies → Osteoblasts trapped → become osteocytes

3. Osteoblasts lay down Trabeculae → becomes spongy bone → some messenchyme turns into periosteum

4. Osteoblasts in the periosteum lay down early compact bone

Location: flat bones (skull, clavicle)

Endochondral Ossification

Endochondral ossification is for: Long bones (femur, tibia, humerus), Most bones of the skeleton, All bones below the skull except clavicles, Bones that start as a hyaline cartilage model

Definition: bone forms by replacing hyaline cartilage

Simple Explanation: Endochondral ossification is how most of your bones—especially long bones like your arms and legs—are made, and it’s a bit like building a bone “on top of a cartilage template.”

Steps Simple:

1. Chondroblasts in perichondrium become osteoblasts

2. Osteoblasts build Bone collar forms on outside; internal cartilage calcifies and chondrocytes die

3. Primary ossification center: osteoblasts replace calcified cartilage with spongy bone; secondary ossification centers and medullary cavity develop

4. Medullary cavity enlarges > remaining cartilage replaced by bone > epiphyses finish ossifying

Steps from book:

1. The chondroblasts in the perichondrium differentiate into osteoblasts

2a. Osteoblasts build the bone collar on the bone's external surface as the bone begins to ossify from the outside

2b. Simultaneously, the internal cartilage begins to calcify and chondrocytes die

3. In the primary ossification center, osteoblasts replace the calcified cartilage with early spongy bone; the secondary ossification centers and medullary cavity develop

4. As the medullary cavity enlarges, the remaining cartilage is replaced by bone, the epiphysis finish ossifying.

Location: most long bones (femur, humerus)

the cycle of bone remodeling including new bone deposition and old bone resorption

Continuous process of bone formation and loss after growth in length is finished

Bone deposition = bone creation

Bone deposition by osteoblasts

1. Osteoblasts make organic matrix (osteoid)

Found in periosteum and endosteum

Secrete proteoglycans and glycoproteins that bind calcium ions

2. Osteoblasts secrete vesicles containing calcium ions, ATP, and enzymes

Vesicles bind to collagen fibers

3. Calcification begins

Calcium ions crystallize, rupturing vesicles → bone hardens

Resorption by Osteoclasts

1. Osteoclasts secrete hydrogen ions on bone ECM

• Hydroxyapatite crystals in inorganic matrix are pH-sensitive and break down

• Calcium ions and other liberated minerals can be reused elsewhere in body

2. Osteoclasts secrete enzymes

•Degrade organic matrix (proteoglycans, glycosaminoglycans, and glycoproteins)

•Then, they reused by osteoclasts

the role of parathyroid hormone and Calcitonin in bone remodeling

Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) → Stimulates osteoclasts to break down bone → Increased bone resorption

Calcitonin → Decreases osteoclasts → Strengthens bone.

know what fontanels are

Fontanels → Soft spots on a baby’s skull where the bones haven’t yet fused.

Function → Allow the skull to compress during birth and the brain to grow after birth.

Types → Anterior (largest), posterior, sphenoidal, and mastoid.

Close → Usually fuse within 18–24 months (anterior) or earlier (others).
