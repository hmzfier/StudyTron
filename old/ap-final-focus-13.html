the common structural features of the vertebrae

Body (Centrum):

Primary weight-bearing part, located anteriorly

Separated by intervertebral discs, which absorb shock from activities like running and jumping

Vertebral Foramen:

Posterior to the body

Large opening that houses the spinal cord and associated tissues

Vertebral Arch: Formed by 2 pedicles (lateral borders of foramen) and 2 laminae (posterior borders)

Articular Processes (Superior & Inferior):

At junctions of pedicles and laminae

Facets form joints with adjacent vertebrae

Transverse Processes:

Project laterally from vertebral arch

Serve as muscle attachment sites

Spinous Process:

Projects posteriorly from vertebral arch

Provides additional sites for muscle attachment

Compare and contrast the cervical, thoracic and lumbar vertebrae

Cervical Vertebrae (C1–C7):

Size: Smallest, lightest

Body: Small and oval

Vertebral foramen: Large, triangular

Spinous process: Often bifid (split)

Transverse processes: Contain transverse foramina for vertebral arteries

Dens (odontoid process) – prominent superior tooth-shaped projection protrudes

from body

UNIQUE

Transverse foramina are only found in cervical vertebrae (C1–C7) and they allow passage of the vertebral arteries, veins, and sympathetic nerves.

Function: Supports head, allows greatest range of motion

Thoracic Vertebrae (T1–T12):

Size: Medium

Body: Heart-shaped

Vertebral foramen: Circular, smaller than cervical

Spinous process: Long, points downward

Transverse processes: Articulate with ribs (costal facets)

UNIQUE

Superior costal facets and inferior costal facets, on lateral sides of vertebral body

Lumbar Vertebrae (L1–L5):

Size: Largest and heaviest of all vertebrae, thick bodies for weight-bearing

Body: Kidney-shaped

Vertebral foramen: Triangular, smaller than cervical

Spinous process: Short, thick, horizontal

Transverse processes: Long, no transverse foramina or rib facets

Function: Supports lower back, allows flexion/extension

the functions of joints

Enable movement – link between bones allow movement when muscles and

tendons exert necessary force across joint

Provide stability – some joints allow limited or no movement; very stable; critical

for structures such as skull; protect underlying structures

Allow long bones to lengthen – epiphyseal plate is where long bones

grow in length during skeletal development; temporary joint

the functional classification of joints by motion

1. Synarthroses: Immovable joints (e.g., sutures of the skull)

2. Amphiarthroses: Slightly movable joints (e.g., intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis)

3. Diarthroses: Freely movable joints (e.g., shoulder, hip)

Structural Classification of joints based on Tissue and Structure

1. Fibrous Joints: Bones joined by dense connective tissue; no joint cavity. NOTE: no joint space

Examples: sutures, syndesmoses, gomphoses

2. Cartilaginous Joints: Bones joined by cartilage; no joint cavity or minimal movement. NO JOINT SPACE

Examples: synchondroses (hyaline cartilage), symphyses (fibrocartilage)

3. Synovial Joints: Bones separated by a fluid-filled joint cavity; highly movable

Have layer of hyaline cartilage on articulating surface of each bone;

Features: articular cartilage, joint cavity, synovial fluid, joint capsule

Examples: hinge (elbow), ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip), pivot, saddle, condylar, plane

the 3 fibrous joints: what they are composed of, where to find them and motility classification

1. Sutures

immoveable joint between edges of bones;

make up cranium; fully fused sutures are very stable, well suited for protecting brain

2. Syndesmoses

joint between tibia, fibula, ulna, and radius

bones joined by interosseous membrane or ligament composed of dense regular collagenous connective tissue

allows for small amount of movement (amphiarthroses)

3. Gomphoses

immoveable joint (synarthrosis) between each tooth and bony socket in jaw

periodontal ligament is strong fibrous membrane; links tooth firmly to jaw bone

the 2 cartilaginous joints: what they are composed of, where to find them and motility classification

1. Synchondrosis – bones linked together by hyaline cartilage; synarthroses

Epiphyseal plates – hyaline cartilage

▪connects diaphyses and epiphyses of developing long bones; replaced with bone during maturation

First sternocostal joint and costochondral joints are synchondroses

▪stabilize rib cage; persist into adulthood

2. Symphysis – bones united by fibrocartilaginous pad or plug; functionally amphiarthroses

the structural elements of synovial joints and their function

1. Joint cavity (synovial cavity) – space between articulating bones

2. Articular capsule: Has two layers:

1. Outer fibrous layer – holds the bones together

2. Inner synovial membrane – lines the inside (except over cartilage) and produces synovial fluid

3. Synovial fluid – thick liquid with 3 main functions

Lubrication – reduces friction between articulating surfaces

Transportation – moves nutrients and waste products in absence of blood vessels within joint

Shock absorption – helps to evenly distribute stress and force during movement

4. Articular Cartilage:

Covers the ends of bones, reduces friction and absorbs shock

the stabilizing and supporting factors of synovial joints

1. Ligaments:

Connect bone to bone

Strengthen and stabilize the joint

2. Tendons (associated, not part of joint proper):

Connect muscle to bone

Assist in joint movement and stability

3. Muscle tone – continuous amount muscle contraction = stabilizes

4. Bursae (associated structures)

Fluid-filled sacs between bones, tendons, or muscles

Reduce friction during movement

the functional classes of synovial joints

1. Nonaxial joints – allow motion in 1+ planes without moving around axis

Examples: Intercarpal joints, intertarsal joints

2. Uniaxial joints – allow motion around only 1 axis

Examples: elbow

3. Biaxial joints – allow motion around 2 axes

Examples: metacarpophalangeal joints - axis 1: flexion/extension, axis 2: move fingers fanned out or in (abduction/adduction)

4. Multiaxial (triaxial) joints – allow motion around 3 axes

Examples: shoulder, hip
