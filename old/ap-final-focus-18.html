what a synapse is and the 3 places that it can occur

Synapse: a junction where a neuron communicates with another cell.

Three locations:

Axodendritic: axon → dendrite

Axosomatic: axon → soma (cell body)

Axoaxonic: axon → axon

Compare and contrast electrical vs. chemical synapses

Electrical synapses: direct ion flow through gap junctions; very fast; bidirectional; rare in humans.

•Axolemmas of each cell in synapse are nearly touching

• Gap junctions align channels forming pores that ions or other small substances can flow through

▪ In areas of brain responsible for programmed, automatic behaviors (breathing)

▪ In cardiac and visceral smooth muscle to allow for coordinated muscle activity

• Transmission is bidirectional and nearly instantaneous

Chemical synapses: use neurotransmitters to signal across a synaptic cleft; slower; unidirectional; most common in humans.

• Make up majority of synapses in nervous system

• More efficient than electrical synapses

• No signal strength is lost (as at electrical synapses)

• Electrical signal becomes weaker as it dissipates into ECF

• Convert electrical signals into chemical signals

the steps of chemical synapse

Chemical synapse steps:

1.An action potential in the presynaptic neuron triggers calcium channels in the axon terminal to open

2. Influx of calcium causes synaptic vesicles to release neurotransmitters in the synaptic cleft

3. neurotransmitters bind to receptors in the postsynaptic neuron

4. Ion channels open, leading to a local potential and possibly an action potential

Chemical synapse steps simplified by chatgpt:

1. AP arrives → Ca²⁺ channels open.

2. Calcium triggers neurotransmitter release.

3. Neurotransmitters bind postsynaptic receptors.

4. Ion channels open → local potential, possibly triggering an AP.

the functions of central nervous system (CNS)

1. Sensory functions – detection of sensations inside and outside of body by peripheral nervous system (PNS)

2. Integrative functions – decision-making processes; exclusive function of Central Nervous system (CNS)

▪Includes wide variety of functions:

▪▪Interpretation of sensory information

▪▪Planning and monitoring movement

▪▪Maintenance of homeostasis

▪▪Higher mental functions such as language and learning

3. Motor functions – stimulation of muscle cell contraction or gland secretion by PNS

what the white and gray matter in the brain and spinal cord are made of and where they are located in both organs.

Brain:

Gray matter: made of neuron cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated axons; located in outer cerebrum (cerebral cortex)

White matter: made of myelinated axons; located beneath the gray matter in the brain (connects different brain regions).

Spinal cord:

Gray matter: located centrally in an H- or butterfly-shaped region; contains neuronal cell bodies and interneurons.

White matter: located peripherally around the gray matter; contains myelinated axons.

the location and function of the 5 lobes of the cerebrum

1.Frontal lobe

Location: Central sulcus and precentral gyrus

Function: Responsible for planning and executing movement and complex mental functions such as behavior, conscience, and personality

2.Parietal lobe

Location: top/back of the cerebrum

Function: Responsible for processing and integrating sensory information and function in attention

3.Temporal lobe

Location: sides of the brain near the temples

Function: Involved in hearing, language, memory, and emotions

4.Occipital lobe

Location: back of the brain

Function: Neurons process all information related to vision

5.Insula (Insular lobe)

Location: deep inside the lateral sulcus, under the temporal and frontal lobes

Function: neurons are currently thought to be involved in functions related to taste and viscera (internal organs)

the 3 areas of the neocortex and their functions.

1.Sensory areas

Receive and process sensory information

Examples: primary visual cortex, primary auditory cortex, primary somatosensory cortex

2.Motor areas

Control voluntary movement

Example: primary motor cortex (initiates movement), premotor cortex (plans movement)

3.Association areas

Integrate information, enable thinking, memory, language, problem-solving

Make up most of the cortex and connect sensory + motor functions

the motor areas of the cerebral cortex

1.Primary Motor Cortex (precentral gyrus)

involved in conscious planning (voluntary) of movements.

2.Premotor Cortex

involved in planning, guidance, coordination and execution of movement

3. Frontal eye fields

involved in back and forth eye movements (as in reading)

the Cerebral cortex’s 2 main somatosensory areas

1.Primary Somatosensory Cortex (postcentral gyrus)

interprets incoming somatic sensory stimuli: touch, pressure, pain, temperature, proprioception.

2.Somatosensory Association Area

Integrates incoming somatic sensory stimuli (recognizing objects by touch, understanding shape/texture).

where the special senses are located in the cerebral cortex

• Vision – primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe (calcarine sulcus).

• Hearing – primary auditory cortex in the temporal lobe (superior temporal gyrus).

• Taste – primary gustatory cortex in the insula and frontal operculum.

• Smell (olfaction) – primary olfactory cortex in the medial temporal lobe (piriform cortex)

• Balance (vestibular sense) – vestibular cortex in the insula and parietal lobe.
