Be able to describe a reflex arc and its components

programmed, automatic responses to stimuli; occur in reflex arc (3-step sequence of events)

Steps

1. Sensory (afferent) division - PNS detects and delivers stimulus to CNS

2. Central Nervous System - CNS integrates stimulus

3. Motor (efferent) division - PNS delivers motor response from CNS to effectors

Shortened steps:

receptor → spinal cord → effector.

the 2 mechanoreceptors within muscles and tendons and how they function

Muscle spindles: detect muscle stretch; cause reflex contraction to prevent overstretching.

Golgi tendon organs: detect tendon tension; cause reflex relaxation to prevent excessive force.

how reflexes are classified

1.Number of synapses between neurons involved in arc

• Simplest reflex arcs (monosynaptic reflexes)one synapse between sensory and motor neuron

• More complicated (polysynaptic reflexes) involve multiple synapses

2. Type of organ in which reflex takes place

• visceral (internal organs)

• somatic (skin, skeletal muscle, glands)

Be able to describe the spinal nerve reflexes that we discussed: a simple stretch reflex, golgi tendon reflex and a flexion-extension reflex

Simple stretch reflex:

1. Muscle stretches

2. Muscle spindle fires an action potential to the spinal cord

3. Spinal cord activates α-motor neuron and trigger a action potential

4. The a-motor neurons stimulate the muscle to contract, and it returns to the optimal length.

Golgi tendon reflex

Causes muscle relaxation

1. too much tension in tendon

2. Golgi tendon organ fires

3. spinal cord

4. muscle relaxes - the agonist muscle relaxes and the antagonist muscles contract, protecting the muscle and tendon from potentially damaging forces.

Flexion-extension reflex (withdrawal + crossed extension)

When you touch a very hot object or step on broken glass, you almost immediately pull your hand or foot away from the source of the pain. These are examples of the flexion, or withdrawal, reflex.

1. When stimulated, nociceptive afferents transmit the painful stimulus to the spinal cord

2a. a-motor neurons stimulate muscles that flex the thumb receiving the painful stimulus

2b. a-motor neurons stimulate muscles that extend the opposite limb to maintain balance

Simplified:

1. painful stimulus

2. pull limb away (flexion) while opposite limb extends to support body.

Be able to compare the Somatic and Autonomic Nervous Systems

Somatic motor division

• Directly innervates their target

• Neurons innervate skeletal muscle

• Leads to voluntary muscle contractions

Autonomic motor division

• Does not directly innervate their target and require two-neuron circuit

• Neurons innervate smooth muscle cells, cardiac muscle cells, and glands

• Produce involuntary actions

the anatomy of the Sympathetic nervous system and how it’s organized.

Sympathetic nervous system

• preganglionic axons are usually short

• postganglionic axons are usually long

Thoracolumbar = Thoracic and lumbar spinal cord

Preganglionic neurons

• originate in lateral horns of thoracic and lumbar spinal cord

• exit with axons of lower motor neurons via anterior root

• Preganglionic axons separate from spinal nerve anterior ramus to form white rami communicans

• Then, leads to postganglionic cell bodies in sympathetic chain ganglion

!IMPORTANT

Sympathetic chain ganglia where most postganglionic cell bodies are found

• Chainlike appearance has given them the name Sympathetic chain ganglia. These ganglia extend

beyond the thoracic and lumbar spinal cord, from the superior cervical ganglion down to the inferior sacral ganglion.

NEURON LOCATIONS IMPORTANT!!!

Preganglionic cell bodies location: Thoracolumbar region (T1–L2) of the spinal cord

Postganglionic cell bodies location: Sympathetic chain ganglia

the Sympathetic Neurotransmitters and Receptors (you don’t need to know the receptors at the level of alpha1 and Beta1...just cholinergic and adrenergic)

Acetylcholine (ACh) –used in synapses between sympathetic preganglionic and postganglionic neurons

• postganglionic axons then transmit action potentials to target cell

• Neurotransmitters bind to specific receptors of target cells

1. Adrenergic receptors (α or β) bind to epinephrine & norepinephrine

2. Cholinergic receptors (Nicotinic & Muscarinic) bind to Acetylcholine

• Muscarinic receptors – on sweat glands in skin

• Nicotinic receptors – in membranes of all postganglionic neurons within sympathetic ganglia and adrenal medullae
