the effects of the Sympathetic Nervous System on the target cells

Smooth muscle cells around the pupil: Dilation of the pupil.

Cells of salivary glands: Increase in secretion in certain cells.

Cardiac muscle cells: Increase in heart rate and force of contraction.

Smooth muscle cells of bronchi: Dilation of bronchioles (bronchodilation).

Cells of sweat glands: Increase in secretion.

Smooth muscle cells of blood vessels to skin: Constriction of blood vessels (vasoconstriction).

Smooth muscle cells of blood vessels to skeletal muscles: Dilation of blood vessels (vasodilation).

Smooth muscle cells of blood vessels to digestive and urinary organs: Vasoconstriction.

Smooth muscle cells of digestive and urinary tracts and sphincters: Relaxation of digestive and urinary tracts, contraction of sphincters.

Cells of digestive glands: Decrease in secretion.

Cells of pancreas and liver: Increase in release of glucose.

Cells of adrenal medulla: Release of epinephrine and norepinephrine.

the relationship between the Adrenal Medulla and the Sympathetic Nervous System.

1. it prolongs the duration of the sympathetic nervous system’s effects.

2. The epinephrine and norepinephrine in the blood can reach cells that are not innervated by sympathetic neurons

3.The adrenal medulla acts as a “backup” for the sympathetic nervous system—even if the pathways connecting this system with many of its target organs are disrupted, it can still affect these organs indirectly via the adrenal medulla

the anatomy of the Parasympathetic Nervous System and how it’s organized.

craniosacral division - cranial and sacral

Parasympathetic cranial nerves

associated with oculomotor (CN III), facial (CN VII), glossopharyngeal (CN IX), and vagus (CN X) nerves

Vagus nerves – main parasympathetic nerves that innervate most thoracic and abdominal viscera; 90% of parasympathetic output

Contribute to cardiac, pulmonary, and esophageal plexuses

Parasympathetic sacral nerves

innervate last segment of large intestine, urinary bladder, and reproductive organs.

Sacral nerve branches form pelvic splanchnic nerves; then form plexuses in pelvic floor

Parasympathetic Neuron Location

Preganglionic cell bodies: Craniosacral region → brainstem nuclei (CN III, VII, IX, X) + S2–S4 spinal cord

Postganglionic cell bodies: located in or very near target organs

the Parasympathetic Neurotransmitters and Receptors

Both pre- and postganglionic parasympathetic neurons release ACh at their synapses

Two Cholinergic Receptors:

1. Nicotinic receptors – in membranes of all postganglionic neurons

2. Muscarinic receptors – in membranes of all parasympathetic target cells

the effects of the Parasympathetic Nervous System on the target cells

Smooth muscle cells around pupil and lens: constriction of pupil, adjustment of lens for near vision.

Cells of lacrimal and salivary glands: increase in secretions.

Cardiac muscle cells: decrease in heart rate.

Smooth muscle cells of bronchioles: bronchoconstriction.

Smooth muscle cells of digestive tract and sphincters: contraction of digestive tract smooth muscle, relaxation of sphincters.

Cells of digestive glands: increase in secretions.

Smooth muscle cells of urinary sphincters: relaxation of sphincters.

the role of the Sympathetic and Parasympathetic Nervous Systems on maintaining homeostasis

Sympathetic division is dominant and maintains homeostasis during physically demanding times

Parasympathetic division maintains homeostasis between periods of increased physical activity

how olfaction receptors are activated.

Before step 1: Odorants are dissolved in mucus

1. Odor binds → G-protein activates → G -Protein Detaches from receptor

2. G-protein triggers adenylate cyclase to turn ATP into cAMP.

3. cAMP opens ion channels → Sodium/Calcium rush in → depolarization → action potential if threshold is hit.

the type of receptors that involved in gustatory sense

Chemoreceptors

It’s complicated!! – Involves olfactory chemoreceptors, thermoreceptors, nociceptors, and gustatory chemoreceptors (taste buds= found on tongue and other surfaces of oral cavity)

the anatomy of the taste bud

Taste buds – usually located on lateral surfaces of papillae

Contain three cell types:

1. Gustatory (taste) cells

▪ specialized epithelial cells with microvilli

▪ project into opening on papilla surface (taste pore)

▪ Have receptors that detect different tastes

▪ Carry taste stimuli to CNS via CN VII, CN IX, CN X

2. Basal cells

stem cells differentiate into new gustatory cells live 10–14 days

3. Supporting cells

▪ physically support gustatory cells

▪ no role in taste sensation

Classified by shape:

1. Vallate (circumvallate) papillae – largest, dome-shaped

▪ Contain 100s of taste buds

2. Fungiform papillae – mushroom-shaped

▪ Contain few taste buds

3. Foliate papillae – ridges on sides of tongue

▪ Only contain taste buds in childhood

4. Filiform papillae – long, thin cylinders scattered across tongue

▪ Devoid of taste buds

▪ Contain sensory nerve endings to detect food texture and temperature

the cranial nerves that are associated with gustation

Cranial nerves for taste: VII (facial), IX (glossopharyngeal), and X (vagus).

the 5 classes of chemicals that we can taste

1. Sweet tastes – elicited by simple sugars (glucose, fructose)

2. Sour tastes – produced by hydrogen ions (citric acid in lemon juice)

3. Salty tastes – elicited by presence of metal ions (Na+ and K + ions)

4. Bitter flavors – produced by nitrogen-containing compounds

▪ commonly in rancid or poisonous substances

5. Umami – (savory) taste associated with meat or broth

▪ produced by glutamate or other amino acids
