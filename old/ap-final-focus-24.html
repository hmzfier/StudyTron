how sound is processed in the inner ear

Perilymph = Fluid in the scala vestibuli

Endolymph = Fluid in the cochlear duct

Chatgpt explanation:

1. Eardrum → ossicles → oval window vibrates →

2. the vibration → produces pressure waves in the perilymph of the scala vestibuli and the endolymph of the cochlear duct → basilar membrane vibrates.

3. High frequency = sounds travel only a short distance into the cochlea > basilar membrane vibrates where it is narrow and stiff

4. Low frequency = travel farther into the cochlea > basilar membrane vibrates where it is wide and flexible

Longer explanation:

1. When sound waves strike the tympanic membrane, movement of the connected auditory ossicles causes the oval window to vibrate at the same frequency.

2. Vibration of the oval window produces pressure waves in the perilymph of the scala vestibuli and the endolymph of the cochlear duct, which then vibrate the basilar membrane.

3a. Pressure waves from high-frequency sounds travel only a short distance into the cochlea and cause the basilar membrane to vibrate where it is narrow and stiff.

3b. Pressure waves from low-frequency sounds travel farther into the cochlea and cause the basilar membrane to vibrate where it is wide and flexible.

the auditory pathway from the cochlea to through the primary auditory cortex

Auditory Pathway — Easy-to-Remember Version

1. Action potentials travel to → CN VIII (vestibulocochlear nerve, specifically cochlear nerve)

2. → Cochlear nuclei (medulla–pons junction)

3. → Superior olivary nucleus (located in pons)

4. → Inferior colliculus of midbrain

5.→ Medial geniculate nucleus (MGN) of the thalamus

6. → Primary auditory cortex (superior temporal lobe)

Long textbook answer:

1. Action potentials propagate through axons of the cochlear portion of the vestibulocochlear nerve to the cochlear nuclei at the medulla-pons junction.

2. Axons from the cochlear nuclei synapse on the superior olivary nucleus in the pons.

3. Auditory stimuli are then sent to the inferior colliculus of the midbrain

4. The auditory stimuli are relayed to the medial geniculate nucleus of the thalamus.

5. The thalamus stimulates neurons of the primary auditory cortex in the superior portion of the temporal lobe.

that anatomy of the maculae and how they detect head position and movement and linear acceleration

maculae = found in utricle and saccule

Maculae Anatomy easy version:

• Located in: Utricle + Saccule (vestibule of inner ear)

• Hair cells: Have stereocilia + one kinocilium

• Gel layer: Hair cells are embedded in a gelatinous membrane

• Otoliths: Tiny calcium carbonate crystals sitting on top of the gel (add weight)

Maculae Anatomy:

Found in utricle and saccule

Macula – contains receptor cells for head position and movement

Each hair cell displays stereocilia and one true cilium (kinocilium)

Kinocilium and stereocilia are embedded in gelatinous mass (otolithic membrane); suspended in endolymph

Otoliths – calcium carbonate crystals in otolithic membrane that increase the membrane density

Gel layer topped with otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals)

How Maculae Detect Head Tilting FROM BOOK

1. Head Tilts > bends the stereocilia.

2. If toward kinocilium = local potential → depolarize → release glutamate → more action potentials produced in the axons of the vestibular nerve.

3. If away from kinocilium = hyperpolarize → release less glutamate → less action potentials produced in the axons of the vestibular nerve.

The maculae are paired, so that tilting your head in one direction depolarizes hair cells in one ear and hyperpolarizes hair cells in the other ear. This increases action potentials in one vestibular nerve and decreases action potentials in the other.

The brain interprets the combination of signals as the tilting of your head in a particular direction.

How Maculae Detect Linear Acceleration (short version)

1. Body moves first; otolithic membrane lags → bends stereocilia.

2. Bend → depolarize or hyperpolarize → change glutamate → change nerve firing.

3. When movement stops, endolymph and the otolithic membrane keeps moving → bends stereocilia in opposite direction → altering glutamate release.

Hair cells release neurotransmitter glutamate onto dendrites of bipolar neurons that form vestibular division of CN VIII

Stereocilia in utricle are vertically oriented when head is upright and stationary

▪bend in response to tilting head side to side or front to back

Stereocilia in saccule are horizontally oriented when head is upright and stationary

▪bend in response to up and down movements of head

the anatomy of the semicircular ducts and how they function to detect rotation of the head

Semicircular Duct Anatomy:

3 ducts: anterior, posterior, lateral

Each duct is connected to utricle at the ampulla

Inside each ampulla is the crista ampullaris (hair cells)

Hair cells’ stereocilia are embedded in a gel structure called the cupula

How They Detect Head Rotation:

1. When the head rotates, endolymph lags behind and pushes the cupula

2. Cupula bends the hair cell stereocilia

3. Bending changes hair cell firing → brain detects rotational movement

Long answer

1. Head turns → endolymph lags and pushes the cupula.

2. Cupula bends stereocilia → hair cells change glutamate release → vestibular nerve firing changes.

3. Brain reads this as starting rotation.

4. During steady rotation → endolymph catches up → little to no bending.

5. When rotation stops → endolymph keeps moving → cupula bends opposite → opposite glutamate change → brain senses stopping rotation.

the vestibular sensation pathway

Vestibular pathway (easy version):

1. Action potentials go to vestibular nuclei (medulla–pons junction).

2. From there, they go to:

• Thalamus → parietal lobe for awareness of head position

• Cranial nerve nuclei for eye–head coordination

• Cerebellum + spinal cord for balance and posture

Longer version from textbook

1. Action potentials propagate to the vestibular nuclei located at the medulla-pons junction.

2. The vestibular nuclei relay the signals simultaneously to the following areas of the CNS:

2a. The thalamus and then the inferior parietal lobe, for conscious awareness of head position and movement.

2b. The cranial nerve nuclei, to coordinate eye movement in response to head movement.

2c. The cerebellum and spinal cord, to coordinate muscle movement that maintains balance in response to head movement.
