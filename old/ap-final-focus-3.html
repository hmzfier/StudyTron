the general processes common to all cells

• Cell metabolism: sum of all chemical reactions that cell carries out to maintain life (Catabolism, Anabolism, Oxidation/reduction)

• Transport of substances: substances cell has produced or ingested to variety of destinations

• Communication: between cell and itself, its surrounding environment, and other cells; chemical and electrical signals

• Cell reproduction: by cell division (Growth, Reproduction, Replacement of old or damaged tissue)

the 3 basic components of all animal cells, including their structure and function

1. Plasma Membrane

Structure: Phospholipid bilayer with proteins.

Function: Controls what enters/exits; protects the cell; helps with communication.

2. Cytoplasm

Structure: Jelly-like fluid (cytosol) + organelles. More structures Below

Organelles – cellular machines with very specific functions; suspended in cytosol; separate potentially damaging chemical reactions from surrounding cell structures (compartmentalization)

• Cytoskeleton – network of protein filaments; creates and maintains shape; holds organelles in place; provides transportation for substances within cel

Function: Site of most cell reactions; holds organelles in place.

3. Nucleus

Structure: Membrane-bound sphere containing DNA.

Function: Stores genetic information; controls cell activities (“control center”).

Passive Transport

No energy needed

Moves from high → low concentration

• Concentration gradient – force that drives many types of passive transport

Types to remember:

• Diffusion – movement from high to low concentration; move down (with) concentration gradient until equilibrium (no net movement) reached

• Osmosis – Water moves from low to high

Water moves across plasma membranes by two methods:

• Water passes through channel proteins (aquaporins); primary route for osmosis of water

• Small amounts of water can pass through phospholipid bilayer directly

NOTE: OSMOSIS IS FROM LOW TO HIGH DESPITE BEING A PASSIVE TRANSPORT. STILL NO ENERGY IS NEEDED

• Simple diffusion – moves nonpolar molecules (oxygen, carbon dioxide, lipids, and hydrocarbons); pass through phospholipid bilayer without membrane protein. No protein needed — they slip through the membrane.

• Facilitated diffusion – moves charged or polar solutes (ions and glucose); cross phospholipid bilayer with help of membrane protein (carrier or channel). Protein needed — uses channels or carriers.

Three variables determine how substance is able to move across plasma membrane by passive or active transport:

1. Type of substance

2. Plasma membrane permeability to substance

3. Concentration of substance in cytosol and ECF

Dehydration, Sports Drinks, and Water

• Strenuous exercise results in water and electrolyte loss (sweating); ECF becomes hypertonic; draws water out of cells by osmosis

• Sports drinks (mixtures of water, electrolytes, and carbohydrates) are hypotonic; replenish lost water, making ECF mildly hypotonic to cells; water moves back into cells; restore normal cytosol concentration

• Plain water rehydrates just as well; care must be taken in severe dehydration; can rehydrate cells too quickly or overhydrate (hypotonic ECF); results in cellular swelling and possibly water poisoning
