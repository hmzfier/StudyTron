Active Transport via Membrane Proteins - uniport, Symport, Antiport

Three types of pumps in plasma membrane:

• Uniport – transport single substance through membrane in one direction (into or out of cell)

• Symport – transport two or more substances through membrane in same direction (into or out of cell)

• Antiport – transport two or more substances in opposite directions through membrane

Endocytosis and the 3 types

A type of active transport

• Endocytosis – fluid, molecules taken into cell

3 types of Endocytosis

1. Phagocytosis (“cell eating”) –cells ingest large particles like bacteria or dead or damaged cells or parts of cell

2. Pinocytosis (fluid-phase endocytosis or “cell drinking”) – cells engulf fluid droplets from E C F

3. Receptor-mediated endocytosis – special form of pinocytosis; receptors fill vesicles with specific molecule (cholesterol, hormones, iron)

Primary and secondary transport

These are both active transport

Primary Active Transport

Directly uses ATP

Pumps molecules against their gradient

Example: Sodium–potassium pump

Memory tip:

“Primary = ATP pump.”

Secondary Active Transport

Does NOT use ATP directly

Uses the energy from one molecule moving down its gradient to push another against its gradient

Depends on gradients created by primary pumps

Two types:

Symport: both molecules move same direction

Antiport: molecules move opposite directions

where and how melanin is produced

Produced by melanocytes in stratum basale

Composed of two molecules of amino acid tyrosine; chemically bonded by series of reactions catalyzed by enzyme tyrosinase; occurs in stepwise fashion within special vesicle (melanosome)

Melanin synthesis increases with exposure to natural or artificial UV radiation; leads to

tanning (darkening of skin pigmentation); has both immediate and delayed effects on

skin pigmentation:

• Immediate response – oxidation of melanin already present in keratinocytes; causes melanin to quickly darken

• Delayed or secondary effects – UV light causes DNA damage in melanocytes; stimulates melanin production; appears within 72 hours; lasts longer than melanin oxidation

the functions of melanin

Primary function – protecting keratinocyte DNA from mutations induced by UV radiation

Secondary function of melanin: reduces synthesis of vitamin D in response to UV radiation; leads to less calcium ion absorption and maintenance of calcium ion homeostasis within narrow range:

• Individuals living in regions exposed to high amounts of UV radiation (Africa) developed darker skin to prevent excess vitamin D production

• People in areas with less UV radiation (northern Europe) developed lighter skin so they could synthesize enough vitamin D

the fascicles and their shapes and how that affects their function

1. Parallel Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles run parallel to the long axis of the muscle

Function: Produce great range of motion but less force

Example: Sartorius, biceps brachii

2. Fusiform Fascicles:

Description: Spindle-shaped, wider in the middle and taper at ends

Function: Moderate force and range of motion

Example: Biceps brachii

3. Circular Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles arranged in concentric rings around openings

Function: Act as sphincters, controlling opening and closing

Example: Orbicularis oris, orbicularis oculi

4. Convergent Fascicles:

Description: Broad origin and converge to a single tendon

Function: Versatile; can generate strong force

Example: Pectoralis major

5. Pennate Fascicles:

Description: Short fascicles attach obliquely to a central tendon

Function: Produce more force but less range of motion

Unipennate: Fibers on one side of tendon – extensor digitorum

Bipennate: Fibers on both sides of tendon – rectus femoris

Multipennate: Tendon branches within muscle – deltoid

the structure of myofibrils

Myofibrils are long, cylindrical-like structures inside each muscle fiber (muscle cell).

100s to 1000s of myofibrils in each myocyte: 50-80% of cell volume

They are the parts of the muscle that actually contract — they make the muscle shorten and generate force.

Each muscle fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils running parallel to each other.

Myofibrils are made up of repeating units called sarcomeres — the smallest functional units of muscle contraction.

how skeletal muscle is affected by physical training

Endurance Training = muscles work longer, not bigger.

Resistance training (strength) = Fewer reps, heavy weight → muscles grow larger and stronger (hypertrophy)

Disuse = No activity → muscles shrink and weaken (Atrophy)

what leads to muscle fatigue

Fatigue - inability to maintain a given level of intensity during activity; caused by multiple factors:

Depletion of key metabolites (creatine phosphate, glycogen, and glucose) involved in ATP production

Decreased availability of oxygen to muscle fibers (increased demand during exercise, depleted myoglobin-bound oxygen and inadequate oxygen intake in lungs)

Accumulation of certain chemicals in fiber (Ca++ , ADP, and phosphate)

Environmental conditions, particularly extreme heat; sweating may also cause electrolyte disturbances

Compare and contrast electrical vs. chemical synapses

Electrical synapses: direct ion flow through gap junctions; very fast; bidirectional; rare in humans.

•Axolemmas of each cell in synapse are nearly touching

• Gap junctions align channels forming pores that ions or other small substances can flow through

▪ In areas of brain responsible for programmed, automatic behaviors (breathing)

▪ In cardiac and visceral smooth muscle to allow for coordinated muscle activity

• Transmission is bidirectional and nearly instantaneous

Chemical synapses: use neurotransmitters to signal across a synaptic cleft; slower; unidirectional; most common in humans.

• Make up majority of synapses in nervous system

• More efficient than electrical synapses

• No signal strength is lost (as at electrical synapses)

• Electrical signal becomes weaker as it dissipates into ECF

• Convert electrical signals into chemical signals

what the white and gray matter in the brain and spinal cord are made of and where they are located in both organs.

Brain:

Gray matter: made of neuron cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated axons; located in outer cerebrum (cerebral cortex)

White matter: made of myelinated axons; located beneath the gray matter in the brain (connects different brain regions).

Spinal cord:

Gray matter: located centrally in an H- or butterfly-shaped region; contains neuronal cell bodies and interneurons.

White matter: located peripherally around the gray matter; contains myelinated axons.
