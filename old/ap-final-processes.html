Intramembranous Ossification

FOR FLAT BONES ONLY - SUCH AS THE SKULL, CLAVICLE

Definition: bone forms directly from mesenchyme (connective tissue)

Simple definition: Intramembranous ossification is the process your body uses to make certain bones directly from a sheet of connective tissue

Forms many flat bones skull and clavicles during fetal development

Forms primary bone (which will become secondary bone)

Steps:

1. Mesenchymal cells in primary ossification center → become osteoblasts

2. Osteoblasts secrete organic matrix → calcifies → Osteoblasts trapped → become osteocytes

3. Osteoblasts lay down Trabeculae → becomes spongy bone → some messenchyme turns into periosteum

4. Osteoblasts in the periosteum lay down early compact bone

Location: flat bones (skull, clavicle)

Endochondral Ossification

Endochondral ossification is for: Long bones (femur, tibia, humerus), Most bones of the skeleton, All bones below the skull except clavicles, Bones that start as a hyaline cartilage model

Definition: bone forms by replacing hyaline cartilage

Simple Explanation: Endochondral ossification is how most of your bones—especially long bones like your arms and legs—are made, and it’s a bit like building a bone “on top of a cartilage template.”

Steps Simple:

1. Chondroblasts in perichondrium become osteoblasts

2. Osteoblasts build Bone collar forms on outside; internal cartilage calcifies and chondrocytes die

3. Primary ossification center: osteoblasts replace calcified cartilage with spongy bone; secondary ossification centers and medullary cavity develop

4. Medullary cavity enlarges > remaining cartilage replaced by bone > epiphyses finish ossifying

Steps from book:

1. The chondroblasts in the perichondrium differentiate into osteoblasts

2a. Osteoblasts build the bone collar on the bone's external surface as the bone begins to ossify from the outside

2b. Simultaneously, the internal cartilage begins to calcify and chondrocytes die

3. In the primary ossification center, osteoblasts replace the calcified cartilage with early spongy bone; the secondary ossification centers and medullary cavity develop

4. As the medullary cavity enlarges, the remaining cartilage is replaced by bone, the epiphysis finish ossifying.

Location: most long bones (femur, humerus)

the events of the excitation-contraction coupling

Excitation-contraction coupling → Process linking muscle action potential to contraction.

Short Answer:

Excitation

1. Action potential reaches axon terminal → calcium ion channels open → calcium enters → ACh released.

2. ACh binds receptors → Na⁺/K⁺ enter → sarcolemma depolarizes → end-plate potential.

Excitation-Contraction Coupling

3. End-plate potential triggers action potential → travels down T-tubules.

4. T-tubule depolarization opens SR calcium channels → calcium enters cytosol.

Contraction

5. Calcium binds troponin → tropomyosin moves → actin sites exposed.

6. Myosin head “cocks” (ATP → ADP + P) → binds actin → power stroke → pulls actin.

7. ADP released → new ATP detaches myosin → cycle repeats while ATP & calcium are present.

Long Answer from book:

1. Excitation

An action potential arrives at the axon terminal and triggers calcium ion channels in the axon terminal to open.

Calcium ion entry triggers exocytosis of synaptic vesicles.

Synaptic vesicles release acetylcholine into the synaptic cleft.

Acetylcholine binds to ligand-gated cation channels in the motor end plate.

Sodium and potassium channels open and cations enter the muscle fiber.

Entry of Sodium and potassium depolarizes the sarcolemma locally, producing an end-plate potential.

2. Excitation-Contraction Coupling

The end-plate potential stimulates an action potential.

The action potential is travels down the T-tubules.

T-tubule depolarization leads to the opening of calcium ion channels in the terminal cisternae of the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and calcium ions enter the cytosol.

3. Contraction

Calcium ions bind to troponin.

Tropomyosin moves, and the active sites of actin are exposed.

ATP hydrolysis “cocks” the myosin head - ATP hydrolysis turns ATP to ADP and a phosphate group (P)

The myosin head binds to actin.

The power stroke occurs when the phosphate detaches from the myosin head

Then the myosin pulls actin toward the center of the sarcomere

ADP leaves the myosin head at the end of the power stroke.

A new ATP breaks the attachment of myosin to actin.

Then the cross-bridge and power stroke cycle repeats as long as: ATP is available, and Calcium remains present in the sarcoplasm.

the cross-bridge cycle components: how ATP is involved, what the power stroke is

Cross-bridge cycle components in order:

1. ATP binds myosin → detaches myosin from actin.

2. ATP hydrolysis (ATP → ADP + P) → “cocks” myosin head into high-energy position.

3. Myosin binds actin → forms cross-bridge.

4. Power stroke → phosphate release causes myosin head to pivot, pulling actin toward sarcomere center.

5. ADP released → myosin remains bound until new ATP attaches → cycle repeats.

skeletal muscle relaxation components - 5 steps

1. Motor neuron stops firing action potentials. No more acetylcholine (ACh) is released

2. Acetylcholinesterase degrades the remaining acetylcholine, and the final repolarization occurs

3. The sarcolemma returns to its resting membrane potential, and the calcium ion channels in the sarcoplasmic reticulum close.

4. Calcium ions are actively pumped back into Sarcoplasmic reticulum (using ATP)

5. Troponin shifts and pulls tropomyosin back into position to block the active sites of actin, and the muscle relaxes

where ATP is required in the skeletal muscle - 4

1. Power Sodium/Potassium pumps that maintain ion gradients (action potentials)

2. Release myosin heads from actin active sites

3. recock heads for another power stroke

4. Pump calcium back into SR (relaxation

how action potential propagate down the axon and what affects the speed of the action potential

Steps

1. The axolemma depolarizes to threshold due to local potentials

2. As sodium channels activate, an action potential is triggered and spreads down the axon

3. the next section of the axolemma depolarizes to threshold and fires an action potential as the previous section of the axolemma repolarizes

4. the current continues to move down the axon, and the process repeats

3. Each new section hits threshold and fires while the previous section repolarizes.

4. This repeating cycle pushes the action potential down the axon.

Myelination (fastest; forces AP to jump node-to-node)

Larger axon diameter (less resistance)

Higher temperature (up to physiological limits)

how taste chemicals are transduced to electrical signal that is sent to the brain

Short Summary

1. Taste depolarizes the cell

2. Voltage-gated calcium channels open > calcium enters the cell

3. Calcium ions trigger the release of neurotransmitters

4. the release produces an action potential in the axon of the sensory neuron

Long explanation

1. Changes in ion movements depolarize the gustatory cell's plasma membrane

2. Depolarization of the membrane opens voltage-gated calcium channels, and calcium channels enter the cell.

3. The calcium trigger the release of neurotransmitters, which produce an action potential in the axon of the sensory neuron

how images are processed in the retina in the dark and light

Dark (no light):

1. Photoreceptor depolarized → releases glutamate onto bipolar cells

2. Glutamate inhibits bipolar cell

3. No action potential

Light:

1. Photoreceptor hyperpolarized → stops glutamate

2. Bipolar cell activated → Releases Neurotransmitters onto the retinal ganglion cell

3. Retinal Ganglion cell fires action potentials to the brain via the optic nerve

how sound is processed in the inner ear

Perilymph = Fluid in the scala vestibuli

Endolymph = Fluid in the cochlear duct

Chatgpt explanation:

1. Eardrum → ossicles → oval window vibrates →

2. the vibration → produces pressure waves in the perilymph of the scala vestibuli and the endolymph of the cochlear duct → basilar membrane vibrates.

3. High frequency = sounds travel only a short distance into the cochlea > basilar membrane vibrates where it is narrow and stiff

4. Low frequency = travel farther into the cochlea > basilar membrane vibrates where it is wide and flexible

Longer explanation:

1. When sound waves strike the tympanic membrane, movement of the connected auditory ossicles causes the oval window to vibrate at the same frequency.

2. Vibration of the oval window produces pressure waves in the perilymph of the scala vestibuli and the endolymph of the cochlear duct, which then vibrate the basilar membrane.

3a. Pressure waves from high-frequency sounds travel only a short distance into the cochlea and cause the basilar membrane to vibrate where it is narrow and stiff.

3b. Pressure waves from low-frequency sounds travel farther into the cochlea and cause the basilar membrane to vibrate where it is wide and flexible.

HOW Maculae DETECT HEAD POSITION & LINEAR ACCELERATION - Easy Version

1. Head tilts / body accelerates

2. Otoliths shift due to gravity or straight-line movement

3. Otolith movement pulls the gel layer

4. Gel bends the stereocilia on hair cells

5. Hair cell bending changes action potentials sent through the vestibular branch of CN VIII

6. Brain interprets this as head position and linear acceleration (not rotation).

Semicircular ducts and how they function to detect rotation of the head

When the head rotates, endolymph lags behind and pushes the cupula

Cupula bends the hair cell stereocilia

Bending changes hair cell firing → brain detects rotational movement

Vestibular Sensation = How Maculae Detect Head Tilting FROM BOOK

1. Head Tilts > bends the stereocilia.

2. If toward kinocilium = local potential → depolarize → release glutamate → more action potentials produced in the axons of the vestibular nerve.

3. If away from kinocilium = hyperpolarize → release less glutamate → less action potentials produced in the axons of the vestibular nerve.

The maculae are paired, so that tilting your head in one direction depolarizes hair cells in one ear and hyperpolarizes hair cells in the other ear. This increases action potentials in one vestibular nerve and decreases action potentials in the other.

The brain interprets the combination of signals as the tilting of your head in a particular direction.

Vestibular Sensation = How Maculae Detect Linear Acceleration (short version)

1. Body moves first; otolithic membrane lags → bends stereocilia.

2. Bend → depolarize or hyperpolarize → change glutamate → change nerve firing.

3. When movement stops, endolymph and the otolithic membrane keeps moving → bends stereocilia in opposite direction → altering glutamate release.

Vestibular Sensation = Semicircular ducts and how they function to detect rotation of the head

How They Detect Head Rotation:

1. When the head rotates, endolymph lags behind and pushes the cupula

2. Cupula bends the hair cell stereocilia

3. Bending changes hair cell firing → brain detects rotational movement

Long answer

1. Head turns → endolymph lags and pushes the cupula.

2. Cupula bends stereocilia → hair cells change glutamate release → vestibular nerve firing changes.

3. Brain reads this as starting rotation.

4. During steady rotation → endolymph catches up → little to no bending.

5. When rotation stops → endolymph keeps moving → cupula bends opposite → opposite glutamate change → brain senses stopping rotation.

the vestibular sensation pathway

Vestibular pathway (easy version):

1. Action potentials go to vestibular nuclei (medulla–pons junction).

2. From there, they go to:

• Thalamus → parietal lobe for awareness of head position

• Cranial nerve nuclei for eye–head coordination

• Cerebellum + spinal cord for balance and posture

Longer version from textbook

1. Action potentials propagate to the vestibular nuclei located at the medulla-pons junction.

2. The vestibular nuclei relay the signals simultaneously to the following areas of the CNS:

2a. The thalamus and then the inferior parietal lobe, for conscious awareness of head position and movement.

2b. The cranial nerve nuclei, to coordinate eye movement in response to head movement.

2c. The cerebellum and spinal cord, to coordinate muscle movement that maintains balance in response to head movement.
