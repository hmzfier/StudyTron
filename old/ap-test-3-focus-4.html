21. the 3 meningeal layers and 3 spaces that help protect the brain

Meningeal layers: dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater.

dura mater- thickest and most durable meningeal layer. double-layered membrane (periosteal and meningeal dura)

arachnoid mater- thin weblike membrane

pia mater-– deepest meningeal layer. Has physical contact with brain tissue. Permeable to substances in brain extracellular fluid and CSF. Allows for substances to move between fluid compartments

Spaces: epidural, subdural, subarachnoid.

epidural space-”Potentially” between skull and dura mater. Only allows passage of blood vessels

subdural space- serous fluid-filled space. houses veins that drain blood from brain

subarachnoid space –contains major blood vessels of brain. filled with CSF

25. the 2 primary roles of the spinal cord

Relay station

The spinal cord acts as a relay station and an intermediate point between the body and the brain; in fact, it’s the only means by which the brain can communicate with most of the body below the head and neck. It receives outgoing stimuli from the brain and sends them to the rest of the body; it also receives incoming stimuli from the body and sends them to the brain.

Processing station

Although the spinal cord cannot perform the higher functions characteristic of the brain, it does do some integration and processing. Indeed, certain activities called spinal reflexes can be carried out by the spinal cord alone, without influence from the brain

29. the spinal cord white matter: know what is the difference between ascending and descending tracts, location of 1st, 2nd and 3rd order neurons. (details of each tract are not required)

White matter tracts:

• Ascending tracts – carry sensory information to the brain.

1st-order neurons – PNS

2nd-order neurons – located in posterior horn of spinal cord or in the brainstem

3rd-order neurons – thalamus

• Descending tracts – carry motor commands from the brain to the body.

Upper motor neurons – from cerebral cortex/brainstem to spinal cord.

Lower motor neurons – from spinal cord to muscles.

31. how CNS plans and coordinates voluntary movement including the role of the cerebral cortex, basal nuclei and cerebellum

• Cerebral cortex – plans and initiates voluntary movement.

• Basal nuclei – help start, stop, and smooth movements; inhibit unnecessary movements.

• Cerebellum – coordinates timing, precision, and balance of movements.

Steps

1. CNS: Upper motor neurons in the premotor cortex select a motor program and stimulate the basal nuclei

2. The basal nuclei remove inhibition from the thalamus which stimulates upper motor neurons of the primary motor cortex

3. CNS to PNS: upper motor neurons stimulate lower motor neurons

4. PNS: Lower motor neurons stimulate a muscle to contract

the 2 divisions (Sensory and motor) divisions

Sensory = Somatic Sensory and Visceral Sensory

Somatic sensory division – detects both internal and external stimuli

▪ General sense receptors detect stimuli from skin and skeletal muscles

▪ Special sensory receptors detect stimuli from special sense organs

Motor = Somatic motor and Visceral Motor

Somatic motor division – voluntary motor functions

– Lower motor neurons (somatic motor neurons) that directly trigger skeletal muscle contractions

Visceral motor division (autonomic motor nervous system, ANS) – maintains homeostasis by controlling involuntary motor functions in body

▪ cardiac muscle cells, smooth muscle cells, and secretory cells of glands

ANS is further divided into:

▪ Sympathetic nervous system (fight or flight division) – homeostasis activities surrounding physical work and visceral responses of emotions

▪ Parasympathetic system (rest and digest division) –digestion and maintaining body’s homeostasis at rest

Visceral sensory division – relays internal info (like blood pressure) from organs of abdominopelvic and thoracic cavities

Name some general senses and special senses from the somatic sensory division.

General senses: touch, pressure, vibration, pain, temperature, proprioception.

Special senses: vision, hearing, equilibrium (balance), taste, smell.

3. the structures of the spinal nerves

Endoneurium = connective tissue around each axon

Perineurium = around each fascicle

Epineurium = connective tissue around the entire nerve. Holds motor and sensory axons together
