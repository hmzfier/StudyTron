What are the diagnostic criteria for a substance use disorder?

A problematic pattern of use (within last 12 mo) causing significant impairment/distress as manifested by 2 or more of the following:

• Tolerance

• Withdrawal

• Craving

• Use longer and more than you intend

• Want to cut down but cannot

• Spend a great deal of time getting, using or recovering from use

• Use results in failure to fulfill major role obligations

• Continued use despite persistent and recurrent social or interpersonal problems that are caused or exacerbated by the effects of the substance

• Important social, recreational, or occupational activities are given up or reduced because of substance use

• Recurrent use in physically dangerous situations

• Continue despite the knowledge that the substance is physically and/or psychologically damaging

Mild: 2-3 symptoms

Moderate: 4-5 symptoms

Severe: 6 or more symptoms

What is tolerance? Intoxication?

Intoxication: Reversible symptoms due to the direct effects of a substance ingested or exposed

Tolerance: Using the same amount of a substance no longer has the desired effect

A need to take more and more of a substance in order to attain the usual level of intoxication or high

Why are alcohol, opioids and sedative hypnotics considered depressants?

Because they slow down CNS activity by enhancing inhibitory neurotransmission (mainly GABA) or reducing excitatory signaling, which decreases brain and body function.

How is the method of ingesting a drug related to the intensity of the high and the risk of addiction?

Faster delivery → stronger high → higher addiction risk.

Smoking or injecting: hits the brain in seconds → very intense high → highest addiction risk.

Snorting: slower than smoking/injecting → moderate high → moderate risk.

Oral (pills/alcohol): slowest → mildest high → lowest risk.

Speed to the brain is the key factor.

What is aversion therapy? Contingency management? Relapse prevention training? What is the abstinence violation effect? Seemingly irrelevant decisions?

Aversion therapy:

Pairing the substance (or substance-related cues) with something unpleasant to create a negative association. Example: Shock and alcohol, alcohol use while on drug that makes you vomit

Contingency management:

Giving rewards (vouchers, prizes, money equivalents) for clean drug tests or other sober behaviors. Typically not used in isolation (used with other treatments

Relapse prevention training:

Teaching patients to identify triggers, avoid high-risk situations, and use coping skills to prevent relapse.

Abstinence violation effect:

When a person slips once, then feels guilt/shame and thinks “I already messed up, so it doesn’t matter,” which leads to a full relapse.

Seemingly irrelevant decisions:

Small choices that don’t look risky on the surface but move someone closer to a high-risk situation (example: driving near an old hangout), increasing relapse chances.

Know the criteria for gambling disorder. What explanations are offered for this disorder? What treatments?

Gambling Disorder: Criteria

• Recurrent and persistent problematic gambling leading to significant distress/impairment within the last 12 months with 4 or more of the following:

1. Need to gamble with increasing amounts of money in order to achieve the desired excitement

2. Restlessness or irritability when attempting to cut down or stop gambling

3. Has unsuccessfully tried to cut back or stop gambling

4. Is often preoccupied with gambling

5. Often gambles when feeling distressed (helpless, guilty, anxious, depressed)

6. After losing money gambling, often returns to gambling the next day to “get even”

7. Lies to conceal the extent of involvement in gambling

8. Has jeopardized/lost a significant relationship, job or educational/career opportunity b/c of gambling

9. Relies on others to provide money to relieve desperate financial situations caused by gambling

**The gambling is not better explained by a manic episode

• Mild: 4-5 criteria met; moderate: 6-7; Severe 8-9

Explanation: No single explanation appears sufficient

Treatments include cognitive-behavioral approaches, such as relapse-prevention training, and biological approaches, such as opioid antagonists.

In addition, the self-help group program Gamblers Anonymous, a network modeled after Alcoholics Anonymous, is available to the many thousands of people with gambling disorder. People who attend such groups seem to have a better recovery rate.

What are the diagnostic criteria for schizophrenia? How long do symptoms need to persist for a

diagnosis?

• Criterion A: 2 (or more) of the following, each present for a sig. portion of time during a 1- month period

• Delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior (or catatonic behavior), negative sxs

• Criterion B: For a significant portion of time since onset, one or more major areas of functioning (work, school, self care, interpersonal) is markedly below the level achieved prior to onset

• Criterion C: Continuous signs of a disturbance in functioning for at least 6 months (w/ at least one month of “active-phase symptoms” from A, unless successfully treated)

• Criterion D: Schizoaffective disorder, MDD and bipolar I disorder have been ruled out

• Criterion E: The disturbance is not attributable to substance use or another medical condition

• Criterion F: If there is a hx of an autism spectrum disorder, the additional diagnosis of schizophrenia is made only if prominent delusions or hallucinations, in addition to the other criteria of schizophrenia, are met and are present for at least 1 month (or less if successfully treated)

Know the broad differences between schizophrenia and brief psychotic disorder, schizoaffective

disorder, delusional disorder and schizophreniform disorder.

Schizophrenia

Various psychotic symptoms, such as delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, restricted

or inappropriate affect, and catatonia

Duration: 6 months or more

Prevalence: 1.0%

Brief psychotic disorder

Various psychotic symptoms, such as delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, restricted or inappropriate affect, and catatonia

Duration: Less than 1 month

Prevalence: Unknown

!!Main difference is duration, less than a month

Schizophreniform disorder

Various psychotic symptoms, such as delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, restricted or inappropriate affect, and catatonia

Duration: 1 to 6 months

Prevalence: 0.2%

!!Main difference is duration, difference is 1-6 months

Schizoaffective disorder

Marked symptoms of both schizophrenia and a major depressive episode or a manic episode

Duration: 6 months or more

Prevalence: Unknown

!!Main difference is chizophrenia symptoms plus a major mood episode (depressive or manic). Psychotic symptoms must occur for at least 2 weeks without mood symptoms at some point.

Delusional disorder

Persistent delusions that are not bizarre and not due to schizophrenia; persecutory, jealous, grandiose, and somatic delusions are common

Duration: 1 month or more

Prevalence: 0.1%

!!Main difference is only delusions (non-bizarre or sometimes bizarre), lasting 1 month or more, with no major hallucinations or disorganized speech/behavior and relatively normal functioning.

What were the outcomes of the Leff’s work of avatars and hallucinations? In the book

Many of today’s therapists use avatars to help clients overcome their psychological problems. In this form of virtual reality therapy, clinicians have the clients interact with computer-generated on-screen virtual human figures.

For a pilot study, the researchers selected 16 participants who were being tormented by imaginary voices (auditory hallucinations). In each case, the therapist presented the individual with a mean-sounding and mean-looking avatar that appeared on-screen. The avatar’s voice pitch and appearance were designed based on the patient’s description of the auditory hallucination and what the patient believed would be a corresponding face

The patient was placed alone in a room with the computer simulation while the therapist generated the on-screen avatar from another room. Initially, the avatar spewed all sorts of frightening and upsetting statements at the patient. Then the therapist encouraged the patient to fight back — to tell the avatar things such as “I will not put up with this,” “What you are saying is nonsense,” “I don’t believe these things,” “You must go away and leave me alone,” and “I do not need this kind of torment.”

After seven 30-minute sessions, most of the participants in the pilot study had less frequent and less intense auditory hallucinations and reported being less upset by the voices that they did continue to hear. The participants also reported improvements in their feelings of depression and suicidal thinking.

The collective results of these studies suggest that confronting one’s hallucinations in a virtual world can indeed help at least some people with schizophrenia.

Backward masking:

A cognitive/perceptual test where a person briefly sees a visual stimulus, and then a second image quickly “masks” or covers it. The task measures how well someone can process visual information under very short time conditions.

Relation to Schizotypal Personality Disorder (STPD):

People with schizotypal personality disorder often perform poorly on backward masking tasks, meaning they need a longer interval to accurately perceive the first image. This suggests early sensory-processing deficits, similar (but milder) than those seen in schizophrenia.

What are some of the criticism of the current DSM with regard to personality disorders?

A key criticism of DSM-5-TR is that it classifies personality disorders categorically instead of dimensionally.

Many theorists argue personality disorders vary more in severity than in type, so classification should focus on how extreme key personality traits are rather than whether specific traits are simply present or absent.

a. How might the Big 5 be used to classify personality?

It can be used to classify personality by measuring where a person falls on each dimension (high, low), rather than assigning them to a fixed category. This allows for a dimensional approach, showing the degree of traits rather than labeling someone with a disorder.

c. Be familiar with the “personality disorder trait specified” approach

Instead of using fixed categories, this approach describes personality disorders based on the specific maladaptive traits a person shows.

Focuses on severity and combination of traits rather than fitting someone into a single disorder type.

d. What is the dark triad?

A group of three socially aversive personality traits:

Narcissism – grandiosity, entitlement, need for admiration

Machiavellianism – manipulation, deceit, strategic exploitation

Psychopathy – callousness, impulsivity, lack of empathy or remorse
