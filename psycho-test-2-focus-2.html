Recap of common Theoretical perspectives of psychology

Psychodynamic

Behavior and thoughts are driven by unconscious motives and conflicts, often from childhood (Freud, Jung, Erikson).

Disorders arise from repressed conflicts, trauma, or unresolved childhood issues.

Behavioral

Behavior is learned through conditioning (classical or operant) and reinforcement (Pavlov, Skinner, Watson).

Disorders are learned maladaptive behaviors; treatment focuses on unlearning them through conditioning and behavior modification.

Cognitive

Focuses on thinking patterns, beliefs, and perceptions (Beck, Ellis).

Disorders stem from distorted or irrational thinking; treatment uses cognitive restructuring (e.g., CBT).

Humanistic

Emphasizes personal growth, self-actualization, and fulfilling one’s potential.

Loss of self-unity and authenticity due to trauma or societal pressure.

Disorders arise when a person’s self-concept and experience are incongruent; therapy focuses on empathy, unconditional positive regard, and self-acceptance.

Existential

Focuses on meaning, freedom, and responsibility (Frankl, May, Yalom).

Disorders reflect a failure to find meaning or avoidance of responsibility and existential anxiety.

Difference between humanistic and existential

Humanistic = Growth.

Emphasizes personal growth, self-actualization, and fulfilling one’s potential.

A humanistic therapist (Rogers) might help them explore their feelings, values, and self-worth,

Existential = Meaning.

Emphasizes finding meaning in life, especially in the face of anxiety, death, freedom, and isolation.

An existential therapist (Frankl or May) might help them confront their fear of emptiness, find purpose, and take responsibility for creating meaning.

Biological

Emphasizes genetic, neurological, and biochemical factors.

Disorders are caused by brain dysfunction, neurotransmitter imbalances, or genetics; treatment involves medication or biological interventions.

Sociocultural

Focuses on social and cultural influences (family, society, norms).

Disorders develop due to societal pressures, family dynamics, or cultural expectations.

Dissociative Disorders

Psychodynamic: Fight anxiety by unconsciously repressing memories, especially traumatic memories

How might a psychodynamic theorist explain DID?

Flee the traumatic world of reality by pretending to be someone else

Depression

How might a psychodynamic theorist explain unipolar depression?

Depression results from loss, unresolved grief, or anger turned inward.

Describe the behavioral model with regard to unipolar depression. What treatments does this approach offer?

Depression results from lack of positive reinforcement.

Treatment: Behavioral activation, increasing rewarding activities.

How does the cognitive model explain unipolar depression?

Depression caused by negative thoughts about self, world, future (Beck’s cognitive triad).

How might a sociocultural theorist explain unipolar depression? What is Interpersonal Psychotherapy? How effective is this type of therapy? Is couple’s therapy an effective treatment for depression?

Sociocultural factors: Lack of support, cultural stress.

Interpersonal Psychotherapy (IPT): Focuses on improving relationships; effective.

Couples therapy: Can help if relationship stress contributes.
