Modeling vs classical conditioning vs operant conditioning

Modeling (Observational Learning)

Learn by watching someone else

You copy their behavior

Example: A child sees a parent yell → child yells too

Key idea: Monkey see, monkey do.

Classical Conditioning

Learn by associating two things together

Something neutral becomes linked to something meaningful

Example: Bell + food → dog salivates

Eventually: Bell alone → salivation

Key idea: Two things get paired in your brain.

Operant Conditioning

Learn based on consequences

Reward makes a behavior increase

Punishment makes a behavior decrease

Key idea: Behavior changes because of rewards or punishments.

Anosognosia

Condition whereby a person does not have insight into their condition

With schizophrenia, it is associated with noncompliance with tx, higher relapse rates, higher rates of involuntary hospitalization, and poorer prognosis

Can happen with other disorders as well (often noted with bipolar I disorder and dementias)

Involuntary lack of insight

Routes of Administration and Amount of Time to Reach Brain

Inhaling (i.e., inhaling while smoking crack): differing reports, 7-60 seconds

Snorting (i.e., powder cocaine): 4 minutes

Injecting: 20 seconds (IV); 4 minutes (IM)

Oral consumption: 20 minutes

Other (mucus membrane, vaginally, anally, placed under tongue): time to reach brain varies

What about the terms “reinforcement” and “punishment”?

Positive reinforcement: Adding something pleasant to increase behavior (e.g., giving candy for doing homework)

Negative reinforcement: Removing something unpleasant to increase behavior (e.g., turning off loud noise when seatbelt fastened)

Positive = add

Negative = remove

Reinforcement = increases behavior

Punishment = decreases behavior

Edinburgh high risk study

A long-term study in Scotland that followed people at high genetic risk for schizophrenia (usually because they had a first-degree relative with schizophrenia).

2 control groups: 1)matched peers with no family hx of schizophrenia 2) matched peers who have experienced a first psychotic break already

Most important thing is the results:

Size of temporal lobe decreases before the first psychotic symptoms appears

Why it’s important:

It helped shift psychiatry toward early detection, early intervention, and understanding biomarkers for psychosis risk

Conventional Antipsychotic Drugs

Maximum level of improvement usually within the first 6 months of treatment

Stop taking the drugs too soon: Symptoms may return

Conventional antipsychotics: Most effective on positive symptoms; have serious side effects

Atypical Antipsychotics or 2nd Generation Antipsychotics

Atypical antipsychotic drugs work differently

More effective than conventional antipsychotics

Also target serotonin

Effectiveness rates; reduce + and – symptoms

Common side effects: Sedation, weight gain, metabolic changes, anticholinergic effects, extrapyramidal effects

Agranulocytosis (1-1.5%) drop in white blood cells

Atypical antipsychotics: Risperdal, Clozaril, Zyprexa, Seroquel

FDA Approved Mediation: 2020: Caplyta (Lumateperone)

Targets serotonin, dopamine, and glutamate neurotransmitters

Fewer side effects

Black box warning for elderly people with dementia related psychosis

Personality Disorders

The DSM-5 identifies 10 personality disorders, which are organized into three clusters:

1. Odd or eccentric behavior

Paranoid, schizoid, and schizotypal personality disorders

2. Dramatic, emotional, or erratic behavior

Antisocial, borderline, narcissistic, and histrionic personality disorders

3. Anxious or fearful behavior

Avoidant, dependent, and obsessive-compulsive personality disorders
