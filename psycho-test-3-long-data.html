Schizophrenia Criteria

Criterion A: 2 (or more) of the following, each present for a sig. portion of time during a 1- month period: Delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior (or catatonic behavior), negative sxs

Criterion B: For a significant portion of time since onset, one or more major areas of functioning (work, school, self care, interpersonal) is markedly below the level achieved prior to onset

Criterion C: Continuous signs of a disturbance in functioning for at least 6 months (w/ at least one month of “active-phase symptoms” from A, unless successfully treated)

Criterion D: Schizoaffective disorder, MDD and bipolar I disorder have been ruled out

Criterion E: The disturbance is not attributable to substance use or another medical condition

Criterion F: If there is a hx of an autism spectrum disorder, the additional diagnosis of schizophrenia is made only if prominent delusions or hallucinations, in addition to the other criteria of schizophrenia, are met and are present for at least 1 month (or less if successfully treated)

Histrionic Personality Disorder

Prevalence = 1.5-2%

Criteria

A pervasive pattern of excessive emotionality and attention seeking, beginning in early adulthood. Present in a variety of contexts. Causes distress/impairment.

Must have 5 or more:

• Uncomfortable in situations where they are not the center of attention

• Sexual seductive or provocative behavior

• Displays rapidly shifting, shallow expressions of emotion

• Consistently uses physical appearance to draw attention to self

• Speech is expressionistic and lacks detail

• Exaggerated, theatrical expressions of emotion

• High suggestible, easily influenced by others

• Considers relationships to be more intimate that they actually

Psychodynamic

• Experienced unhealthy relationships - cold parents left them feeling unloved and fearing abandonment

• To defend, individuals learned to behave dramatically, inventing crises requiring protection

Cognitive:

• Self focus and emotionality interfere ability to observe others and the world around them; rely on others

• People with HPD hold a general assumption that they are helpless to care for themselves

Treating HPD

• Those with HPD often seek treatment

• Cognitive/Behavioral: change the belief that they are helpless and try to help them develop better, more deliberate ways of thinking and solving problems

• Psychodynamic and group therapy have also been successfully applied

• Clinical case reports (not experiments) suggest that these approaches can be useful

• Drug therapy is less successful, except as a means of relieving depressive symptoms

Dependent Personality Disorder

Criteria

A pervasive and excessive need to be taken care of that leads to submissive and clinging behavior and fears of separation, beginning in early adulthood. Present in a variety of contexts. Causes distress/impairment. Must have 5 or more:

• Difficulty making everyday decisions w/o excessive advice and/or reassurance

• Needs others to assume responsibility for most areas of his/her life

• Has difficulty disagreeing with others due to fear of losing support or approval

• Difficulty initiating projects alone due to low self confidence

• Goes to excessive lengths to obtain nurturance and support of others, may volunteer to do unpleasant things in order to be liked

• Uncomfortable/helpless when alone due to fears of being unable to care for self

• Urgently seeks another relationship if one ends

• Unrealistically preoccupied with fear of being left to care for self

Psychodynamic explanations: Similar to depression

• Unresolved oral stage: lifelong need for nurturance

• Early parental loss/rejection prevents normal attachment

Behavioral: Parents unintentionally rewarded child’s clingy behavior while punishing independence

Modeling

Cognitive: 2 Common Maladaptive Attitudes:

• “I am inadequate and helpless to deal with the world”

• “I must find a person to provide protection so I can cope”

• Such thinking thwarts efforts to be independent

Treating DPD

• Key task: Self responsibility

• Treatment can be at least modestly helpful

• Couple or family therapy often helpful

• Psychodynamic therapy: tx like depression

• Cognitive-behavioral: challenge/change assumptions of incompetence and helplessness, and provide assertiveness training

• Antidepressant drugs: w/dx of depression

• Group therapy: Often helpful. Provides an opportunity to receive support. Group members may serve as models

Paranoid Personality Disorder

A pervasive distrust and suspiciousness of others such that their motives are interpreted as malevolent, beginning in early adulthood. Present in a variety of contexts. Causes distress/impairment.

Must have 4 or more of the following

• Suspects, without sufficient basis, that others are exploiting, harming or deceiving him or her

• Preoccupied with unjustified doubts about loyalty or trustworthiness of friends and associates

• Reluctant to confide in others due to fear that information will be used against him/her

• Reads hidden demeaning or threatening meanings into benign remarks or events

• Persistently bears grudges

• Perceives attacks on one’s character or reputation that others do not see and quick to react angrily or to counterattack

• Recurrent suspicions without justification of sexual partner’s fidelity

Psychodynamic

Early interactions with demanding parents

• Rigid, distant fathers and over controlling, rejecting mothers

• Learn that the world is a hostile place

Cognitive

Hold maladaptive assumptions such as “People are evil and will attack you if given the chance”

Biological

Propose genetic causes and have looked at twin studies to support this model

Treating PPD

• Rarely seek treatment for PD symptoms

Cognitive-behavioral

Develop more realistic ways of seeing their world and relationships

• Modify thoughts

• Increase interpersonal effectiveness

• More realistic interpretations of others’ behavior

• Medications have been used, but data are sparse and currently meds appear to be of limited utility

Schizoid Personality Disorder

Criteria

A pervasive pattern of detachment from social relationships and a restricted range of expression of emotion in interpersonal settings, beginning in early adulthood. Present in a variety of contexts.

Causes distress/impairment. Must have 4 or more of the following:

• Neither desires nor enjoys close relationships, including family

• Almost always chooses solitary activities

• Has little, if any, interest in sexual experiences with another person

• Takes pleasure in few activities

• Lacks close friends, confidants (exception: 1st degree relatives)

• Appears indifferent to praise or criticism

• Show emotional coldness, detachment or flat affect

*R/O schizophrenia, bipolar, MDD with psychotic features episode

Link schizoid personality disorder to an unsatisfied need for human contact; cope through avoidance of relationships

Cognitive

Suffer from deficiencies in their thinking and therefore stop responding to people

Thoughts tend to be vague and empty, and they have trouble scanning the environment for accurate perceptions

Help people experience more positive emotions and more satisfying social interactions

Schizotypal Personality Disorder

A pervasive pattern of social and interpersonal deficits marked by acute discomfort with, and reduced capacity for, close relationships as well as cognitive or perceptual distortions and eccentricities of behavior, beginning in early adulthood. Present in a variety of contexts. Causes distress/impairment. Must have 5 or more:

• Ideas of reference (except delusions of reference)

• Odd beliefs and magical thinking that influence behavior but are inconsistent with subculture norms (superstitiousness, clairvoyance, telepathy, 6th sense)

• Unusual perceptions or paranoid ideation (including bodily illusions)

• Odd thinking and speech (i.e.,vague, circumstantial, over elaborate)

• Suspiciousness or paranoid ideation

• Inappropriate or constricted affect

• Behavior and/or appearance that is odd or eccentric

• Lacks close friends or confidents (besides 1st degree relatives)

• Excessive social anxiety that does not diminish with familiarity, associated with paranoid fear rather than negative judgment of self

*Rule of schizophrenia, MDD with psychotic features episodes

Explaining Schizotypal PD

Some researchers hypothesize a similar etiology as Schizophrenia

Schizotypal symptoms are often linked to family conflicts and to psychological disorders in parents

Researchers have also begun to link schizotypal personality disorder to some of the same biological

factors found in schizophrenia

High dopamine activity, smaller ventricles, etc.

Also related to higher rates of familiar mood disorders

Treating Schizotypal PD

Most therapists agree on the need to help clients “reconnect” and recognize the limits of their thinking and powers

Cognitive-behavioral

Try to teach clients to objectively evaluate their thoughts and perceptions and provide speech lessons and social skills training

Antipsychotic

drugs appear to be somewhat helpful in reducing certain thought problems
