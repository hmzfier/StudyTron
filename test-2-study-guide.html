The functions of joints (3)

Enable movement – link between bones allow movement when muscles and

tendons exert necessary force across joint

Provide stability – some joints allow limited or no movement; very stable; critical

for structures such as skull; protect underlying structures

Allow long bones to lengthen – epiphyseal plate is where long bones

grow in length during skeletal development; temporary joint

the functional classification of joints by motion and structural features

Functional Classification (Based on Movement): (3)

1. Synarthroses: Immovable joints (e.g., sutures of the skull)

2. Amphiarthroses: Slightly movable joints (e.g., intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis)

3. Diarthroses: Freely movable joints (e.g., shoulder, hip)

Structural Classification (Based on Tissue and Structure):

1. Fibrous Joints: Bones joined by dense connective tissue; no joint cavity. NOTE: no joint space

Examples: sutures, syndesmoses, gomphoses

2. Cartilaginous Joints: Bones joined by cartilage; no joint cavity or minimal movement

Examples: synchondroses (hyaline cartilage), symphyses (fibrocartilage)

3. Synovial Joints: Bones separated by a fluid-filled joint cavity; highly movable

Have layer of hyaline cartilage on articulating surface of each bone;

Features: articular cartilage, joint cavity, synovial fluid, joint capsule

Examples: hinge (elbow), ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip), pivot, saddle, condylar, plane

Bundle of 10–100 muscle cells

Not an official term

All fascicles enclosed by epimysium (outer fibrous connective tissue)

Fascia:

A bunch of entire muscles

Most superficial connective tissue layer

Continuous with epimysium (located under fascia)

Separates individual muscles from one another

Fascicles and their shapes and how that affects their function (know the examples)

1. Parallel Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles run parallel to the long axis of the muscle

Function: Produce great range of motion but less force

Example: Sartorius, biceps brachii

2. Fusiform Fascicles:

Description: Spindle-shaped, wider in the middle and taper at ends

Function: Moderate force and range of motion

Example: Biceps brachii

3. Circular Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles arranged in concentric rings around openings

Function: Act as sphincters, controlling opening and closing

Example: Orbicularis oris, orbicularis oculi

4. Convergent Fascicles:

Description: Broad origin and converge to a single tendon

Function: Versatile; can generate strong force

Example: Pectoralis major

5. Pennate Fascicles:

Description: Short fascicles attach obliquely to a central tendon

Function: Produce more force but less range of motion

Unipennate: Fibers on one side of tendon – extensor digitorum

Bipennate: Fibers on both sides of tendon – rectus femoris

Multipennate: Tendon branches within muscle – deltoid

6.Spiral Fascicles

Description: Fascicles wrap around a bone or another structure in a spiral pattern.

Function: Can twist during contraction, allowing complex movements and transfer of force along a curved path.

Example: Supinator (forearm), latissimus dorsi (partially spiral orientation)

the functions of muscle tissue

Generating force (muscle tension) is a basic function of all muscle tissue

Other functions of muscle tissue:

create movement

maintain posture

stabilize joints

generate heat

regulate flow of materials through hollow organs

Compare and contrast the 3 types of muscle tissue

Skeletal

Long, cylindrical, striated muscle fibers. Cells are multinucleated

Location: Mostly attach to skeleton

Voluntary

Function: Produces movement of the body

Cardiac

Short, Wide, branching striated cardiac muscle cells with intercalated discs. Cells have 1-2 nuclei.

Location: Heart

Involuntary

Function: Produces beating of heart

Smooth

Thin, smooth muscle cells, joined by gap junctions, cells have a single nucleus.

Location: wall of hollow organs, as well as the skin and eyes

Involuntary

Function: Allows urine/poop to pass by changing shape

Function 2: Adjusts the lens/pupils of the eye

the properties of muscle cells

1. Contractility - ability to contract; proteins in cell draw closer together

2. Excitability - ability to respond to stimulus (chemical, mechanical stretch, or local

electrical signals)

3. Conductivity - ability to conduct electrical changes across entire plasma

Membrane

4. Extensibility - ability to be stretched (up to 3 times resting length) without

Rupturing

5. Elasticity - ability to return to original length after stretching

the structure of the muscle cells

Myo = prefix meaning muscle

Myocyte = muscle cell

Muscle fiber = skeletal muscle cell

Sarcoplasm = cytoplasm

Sarcolemma = plasma membrane, but is different from general cell

It forms inward extensions called transverse tubules (T-tubules) that surround myofibrils

Sarcoplasmic reticulum = modified smooth ER

Stores and releases calcium ions. Forms web-like network surrounding myofibrils

the structure of myofibrils

Myofibrils are long, cylindrical-like structures inside each muscle fiber (muscle cell).

100s to 1000s of myofibrils in each myocyte: 50-80% of cell volume

They are the parts of the muscle that actually contract — they make the muscle shorten and generate force.

Each muscle fiber contains hundreds to thousands of myofibrils running parallel to each other.

Myofibrils are made up of repeating units called sarcomeres — the smallest functional units of muscle contraction.

the structure of the three myofilaments and their 3 types of proteins

1. Thick filaments - bundles of contractile protein myosin

2. Thin filaments - proteins actin, tropomyosin, and troponin

3. Elastic filaments - single massive, spring-like structural protein(titin);

What is the importance of Z-disc?

Line bisecting the I band.

Both the thin and elastic filaments anchor to the z-discs, which also attach myofibrils to one another

It anchors the actin (thin) filaments, keeping the structure organized during muscle contraction.

What are I bands and what do they contain?

Light regions of striations containing only thin filaments; each I band has a Z-disc in its middle.

shortens during contraction.

What are A bands and what do they contain?

The dark band that contains both thick and thin filaments

This region stays the same length during contraction.

What is the importance of H-zones?

The middle portion of the A band in which only thick filaments are found

KNOW THAT IT IS IN THE A BAND. HA! acronym

Disappears when muscle contracts

What is the importance of M-line?

Middle line of the A band.

Holds myosin filament in place

what membrane potentials are and how to create the resting membrane potential

The membrane potential is the difference in electrical charge between the inside and outside of a cell’s membrane.

The inside of a muscle cell is more negative than the outside.

Resting membrane potential - When the muscle cell is at rest (not contracting), it maintains a steady charge difference called the resting membrane potential.

3 ways to create the resting potential:

1. Concentration gradient = the inside is high in potassium and low in sodium. The outside is high in sodium and low in potassium

2. Potassium Leak channels - potassium leaks out of the cell faster than sodium leaks in

3. Sodium-Potassium Pump - Moves 3 sodium out of the cell, 2 potassium into the cell.

the structure of the neuromuscular junction (4)

Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)- Synapse where a single motor neuron communicates with many muscle fibers

axon terminal = Small sacs filled with Ach. Release ACh into the synaptic cleft when an action potential arrives

Synaptic Cleft - Narrow space between axon terminal and muscle fiber. Space ACh diffuses across to reach the muscle

Motor end plate = Where receptors for ACH are located. Contains ligand-gated sodium channels that open when ACh binds

the events of the excitation-contraction coupling

1. Excitation

An action potential arrives at the axon terminal and triggers calcium ion channels in the axon terminal to open.

Calcium ion entry triggers exocytosis of synaptic vesicles.

Synaptic vesicles release acetylcholine into the synaptic cleft.

Acetylcholine binds to ligand-gated cation channels in the motor end plate.

Sodium and potassium channels open and cations enter the muscle fiber.

Entry of Sodium and potassium depolarizes the sarcolemma locally, producing an end-plate potential.

2. Excitation-Contraction Coupling

The end-plate potential stimulates an action potential.

The action potential is travels down the T-tubules.

T-tubule depolarization leads to the opening of calcium ion channels in the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and calcium ions enter the cytosol.

3. Contraction

Calcium ions bind to troponin.

Tropomyosin moves, and the active sites of actin are exposed.

ATP hydrolysis “cocks” the myosin head - ATP hydrolysis turns ATP to ADP and a phosphate group (P)

The myosin head binds to actin.

The power stroke occurs when the phosphate detaches from the myosin head

Then the myosin pulls actin toward the center of the sarcomere

ADP leaves the myosin head at the end of the power stroke.

ATP breaks the attachment of myosin to actin. Another ATP binds to the myosin head, which breaks its attachment to actin.

Then the cross-bridge and power stroke cycle repeats as long as: ATP is available, and Calcium remains present in the sarcoplasm.

the role of calcium in a muscle contraction and where it comes from

Without calcium, no contraction can happen — actin and myosin can’t interact.

The action potential triggers voltage-gated calcium channels in the sarcoplasmic reticulum to open. Calcium floods into the cytoplasm (sarcoplasm). Calcium attaches to troponin on the thin filament. And so on

the cross-bridge cycle components: how ATP is involved, what the power stroke is

During cross bridge detachment a new ATP binds to the myosin head → causes it to release actin.

Myosin breaks down ATP → ADP + Pi.

skeletal muscle relaxation components - 5 steps

1. Motor neuron stops firing action potentials. No more acetylcholine (ACh) is released

2.Acetylcholinesterase degrades the remaining acetylcholine, and the final repolarization occurs

3.The sarcolemma returns to its resting membrane potential, and the calcium ion channels in the sarcoplasmic reticulum close.

4. Calcium ions are actively pumped back into Sarcoplasmic reticulum (using ATP)

5.Troponin shifts and pulls tropomyosin back into position to block the active sites of actin, and the muscle relaxes

where ATP is required in the skeletal muscle - 4

1. Power Sodium/Potassium pumps that maintain ion gradients (action potentials)

2. Release myosin heads from actin active sites

3. recock heads for another power stroke

4. Pump calcium back into SR (relaxation

how ATP is generated in the skeletal muscle

1. Immediate cytosolic reactions: In the cytosol, the muscle uses creatine phosphate (CP) stored in the cytosol to generate ATP.

ATP yield: Small (enough for ~10 seconds)

2. Glycolysis catabolism in cytosol (results in production of 2 ATP)

anaerobic - no oxygen required

This process breaks down glucose (from blood sugar or glycogen) into pyruvate.

ATP Duration: 30 sec–2 min

3. Oxidative catabolism in mitochondria (results in production of many ATP)

aerobic = requires oxygen

Allows for longer lasting muscle contractions

ATP Duration: Minutes–hours

3 phases of a muscle fiber twitch

1. Latent period - time for action potential to propagate across sarcolemma

2. Contraction period - repeated crossbridge cycles generate tension

3. Relaxation period - calcium ion levels reduced in cytosol by SR pumps; tension diminishes

Basically, this is a summary of muscle contraction

3 factors that determine the amount of tension produced by a twitch contraction

1. Repeated stimulation = results in twitches with greater tension

2. Number of crossbridges that can form

The sarcomere’s length before contraction decides how many cross-bridges can form.

Perfect length = strongest pull.

3. Type of muscle fiber

Slow fibers (Type I): steady, endurance, less force.

Fast fibers (Type II): quick, powerful, fatigue faster.

the difference between unfused and fused tetanus of wave summation

Unfused Tetanus = The muscle is stimulated repeatedly, but there’s a short relaxation period.

Tension = Moderated

Fused Tetanus = The muscle is stimulated rapidly. No relaxation occurs.

Tension = maximum

Understand how the length of the sarcomere prior to contraction affects muscle tension

optimal length of sarcomere is about 100–120% of natural length

At optimal length, sarcomere can generate greatest tension

Too short = weak tension

Too long = weak tension

Rely mainly on glycolytic catabolism for ATP production

White Muscle

IIX is the fastest

what a motor unit consists of

Single motor neuron + ALL of muscle fibers that it controls

the 3 types of muscle contractions

Isotonic concentric - shortening

isotonic eccentric - lengthening

Isometric - no change in length

how skeletal muscle is affected by physical training

Endurance Training = muscles work longer, not bigger.

Resistance training (strength) = Fewer reps, heavy weight → muscles grow larger and stronger (hypertrophy)

Disuse = No activity → muscles shrink and weaken (Atrophy)

the functions of smooth muscle - 3

1. Propels materials through hollow organs (peristalsis)

2. Forms sphincters (digestive and urinary systems); control passage of materials by opening

and closing

3. Regulates flow rates through hollow organs (blood vessels, respiratory tract, and

gastrointestinal tract) by changing diameter of tubing

how smooth muscle structure compares to skeletal muscle

Smooth muscle cells contain myosin and actin filaments arranged differently than in skeletal muscle

no sarcomeres (= no striations), no T-tubules, no troponin

Actin filaments are arranged obliquely in sarcoplasm, anchored to proteins called dense bodies

More thin filaments than thick

Spindle-shaped cells with one nucleus each.

Both thick and thin filaments are longer

Myosin heads along entire length of thick filament, with opposite-facing heads !!IMPORTANT!!

Smooth Muscle Contraction and Relaxation

1. Calcium binds to calmodulin

2. This activates myosin light-chain kinase (MLCK)

3. MLCK activates myosin ATPase

4. Crossbridge cycle begins

Relaxation occurs when

1. Calcium is removed from cytosol

2. MLCK is deactivated

3. myosin ATPase is deactivated
