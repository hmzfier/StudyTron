Common structural features of the vertebrae

Body (Centrum):

Primary weight-bearing part, located anteriorly

Separated by intervertebral discs, which absorb shock from activities like running and jumping

Vertebral Foramen:

Posterior to the body

Large opening that houses the spinal cord and associated tissues

Vertebral Arch: Formed by 2 pedicles (lateral borders of foramen) and 2 laminae (posterior borders)

Articular Processes (Superior & Inferior):

At junctions of pedicles and laminae

Facets form joints with adjacent vertebrae

Transverse Processes:

Project laterally from vertebral arch

Serve as muscle attachment sites

Spinous Process:

Projects posteriorly from vertebral arch

Provides additional sites for muscle attachment

Compare and contrast the cervical, thoracic and lumbar vertebrae

Cervical Vertebrae (C1–C7):

Size: Smallest, lightest

Body: Small and oval

Vertebral foramen: Large, triangular

Spinous process: Often bifid (split)

Transverse processes: Contain transverse foramina for vertebral arteries

Dens (odontoid process) – prominent superior tooth-shaped projection protrudes

from body

Function: Supports head, allows greatest range of motion

Thoracic Vertebrae (T1–T12):

Size: Medium

Body: Heart-shaped

Vertebral foramen: Circular, smaller than cervical

Spinous process: Long, points downward

Transverse processes: Articulate with ribs (costal facets)

Superior costal facets and inferior costal facets, on lateral sides of vertebral

body

Lumbar Vertebrae (L1–L5):

Size: Largest and heaviest of all vertebrae, thick bodies for weight-bearing

Body: Kidney-shaped

Vertebral foramen: Triangular, smaller than cervical

Spinous process: Short, thick, horizontal

Transverse processes: Long, no transverse foramina or rib facets

Function: Supports lower back, allows flexion/extension

The functions of joints (3)

Enable movement – link between bones allow movement when muscles and

tendons exert necessary force across joint

Provide stability – some joints allow limited or no movement; very stable; critical

for structures such as skull; protect underlying structures

Allow long bones to lengthen – epiphyseal plate is where long bones

grow in length during skeletal development; temporary joint

the functional classification of joints by motion and structural features

Functional Classification (Based on Movement): (3)

1. Synarthroses: Immovable joints (e.g., sutures of the skull)

2. Amphiarthroses: Slightly movable joints (e.g., intervertebral discs, pubic symphysis)

3. Diarthroses: Freely movable joints (e.g., shoulder, hip)

Structural Classification (Based on Tissue and Structure):

1. Fibrous Joints: Bones joined by dense connective tissue; no joint cavity. NOTE: no joint space

Examples: sutures, syndesmoses, gomphoses

2. Cartilaginous Joints: Bones joined by cartilage; no joint cavity or minimal movement

Examples: synchondroses (hyaline cartilage), symphyses (fibrocartilage)

3. Synovial Joints: Bones separated by a fluid-filled joint cavity; highly movable

Have layer of hyaline cartilage on articulating surface of each bone;

Features: articular cartilage, joint cavity, synovial fluid, joint capsule

Examples: hinge (elbow), ball-and-socket (shoulder, hip), pivot, saddle, condylar, plane

the 3 fibrous joints: what they are composed of, where to find them and motility

Classification

1. Sutures

immoveable joint between edges of bones;

make up cranium; fully fused sutures are very stable, well suited for protecting brain

2. Syndesmoses

joint between tibia, fibula, ulna, and radius

bones joined by interosseous membrane or ligament composed of dense regular collagenous connective tissue

allows for small amount of movement (amphiarthroses)

3. Gomphoses

immoveable joint (synarthrosis) between each tooth and bony socket in jaw

periodontal ligament is strong fibrous membrane; links tooth firmly to jaw bone

the 2 cartilaginous joints: what they are composed of, where to find them and motility

Classification

Synchondrosis – bones linked together by hyaline cartilage; synarthroses

Epiphyseal plates – hyaline cartilage

▪connects diaphyses and epiphyses of developing long bones; replaced with bone during maturation

First sternocostal joint and costochondral joints are synchondroses

▪stabilize rib cage; persist into adulthood

Symphysis – bones united by fibrocartilaginous pad or plug; functionally amphiarthroses

the structural elements of synovial joints and their function (4)

1.Joint cavity (synovial cavity) – space between articulating bones

2. Articular capsule: Has two layers: Outer fibrous layer – holds the bones together Inner synovial membrane – lines the inside (except over cartilage) and produces synovial fluid

3.Synovial fluid – thick liquid with 3 main functions

Lubrication – reduces friction between articulating surfaces

Transportation – moves nutrients and waste products in absence of blood vessels within joint

Shock absorption – helps to evenly distribute stress and force during movement

4.Articular Cartilage:

Covers the ends of bones, reduces friction and absorbs shock

the stabilizing and supporting factors of synovial joints

1.Ligaments:

Connect bone to bone

Strengthen and stabilize the joint

2.Tendons (associated, not part of joint proper):

Connect muscle to bone

Assist in joint movement and stability

3.Muscle tone – continuous amount muscle contraction = stabilizes

4.Bursae (associated structures)

Fluid-filled sacs between bones, tendons, or muscles

Reduce friction during movement

the functional classes of synovial joints

1.Nonaxial joints – allow motion in 1+ planes without moving around axis

Examples: Intercarpal joints, intertarsal joints

2. Uniaxial joints – allow motion around only 1 axis

Examples: elbow

3. Biaxial joints – allow motion around 2 axes

Examples: metacarpophalangeal joints - axis 1: flexion/extension, axis 2: move fingers fanned out or in (abduction/adduction)

4. Multiaxial (triaxial) joints – allow motion around 3 axes

Examples: shoulder, hip

the general types of movements at synovial joint

Gliding = sliding

Flexion = decrease angle

Extension = Increase angle

Hyperextension = extension beyond normal

Abduction = away from midline

Adduction = toward the midline

Circumduction = circular motion

types of synovial joints

Plane = gliding joint

Hinge = uniaxial movement

Pivot = uniaxial movement

Condylar = biaxial joint

Saddle = biaxial joint

Ball and socket = multiaxial articulation

Chapter 9 study guide

The gross anatomy of the skeletal muscle

Summary:

Muscle cell (endomysium) > Fascicle (perimysium) > The entire muscle (epimysium) > Fascia (epimysium)

Muscle Cell (Myocyte):

Surrounded by endomysium (thin layer of connective tissue)

Fascicle:

Bundle of 10–100 muscle cells

Surrounded by perimysium (connective tissue)

The entire Muscle:

Not an official term

All fascicles enclosed by epimysium (outer fibrous connective tissue)

Fascia:

A bunch of entire muscles

Most superficial connective tissue layer

Continuous with epimysium (located under fascia)

Separates individual muscles from one another

Fascicles and their shapes and how that affects their function (know the examples)

1. Parallel Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles run parallel to the long axis of the muscle

Function: Produce great range of motion but less force

Example: Sartorius, biceps brachii

2. Fusiform Fascicles:

Description: Spindle-shaped, wider in the middle and taper at ends

Function: Moderate force and range of motion

Example: Biceps brachii

3. Circular Fascicles:

Description: Fascicles arranged in concentric rings around openings

Function: Act as sphincters, controlling opening and closing

Example: Orbicularis oris, orbicularis oculi

4. Convergent Fascicles:

Description: Broad origin and converge to a single tendon

Function: Versatile; can generate strong force

Example: Pectoralis major

5. Pennate Fascicles:

Description: Short fascicles attach obliquely to a central tendon

Function: Produce more force but less range of motion

Unipennate: Fibers on one side of tendon – extensor digitorum

Bipennate: Fibers on both sides of tendon – rectus femoris

Multipennate: Tendon branches within muscle – deltoid

6.Spiral Fascicles

Description: Fascicles wrap around a bone or another structure in a spiral pattern.

Function: Can twist during contraction, allowing complex movements and transfer of force along a curved path.

Example: Supinator (forearm), latissimus dorsi (partially spiral orientation)

Functions of skeletal muscle

Movement: Contractions move bones at joints.

Heat Production: Contractions generate heat, helping maintain body temperature.

Respiration: Diaphragm contraction enables breathing.

Facial Expression: Muscles attached to skin produce expressions.

Swallowing: Throat muscles assist in moving food.

Control of Openings: Skeletal muscle sphincters allow voluntary control of body openings.

Functional groups of muscles working together with the examples (agonist, antagonist etc)

Agonists (prime movers) – provide most force for muscle action

Antagonists – opposes action of agonist

allows for modulation and control of agonist movement

Synergists – aid agonists

supplies supplemental force

minimizes unwanted movement

help stabilize joints

provide for more efficient movement

Fixators – provide stabilizing force that anchors bone

provides movement efficiency

Protects muscle from injury

Define a muscle’s origin and insertion

Origin – (Stationary) anchoring point on bone, where skeletal muscle “originates from”

Typically not involved directly with movement of joint

Insertion – (Moves closer to origin) moving end of muscle

whose tendon attaches to bone or other structures

usually on far side of joint
